The Battle of Mycale Battle_of_Mycale (Ancient Greek Ancient_Greece: Μάχη τῆς Μυκάλης; Machē tēs Mykalēs) was one of the two major battles that ended the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece during the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. It took place on or about August 27, 479 BC on the slopes of Mount Mycale Mycale, on the coast of Ionia, opposite the island of Samos. The battle Battle was fought between an alliance of the Greek Ancient_Greece city-states, including Sparta, Athens History_of_Athens and Corinth, and the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Xerxes I Xerxes_I.

The previous year, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion force, led by Xerxes I Xerxes_I himself, had scored victories at the battles of Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae and Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium, and conquered Thessaly, Boeotia and Attica; however, at the ensuing Battle of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, the allied Greek Ancient_Greece navies had won an unlikely victory, and therefore prevented the conquest of the Peloponnese. Xerxes I Xerxes_I then retreated, leaving his general Mardonius with a substantial army to finish off the Greeks Ancient_Greece the following year.

In the summer of 479 BC, the Greeks Ancient_Greece assembled a huge army (by contemporary standards), and marched to confront Mardonius at the Battle of Plataea. At the same time, the allied fleet sailed to Samos, where the demoralised remnants of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy were based. The Persians The_Persians, seeking to avoid a battle Battle, beached their fleet below the slopes of Mycale Battle_of_Mycale, and, with the support of a Persian Achaemenid_Empire army group Army_group, built a palisaded camp. The Greek Ancient_Greece commander Leotychides Leotychidas decided to attack the Persians Achaemenid_Empire anyway, landing the fleet's complement of marines to do so.

Although the Persian Achaemenid_Empire forces put up stout Stout resistance, the heavily armoured Greek Ancient_Greece hoplites Hoplite again proved themselves superior in combat, and eventually routed the Persian Achaemenid_Empire troops, who fled to their camp. The Ionian Ionia Greek Ancient_Greece contingents in the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army defected, and the camp was assailed and a large number of Persians Achaemenid_Empire slaughtered. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire ships were then captured and burned. The complete destruction of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy, along with the destruction of Mardonius's army at Plataea (allegedly on the same day as the Battle of Mycale Battle_of_Mycale), decisively ended the invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece. After Plataea and Mycale Battle_of_Mycale, the allied Greeks Ancient_Greece would take the offensive against the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, marking a new phase of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. Although Mycale Battle_of_Mycale was in every sense a decisive victory, it does not seem to have been attributed the same significance (even at the time) as, for example the Athenian History_of_Athens victory at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon or even the Greek Ancient_Greece defeat at Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae.

The main source Main_Source for the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[3] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his 'Enquiries' (Greek—Historia; English Kingdom_of_England—(The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[4] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented 'history' as we know it.[4] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[4]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[5][6] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[6] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On The Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[7] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[8] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds which have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[9] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[9] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[10]

The Sicilian The_Sicilian historian Diodorus Siculus Diodorus_Siculus, writing in the 1st century BC in his Bibliotheca Historica Bibliotheca_historica, also provides an account of the Battle of Mykale, derived directly from the earlier Greek Ancient_Greece historian Ephorus. This account is fairly consistent with Herodotus's.[11] Archaeological evidence, such as the Serpent Column Serpent_Column, also supports some of Herodotus's specific claims.[12]

The Greek Ancient_Greece city-states of Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria had supported the unsuccessful Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt against the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Darius I Darius_I in 499-494 BC. The Persian Empire Roman_Empire was still relatively young, and prone to revolts amongst its subject peoples.[13][14] Moreover, Darius I Darius_I was a usurper, and had spent considerable time extinguishing revolts against his rule.[13] The Ionian revolt Ionian_Revolt threatened the integrity of his empire, and Darius I Darius_I thus vowed to punish those involved (especially those not already part of the empire).[15][16] Darius I Darius_I also saw the opportunity to expand his empire into the fractious world of Ancient Greece Ancient_Greece.[16] A preliminary expedition under Mardonius, in 492 BC, to secure the land approaches to Greece Ancient_Greece ended with the re-conquest of Thrace and forced Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia to become a client kingdom Client_state of Persia Achaemenid_Empire.[17] An amphibious task force was then sent out under Datis and Artaphernes in 490 BC, successfully sacking Naxos and Eretria,[18] before moving to attack Athens History_of_Athens. Darius I Darius_I therefore began raising a huge new army with which he meant to completely subjugate Greece Ancient_Greece. The Allies Allies_of_World_War_II initially adopted a strategy of blocking the land and sea approaches to southern Greece Ancient_Greece.[25] Thus, in August 480 BC, after hearing of Xerxes I Xerxes_I's approach, a small Allied Allies_of_World_War_II army led by the Spartan king Leonidas Leonidas_I blocked the Pass of Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae, whilst an Athenian-dominated navy sailed to the Straits of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium. Following Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army had proceeded to burn and sack the Boeotian cities which had not surrendered, Plataea and Thespiae, before taking possession of the now-evacuated city City of Athens History_of_Athens. Following the defeat of his navy at the Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, Xerxes I Xerxes_I retreated to Asia Anatolia with, according to Herodotus at least, the majority of the army. The number of Persian Achaemenid_Empire ships and men involved with the battle Battle are, as so often in the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, somewhat problematic. It is clear that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet did not dare conduct operations against the Greeks Ancient_Greece, and thus must have been approximately equal to, or smaller than the Greek Ancient_Greece fleet.[49][50] Herodotus gives the size of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet at 300 ships;[50] the Greeks Ancient_Greece had 378 at Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, but must have suffered significant losses, and so they probably also had around 300 in total (though not necessarily all these ships formed part of the allied fleet for 479 BC).[49] The Phoenician Phoenicia ships were dismissed from the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet before the battle Battle, which reduced its strength further.[41]

Diodorus tells us that to guard the camp and the ships the Persians Achaemenid_Empire gathered 100,000 men in total,[51] while Herodotus suggests that there were 60,000 men in the army under the command of Tigranes Tigranes_the_Great.[41] Squaring these two accounts, might suggest that there were c. 40,000 men with the fleet. Given that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet appears to have been undermanned in the aftermath of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, 200-300 ships would indeed give this number of naval personnel (using Herodotus's standard complement of 200 men per ship).[39] However, this total of 100,000 is probably too high; to accommodate 100,000 men and 200+ ships, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire camp would have to have been enormous. Estimates made of Mardonius's huge camp at Plataea, which was planned and built with plenty of time, suggest it might have accommodated 70,000-120,000 men;[52][53][54] it is improbable that such a large camp could have been built at Mycale Battle_of_Mycale in the time-frame that Herodotus suggests. It is therefore possible that the 60,000 quoted by Herodotus is actually the total number of Persians Achaemenid_Empire present at Mycale Battle_of_Mycale; the Persians Achaemenid_Empire certainly outnumbered the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II, emerging from the palisade in confidence after seeing the smaller number of the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II troops.[45][55]

Numbers of ships and men for the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II are also somewhat problematic. Herodotus claims that Leotychides Leotychidas had 110 triremes under his command.[56] However, the previous year, the allies had fielded 271 triremes at the Battle of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium, and then 378 at the Battle of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis.[57][58] We are also told that the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II had "command of the sea" after Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, which implies that they could at least equal the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet.[49] Diodorus, on the other hand, tells us the allies had 250 ships, which is more consistent with their force levels of the previous year.[51] These two numbers can be reconciled by assuming that Leotychides Leotychidas had 110 triremes under his command before being joined by Xanthippus and the Athenian History_of_Athens ships, after the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II army had marched out from the Peloponnesus Peloponnese. Although the Athenians had sent 8,000 hoplites Hoplite to Plataea,[59] they would still have had ample manpower to man a large fleet of triremes, especially since rowers tended to be of the lower classes (the thetes Solonian_Constitution) who could not afford the equipment to fight as hoplites Hoplite.[60] The standard London_Evening_Standard complement of a trireme Trireme was 200 men, including 14 marines.[61] In the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, each Persian Achaemenid_Empire ship had carried thirty extra marines,[62] and this was probably also true in the first invasion when the whole invasion force was apparently carried in triremes.[61] Furthermore, the Chian Chios ships at the Battle of Lade Miletus also carried 40 marines each. This suggests that a trireme Trireme could probably carry a maximum of 40–45 soldiers—triremes seem to have been easily destabilised by extra weight.[63] Combining these numbers yields a range of 22,000–58,000 men for the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II, with 3,300–11,250 more heavily armoured marines.[62] Estimates of around 40,000 men are given in some sources, which is approximately the median of the possible range, and seems as likely a number as any.[64] However, since only the marines were expected to fight hand to hand, the rowers in the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet were probably not equipped to fight in a land battle Battle; it is likely therefore that it was only the marines who contested the battle Battle.[45]

From a strategic point of view, battle Battle was not necessary for either side; the main strategic theatre was mainland Greece Ancient_Greece itself. Although destroying the enemy navy would result in a clear strategic advantage for both sides, attempting this risked the loss of their own navy. The actions of the two sides thus reflect more upon their morale and confidence than on any strategic considerations. The Persians The_Persians, seeing little to gain in battle Battle, demoralised and riven with dissent, thus sought to avoid a naval battle Naval_warfare.[41][49][50][51] Conversely, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II, who had initially been as nervous of a battle Battle as the Persians Achaemenid_Empire,[65] sought to press home their morale advantage once they were informed of the state of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet.[40]

Tactically, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet should have held the advantage at sea, since the Athenian History_of_Athens part of the Greek Ancient_Greece fleet was, despite their efforts at Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium and Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, still raw in seamanship.[66] However, whether because of their low morale, or because they were in fact outnumbered, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire sought instead the tactical advantage of joining up with the army under Tigranes Tigranes_the_Great, and fortifying a position.[40][41] However, when the Greeks Ancient_Greece chose to fight on land, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire then threw away the advantage of their fortifications by emerging to fight the Greeks Ancient_Greece in the open field.[51] Furthermore, as Marathon Battle_of_Marathon and Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae had shown, large numbers conferred little advantage against the more heavily armoured hoplites Hoplite; thus, as the battle Battle began, it was the Greeks Ancient_Greece who had the tactical upper hand.[67]

The Allies Allies_of_World_War_II seem to have formed into two wings; on the right were the Athenians, Corinthians Ancient_Corinth, Sicyonians and Troezenians, and on the left were the Spartans Spartan_army with other contingents.[68] The right wing marched across level ground straight towards the Persian Achaemenid_Empire camp, whilst the left wing attempted to outflank the Persians Achaemenid_Empire by passing through more broken ground.[68] The right wing thus began fighting with the Persians Achaemenid_Empire while the left wing was still approaching.[68] Herodotus reports that the Persians Achaemenid_Empire fought well at first, but that the Athenians and the contingents with them wished to win the victory before the Spartans Spartan_army arrived, and thus attacked ever more zealously.[68]

Although the Persians Achaemenid_Empire stood their ground for a while, they eventually broke and fled to the palisade.[68] The soldiers The_Soldiers of the right wing followed them into the camp, at which point many of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army fled from the camp, except the ethnic Persian Achaemenid_Empire troops, who grouped together and fought the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II soldiers who entered the camp.[68] Finally, the left wing arrived, outflanking the camp and falling on the rear of the remaining Persian Achaemenid_Empire forces, thereby completing the rout.[45][69]

When the Spartans Spartan_army arrived, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire camp was looted and their beached ships destroyed.[72] Returning to Samos they then discussed their next moves.[72] Leotychides Leotychidas proposed that they evacuate the cities of the Ionian Greeks Ionia and bring the population to the Greek Ancient_Greece mainland, since it would be difficult to defend Ionia against further Persian Achaemenid_Empire attacks.[72] Xanthippus however vehemently objected to this, since the Ionian Ionia cities were originally Greek colonies Colonies_in_antiquity.[72] The Ionian Greeks Ionia later joined the Athenians in the "Delian League Delian_League" against Persia Achaemenid_Empire.[72]

With the twin victories of Plataea and Mycale Battle_of_Mycale, the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece was over. Moreover, the threat of a future invasion was abated; although the Greeks Ancient_Greece remained worried that Xerxes I Xerxes_I would try again, over time it became apparent that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire desire to conquer Greece Ancient_Greece was much diminished.[73]

Mycale Battle_of_Mycale and Plataea have great significance in ancient history as the battles which decisively ended the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, thereby swinging the balance of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars in favour of the Greeks Ancient_Greece.[75] The Battle of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis saved Greece Ancient_Greece from immediate conquest, but it was Mycale Battle_of_Mycale and Plataea which effectively ended that threat.[75] However, neither of these battles are as well known as Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae, Salamis Battle_of_Salamis or Marathon Battle_of_Marathon.
