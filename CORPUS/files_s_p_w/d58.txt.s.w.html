As part of the only passage between the Black Black_Sea and the Mediterranean Mediterranean_Sea, the Dardanelles has always been of great importance from a commercial and military point of view, and remains strategically important today. It is a major sea access route for numerous countries, including Russia Russian_Empire and Ukraine. Control over it has been an objective of a number of hostilities in modern history, notably the attack of the Allied Powers Allies_of_World_War_I on the Dardanelles during the 1915 Battle of Gallipoli in the course of World War I World_War_I.

The ancient city City of Troy was located near the western entrance of the strait, and the strait's Asiatic shore was the focus of the Trojan War Trojan_War. Troy was able to control the marine traffic entering this vital waterway Waterway. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire army of Xerxes I Xerxes_I of Persia Achaemenid_Empire and later the Macedonian army Ancient_Macedonian_army of Alexander Alexander_the_Great crossed the Dardanelles in opposite directions to invade each other's lands, in 480 BC and 334 BC respectively.

According to Herodotus (vv.34), both bridges were destroyed by a storm and Xerxes I Xerxes_I had those responsible for building the bridges beheaded and the strait itself whipped. Harpalus the engineer eventually helped the invading armies to cross by lashing the ships together with their bows facing the current and, so it is said, two additional anchors.

The Dardanelles were vital to the defence of Constantinople during the Byzantine Byzantium period.

Also, the Dardanelles was an important source of income for the ruler of the region. At the Istanbul Archaeological Museum İstanbul_Archaeology_Museums a marble Marble plate contains a law by the Byzantine Emperor Anastasius I Anastasius_I_DicorusAnastasius I Anastasius_I_Dicorus), that regulated fees for passage through the customs office of the Dardanelles. Translation:

... Whoever dares to violate these regulations shall no longer be regarded as a friend, and he shall be punished. Besides, the administrator of the Dardanelles must have the right to receive 50 golden Litrons, so that these rules, which we make out of piety, shall never ever be violated... ... The distinguished governor and major of the capital, who already has both hands full of things to do, has turned to our lofty piety in order to reorganize the entry and exit of all ships through the Dardanelles... ... Starting from our day and also in the future, anybody who wants to pass through the Dardanelles must pay the following:
– All wine Wine merchants who bring wine Wine to the capital (Constantinopolis Constantinople), except Cilicians, have to pay the Dardanelles officials 6 follis Follis and 2 sextarius Ancient_Roman_units_of_measurement of wine Wine.
– In the same manner, all merchants of olive-oil, vegetables and lard Lard must pay the Dardanelles officials 6 follis Follis. Cilician sea-merchants have to pay 3 follis Follis and in addition to that, 1 keration (12 follis Follis) to enter, and 2 keration to exit.
– All wheat Wheat merchants have to pay the officials 3 follis Follis per modius Ancient_Roman_units_of_measurement, and a further sum of 3 follis Follis when leaving.
