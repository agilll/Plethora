Miltiades (/mɪlˈtaɪəˌdiːz/; Greek Ancient_Greece: Μιλτιάδης; c. 550 – 489 BC), also known as Miltiades the Younger, was an Athenian History_of_Athens citizen known mostly for his role in the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, as well as for his downfall afterwards. He was the son of Cimon Coalemos Cimon_Coalemos, a renowned Olympic chariot-racer Chariot_racing, and the father of Cimon, the noted Athenian History_of_Athens statesman Winston_Churchill.

Miltiades was a well-born Athenian History_of_Athens, and considered himself a member of the Aeacidae,[1] as well as a member of the prominent Philaid Philaidae clan Clan. He came of age during the tyranny of the Peisistratids Peisistratos.

His family was prominent, due in good part to their success with Olympic chariot-racing.[1][2] Plutarch claimed that Cimon, Miltiades' father, was known as "Coalemos", meaning "simpleton", because he had a reputation for being rough around the edges,[3] but whose 3 successive chariot-racing victories at the Olympics made him popular, so popular in fact that, Herodotus claims, Peisistratos murdered him out of jealousy.[4]

Miltiades was named after his father's maternal half-brother, Miltiades the Elder, who was also a victor at Olympic chariot-racing.

Miltiades's son Cimon was a major Athenian History_of_Athens figure of the 470s and 460s BC 460s_BC. His daughter Elpinice is remembered for her confrontations with Pericles, as recorded by Plutarch.

Around 555 BC 555_BC, Miltiades the Elder left Athens History_of_Athens to establish a colony Colonies_in_antiquity on the Thracian Chersonese Gallipoli (now the Gallipoli Peninsula Gallipoli), setting himself up as a semi-autonomous tyrant Tyrant under the protection of Athens History_of_Athens.[5] Meanwhile, contrary to what one would expect from a man whose father was rumoured to have been murdered by the city City leaders, Miltiades the Younger rose through the ranks of Athens History_of_Athens to become eponymous archon Archon under the rule of the Peisistratid Peisistratos tyrant Tyrant Hippias in 524/23 BC.[6]

Miltiades the Elder was childless, so when he died around 520 BC,[7] his nephew, Miltiades the Younger's brother, Stesagoras, inherited the tyranny of the Chersonese Gallipoli. Four years later (516 BC 516_BC), Stesagoras met his death by an axe Axe to the head,[8] so the tyrant Tyrant Hippias sent Miltiades the Younger to claim his brother's lands.[9] Stesagoras' reign had been tumultuous, full of war and revolt. Wishing to achieve stronger control over his lands than his brother had, Miltiades feigned mourning for his brother's death. When the men of rank from the Chersonese Gallipoli came to console him, he imprisoned them. He then ensured his power by employing 500 troops. He also made an alliance with King Olorus of Thrace by marrying his daughter, Hegesipyle.[10]

In around 513 BC, Darius I Darius_I, the king of Persia Achaemenid_Empire, led a large army into the area, forcing the Thracian Chersonese Gallipoli into submission and making Miltiades a vassal Vassal of Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule. Miltiades joined Darius I Darius_I' northern expedition against the Scythians, and was left with other Greek Ancient_Greece officers to guard a bridge Contract_bridge across the Danube, which Darius I Darius_I had used to cross into Scythia Scythians. Miltiades later claimed that he had tried to convince the other officers to destroy the bridge Contract_bridge and leave Darius I Darius_I and his forces to die, but the others were afraid, and Darius I Darius_I was able to recross,[11] though some historians are skeptical of this claim.[12][13] When the king heard of the planned sabotage, Miltiades' rule became a perilous affair and he had to flee around 511/510 BC. Miltiades joined the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt of 499 BC against Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule, returning to the Chersonese Gallipoli around 496 BC. He established friendly relations with Athens History_of_Athens by capturing the islands of Lemnos and Imbros, which he then ceded to Athens History_of_Athens, who had ancient claims to these lands.[14][15]

The Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt collapsed in 494 BC, and in 492 BC Miltiades and his family fled to Athens History_of_Athens in five ships to escape a retaliatory Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion.[n 1]

The Athens History_of_Athens to which Miltiades returned was no longer a tyranny, but had overthrown the Peisistratids Peisistratos and become a democracy Athenian_democracy 15 years earlier. Thus, Miltiades initially faced a hostile reception for his tyrannical rule in the Thracian Chersonese Gallipoli.
