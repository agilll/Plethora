The Battle of Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae was fought between an alliance of Greek Ancient_Greece city-states, led by King Leonidas Leonidas_I of Sparta, and the Persian Empire of Xerxes I Xerxes_I over the course of three days, during the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion was a delayed response to the defeat of the first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, which had been ended by the Athenian Classical_Athens victory at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon in 490 BC. By 480 BC Xerxes I Xerxes_I had amassed a huge army and navy, and set out to conquer all of Greece Ancient_Greece. The Athenian Classical_Athens politician and general Themistocles had proposed that the allied Greeks Ancient_Greece block the advance of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army at the pass of Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae, and simultaneously block the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy at the Straits of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium.

A Greek Ancient_Greece force of approximately 7,000 men marched north to block the pass in the middle of 480 BC. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire army, alleged by the ancient sources to have numbered over one million, but today considered to have been much smaller (various figures are given by scholars, ranging between about 100,000 and 150,000), arrived at the pass in late August or early September. The vastly outnumbered Greeks Ancient_Greece held off the Persians Achaemenid_Empire for seven days (including three of battle Battle) before the rear-guard was annihilated in one of history's most famous last stands. During two full days of battle Battle, the small force led by Leonidas Leonidas_I blocked the only road by which the massive Persian Achaemenid_Empire army could pass. After the second day, a local resident named Ephialtes Ephialtes_of_Trachis betrayed the Greeks Ancient_Greece by revealing that a small path led behind the Greek Greek_language lines. Leonidas Leonidas_I, aware that his force was being outflanked, dismissed the bulk of the Greek Greek_language army and remained to guard their retreat with 300 Spartans Battle_of_Thermopylae, 700 Thespians Thespiae, and 400 Thebans Thebes,_Greece, fighting to the death.

Themistocles was in command of the Greek Navy Hellenic_Navy at Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium when he received news that the Persians Achaemenid_Empire had taken the pass at Thermopylae. Since the Greek Greek_language strategy required both Thermopylae and Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium to be held, given their losses, it was decided to withdraw to Salamis Salamis_Island. The Persians The_Persians overran Boeotia and then captured the evacuated Athens Classical_Athens. The Greek Greek_language fleet—seeking a decisive victory over the Persian Achaemenid_Empire armada—attacked and defeated the invaders at the Battle of Salamis Salamis_Island in late 480 BC. Wary of being trapped in Europe, Xerxes I Xerxes_I withdrew with much of his army to Asia Anatolia (losing most to starvation and disease), leaving Mardonius to attempt to complete the conquest of Greece Ancient_Greece. However, the following year saw a Greek Ancient_Greece army decisively defeat the Persians Achaemenid_Empire at the Battle of Plataea, thereby ending the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion.

Both ancient and modern writers have used the Battle of Thermopylae as an example of the power of a patriotic army defending its native soil. The performance of the defenders is also used as an example of the advantages of training, equipment, and good use of terrain as force multipliers and has become a symbol of courage against overwhelming odds.
