The Battle of Plataea was the final land battle Battle during the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece. It took place in 479 BC near the city City of Plataea in Boeotia, and was fought between an alliance of the Greek Ancient_Greece city-states (including Sparta, Athens History_of_Athens, Corinth and Megara), and the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Xerxes I Xerxes_I.

The previous year the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion force, led by the Persian Achaemenid_Empire king in person, had scored victories at the battles of Thermopylae and Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium and conquered Thessaly, Phocis, Boeotia, Euboea and Attica. However, at the ensuing Battle of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I Greek navy Hellenic_Navy had won an unlikely but decisive victory, preventing the conquest of the Peloponnesus Peloponnese. Xerxes I Xerxes_I then retreated with much of his army, leaving his general Mardonius to finish off the Greeks Ancient_Greece the following year.

In the summer of 479 BC the Greeks Ancient_Greece assembled a huge (by ancient standards) army and marched out of the Peloponnesus Peloponnese. The Persians The_Persians retreated to Boeotia and built a fortified camp near Plataea. The Greeks Ancient_Greece, however, refused to be drawn into the prime cavalry Cavalry terrain around the Persian Achaemenid_Empire camp, resulting in a stalemate that lasted 11 days. While attempting a retreat after their supply lines were disrupted, the Greek Ancient_Greece battle Battle line fragmented. Thinking the Greeks Ancient_Greece in full retreat, Mardonius ordered his forces to pursue them, but the Greeks Ancient_Greece (particularly the Spartans Spartan_army, Tegeans and Athenians) halted and gave battle Battle, routing the lightly armed Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry and killing Mardonius.

A large portion of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army was trapped in its camp and slaughtered. The destruction of this army, and the remnants of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy allegedly on the same day at the Battle of Mycale Battle_of_Mycale, decisively ended the invasion. After Plataea and Mycale Battle_of_Mycale the Greek Ancient_Greece allies would take the offensive against the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, marking a new phase of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. Although Plataea was in every sense a resounding victory, it does not seem to have been attributed the same significance (even at the time) as, for example, the Athenian History_of_Athens victory at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon or the Spartan defeat at Thermopylae.

The Greek Ancient_Greece city-states of Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria had supported the unsuccessful Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt against the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Darius I Darius_I in 499–494 BC. The Persian Empire Roman_Empire was still relatively young and prone to revolts by its subject peoples.[1][2] Moreover, Darius I Darius_I was an usurper and had to spend considerable time putting down revolts against his rule.[1] The Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt threatened the integrity of his empire, and he thus vowed to punish those involved (especially those not already part of the empire).[3][4] Darius I Darius_I also saw the opportunity to expand his empire into the fractious world of Ancient Greece Ancient_Greece.[4]

The Allies Allies_of_World_War_I initially adopted a strategy of blocking land and sea approaches to southern Greece Ancient_Greece.[15] Thus, in August 480 BC, after hearing of Xerxes I Xerxes_I' approach, a small Allied Allies_of_World_War_I army led by Spartan King Leonidas Leonidas_I blocked the Pass of Thermopylae, while an Athenian-dominated navy sailed to the Straits of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium. Following Thermopylae, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army proceeded to burn and sack the Boeotian cities that had not surrendered, Plataea and Thespiae, before taking possession of the now-evacuated city City of Athens History_of_Athens. In some ways the run-up to Plataea resembled that at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon; there was a prolonged stalemate in which neither side risked attacking the other.[32] The reasons for this stalemate were primarily tactical, and similar to the situation at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon; the Greek Ancient_Greece hoplites Hoplite did not want to risk being outflanked by the Persian Achaemenid_Empire cavalry Cavalry and the lightly armed Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry could not hope to assault well-defended positions.[32][60]

According to Herodotus, both sides wished for a decisive battle Battle that would tip the war in their favor.[32][61] However, Lazenby believed that Mardonius' actions during the Plataea campaign were not consistent with an aggressive policy.[60] He interprets the Persian Achaemenid_Empire operations during the prelude not as attempts to force the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I into battle Battle but as attempts to force the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I into retreat (which indeed became the case).[62] Mardonius may have felt he had little to gain in battle Battle and that he could simply wait for the Greek Ancient_Greece alliance to fall apart (as it had nearly done over the winter).[60] There can be little doubt from Herodotus' account that Mardonius was prepared to accept battle Battle on his own terms, however. Regardless of the exact motives, the initial strategic situation allowed both sides to procrastinate, since food supplies were ample for both armies.[32][61] Under these conditions, the tactical considerations outweighed the strategic need for action.

When Mardonius' raids disrupted the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I supply chain Military_supply_chain_management, it forced the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I to rethink their strategy. The main source Main_Source for the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[102] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his 'Enquiries' (Greek Ancient_Greece – Historia; English Kingdom_of_England – (The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[97] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented 'history' as we know it.[97] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally".[97]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[103][104] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[104] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On The Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[105] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[106] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds which have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[107] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[107] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[108]
