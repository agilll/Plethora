Darius I Darius_I (c. 550-486 BCE Common_Era), also known as Darius I Darius_I the Great, was the third Persian Achaemenid_Empire King of the Achaemenid Empire. His reign lasted 36 years, from c. 522 to 486 BCE Common_Era; during this time the Persian Empire reached its peak. Darius I Darius_I led military campaigns in Europe, Greece Ancient_Greece, and even in the Indus Indus_River valley, conquering lands and expanding his empire. Not only resuming to military prowess, Darius I Darius_I also improved the legal and economic system and conducted impressive construction projects across the Persian Empire.

The most important primary sources, that tell us about his life and reign, are his inscriptions, the most famous example being the trilingual inscription, in Akkadian Akkadian_language or Babylonian Babylon, Elamite Elamite_language, and old Persian Achaemenid_Empire, carved on the Bisitun Behistun_Inscription (Behistun) rock relief Rock_relief from the village of the same name and from his palace at Persepolis. Also, accounts about his reign were chronicled by the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus.

Darius I Darius_I' rule was marked by vast military expeditions. After consolidating his power at home, he set off to secure the lands of Egypt, which had been conquered before by Cambyses II Cambyses_II, and in 519 BCE Common_Era he incorporated a large part of Egypt into his empire. The following year, in 518 BCE Common_Era, he conquered parts of India History_of_India, namely northern Punjab Punjab_(region) as his inscriptions testify. The Persian Empire witnessed many improvements during Darius I Darius_I' reign. He established 20 provinces or satrapies Satrap, with an archon Archon or satrap Satrap assigned to each. Neighbouring regions paid a fixed tribute; a fair amount was stipulated by a commission of Darius I Darius_I' trusted officials.

He also improved the legal system, using the Babylonian Babylon Hammurabi as a model and copying some of his laws completely. The laws were enforced by the judges of the empire, who needed to be incorruptible. Darius I Darius_I removed the previous native officials, replacing them with new people loyal to him. While the punishments may seem brutal today, ranging from mutilation Political_mutilation_in_Byzantine_culture to blinding, fairness was not omitted as punishment depended on the nature and severity of the crime. The new system proved to be popular, even after Darius I Darius_I’s death, some laws were still in use in 218 BCE Common_Era.

In the matters of religion, it is well known that Darius I Darius_I was an adherent of Zoroastrianism or at least a firm believer of Ahura Mazda Ahura_Mazda. This we can see in his inscriptions, where he attributes his success to Ahura Mazda Ahura_Mazda, and in his legal system where all laws were created in the name of the Zoroastrian Zoroastrianism god. In the lands that were under Persian Achaemenid_Empire control, all other religions were tolerated as long as they remained submissive and peaceful.

During his reign, Darius I Darius_I undertook impressive construction projects across the empire. In Susa, he built a palace complex in the northern part of the city City, which became his favourite residence. A grand project in Persepolis followed; the palace complex included a military quarter, treasury, the king’s quarters and guest house. Besides the palaces, Darius I Darius_I also finished previously incomplete construction projects of Cyrus Cyrus_the_Great in Pasargadae. In Egypt, he built many temples and restored those that had previously been destroyed.

Darius I Darius_I introduced a new universal currency Currency, the darayaka, sometime before 500 BCE Common_Era. This innovation made it easier to collect taxes on land, livestock, and marketplaces, which led to improved revenues for the empire. To further improve the economy and help traders, a new standardised system of weights and measures was introduced.

After the defeat at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, Darius I Darius_I did not want to give up on his dream to conquer Greece Ancient_Greece. He vowed to gather an even larger army, this time leading it personally, to fight the Greeks Ancient_Greece. After three years of preparing, during which he became ill, a revolt broke out in Egypt that only worsened his condition. Darius I Darius_I died in October 486 BCE Common_Era; his body was interred at Naqsh-e Rustam Naqsh-e_Rustam in a tomb prepared by him beforehand, a custom of Persian Achaemenid_Empire kings. After his death, the throne was inherited by his eldest son from his marriage with Atossa, Xerxes Xerxes_I.
