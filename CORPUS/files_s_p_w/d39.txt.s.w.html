The Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon (Greek Ancient_Greece: Μάχη τοῦ Μαραθῶνος, Machē tou Marathōnos) took place in 490 BC, during the first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece. It was fought between the citizens of Athens History_of_Athens, aided by Plataea, and a Persian Achaemenid_Empire force commanded by Datis and Artaphernes. The battle Battle was the culmination of the first attempt by Persia Achaemenid_Empire, under King Darius I Darius_I, to subjugate Greece Ancient_Greece. The Greek Ancient_Greece army decisively defeated the more numerous Persians Achaemenid_Empire, marking a turning point in the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars.

The first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion was a response to Athenian History_of_Athens involvement in the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, when Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria had sent a force to support the cities of Ionia in their attempt to overthrow Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule. The Athenians and Eretrians had succeeded in capturing and burning Sardis, but they were then forced to retreat with heavy losses. In response to this raid, Darius I Darius_I swore to burn down Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria. According to Herodotus, Darius I Darius_I had his bow Bow_and_arrow brought to him and then shot an arrow "upwards towards heaven", saying as he did so: "Zeus, that it may be granted me to take vengeance upon the Athenians!". Herodotus further writes that Darius I Darius_I charged one of his servants to say "Master, remember the Athenians" three times before dinner each day.[4]

At the time of the battle Battle, Sparta and Athens History_of_Athens were the two largest city-states in Greece Ancient_Greece. Once the Ionian revolt Ionian_Revolt was finally crushed by the Persian Achaemenid_Empire victory at the Battle of Lade Miletus in 494 BC, Darius I Darius_I began plans to subjugate Greece Ancient_Greece. In 490 BC, he sent a naval task force under Datis and Artaphernes across the Aegean Aegean_Sea, to subjugate the Cyclades, and then to make punitive attacks on Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria. Reaching Euboea in mid-summer after a successful campaign in the Aegean Aegean_Sea, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire proceeded to besiege and capture Eretria. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire force then sailed for Attica, landing in the bay near the town of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. The Athenians, joined by a small force from Plataea, marched to Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, and succeeded in blocking the two exits from the plain Great_Plains of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. The Athenians also sent a message asking for support to the Spartans Spartan_army. When the messenger arrived in Sparta, the Spartans Spartan_army were involved in a religious festival and gave this as a reason for not coming to aid of the Athenians.

The Athenians and their allies chose a location for the battle Battle, with marshes and mountainous terrain, that prevented the Persian Achaemenid_Empire cavalry Cavalry from joining the Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry. Miltiades, the Athenian History_of_Athens general, ordered a general attack against the Persian Achaemenid_Empire forces, composed primarily of missile Missile troops. He reinforced his flanks, luring the Persians Achaemenid_Empire' best fighters into his center. The inward wheeling flanks enveloped the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, routing them. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire army broke in panic towards their ships, and large numbers were slaughtered. The defeat at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon marked the end of the first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, and the Persian Achaemenid_Empire force retreated to Asia Anatolia. Darius I Darius_I then began raising a huge new army with which he meant to completely subjugate Greece Ancient_Greece; however, in 486 BC, his Egyptian Ancient_Egypt subjects revolted, indefinitely postponing any Greek Ancient_Greece expedition. After Darius I Darius_I died, his son Xerxes I Xerxes_I restarted the preparations for a second invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, which finally began in 480 BC.

The Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon was a watershed Drainage_basin in the Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars, showing the Greeks Ancient_Greece that the Persians Achaemenid_Empire could be beaten; the eventual Greek Ancient_Greece triumph in these wars can be seen to begin at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. Since the following two hundred years saw the rise of the Classical Greek Ancient_Greece civilization, which has been enduringly influential in western society, the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon is often seen as a pivotal moment in Mediterranean Mediterranean_Sea and European history.

The main source Main_Source for the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the "Father of History",[5] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his Enquiries (Greek Ancient_Greece – Historia; English Kingdom_of_England – (The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ended in 450 BC).[6] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented "history" as we know it.[6] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[6]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[7][8] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and may therefore have felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[8] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay On the malice of Herodotus, describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[9] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[10] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds which have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[11] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[11] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[12]

The first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece had its immediate roots in the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, the earliest phase of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. Herodotus explicitly tells us that the Greeks Ancient_Greece attacked the Persians Achaemenid_Empire (and the other sources confirm this), but it is not clear why they did this before the arrival of the Spartans Spartan_army.[51] There are two main theories to explain this.[51]

The first theory is that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire cavalry Cavalry left Marathon Battle_of_Marathon for an unspecified reason, and that the Greeks Ancient_Greece moved to take advantage of this by attacking. This theory is based on the absence of any mention of cavalry Cavalry in Herodotus' account of the battle Battle, and an entry in the Suda dictionary.[51] The entry χωρίς ἰππεῖς ("without cavalry Cavalry") is explained thus:

The cavalry Cavalry left. When Datis surrendered and was ready for retreat, the Ionians climbed the trees and gave the Athenians the signal that the cavalry Cavalry had left. And when Miltiades realized that, he attacked and thus won. From there comes the above-mentioned quote, which is used when someone breaks ranks before battle Battle.[54]

There are many variations of this theory, but perhaps the most prevalent is that the cavalry Cavalry were completing the time-consuming process of re-embarking on the ships, and were to be sent by sea to attack (undefended) Athens History_of_Athens in the rear, whilst the rest of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire pinned down the Athenian History_of_Athens army at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon.[43] This theory therefore utilises Herodotus' suggestion that after Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army began to re-embark, intending to sail around Cape Sounion Sounion to attack Athens History_of_Athens directly.[55] Thus, this re-embarcation would have occurred before the battle Battle (and indeed have triggered the battle Battle).[53]

The second theory is simply that the battle Battle occurred because the Persians Achaemenid_Empire finally moved to attack the Athenians.[51] Although this theory has the Persians Achaemenid_Empire moving to the strategic offensive Offensive_(military), this can be reconciled with the traditional account of the Athenians attacking the Persians Achaemenid_Empire by assuming that, seeing the Persians Achaemenid_Empire advancing, the Athenians took the tactical offensive, and attacked them.[51] Obviously, it cannot be firmly established which theory (if either) is correct. However, both theories imply that there was some kind of Persian Achaemenid_Empire activity which occurred on or about the fifth day which ultimately triggered the battle Battle.[51] It is also possible that both theories are correct: when the Persians Achaemenid_Empire sent the cavalry Cavalry by ship to attack Athens History_of_Athens, they simultaneously sent their infantry to attack at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, triggering the Greek counterattack Greco-Persian_Wars.

From a strategic point of view, the Athenians had some disadvantages at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. In order to face the Persians Achaemenid_Empire in battle Battle, the Athenians had to summon all available hoplites Hoplite;[43] and even then they were still probably outnumbered at least 2 to 1.[47] Furthermore, raising such a large army had denuded Athens History_of_Athens of defenders, and thus any secondary attack in the Athenian History_of_Athens rear would cut the army off from the city City; and any direct attack on the city City could not be defended against.[53] Still further, defeat at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon would mean the complete defeat of Athens History_of_Athens, since no other Athenian History_of_Athens army existed. The Athenian History_of_Athens strategy was therefore to keep the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army pinned down at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, blocking both exits from the plain Great_Plains, and thus preventing themselves from being outmaneuvered.[43] However, these disadvantages were balanced by some advantages. The Athenians initially had no need to seek battle Battle, since they had managed to confine Confine the Persians Achaemenid_Empire to the plain Great_Plains of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. Furthermore, time worked in their favour, as every day brought the arrival of the Spartans Spartan_army closer.[43][51] Having everything to lose by attacking, and much to gain by waiting, the Athenians remained on the defensive in the run up to the battle Battle.[51] Tactically, hoplites Hoplite were vulnerable to attacks by cavalry Cavalry, and since the Persians Achaemenid_Empire had substantial numbers of cavalry Cavalry, this made any offensive maneuver by the Athenians even more of a risk, and thus reinforced the defensive strategy of the Athenians.[53]

The Persian Achaemenid_Empire strategy, on the other hand, was probably principally determined by tactical considerations. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry was evidently lightly armoured, and no match for hoplites Hoplite in a head-on confrontation (as would be demonstrated at the later battles of Thermopylae and Plataea.[78]) Since the Athenians seem to have taken up a strong defensive position at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire hesitance was probably a reluctance to attack the Athenians head-on.[53]

Whatever event eventually triggered the battle Battle, it obviously altered the strategic or tactical balance sufficiently to induce the Athenians to attack the Persians Achaemenid_Empire. If the first theory is correct (see above), then the absence of cavalry Cavalry removed the main Athenian History_of_Athens tactical disadvantage, and the threat of being outflanked made it imperative Imperative_mood to attack.[53] Conversely, if the second theory is correct, then the Athenians were merely reacting to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire attacking them.[51] Since the Persian Achaemenid_Empire force obviously contained a high proportion of missile Missile troops, a static defensive position would have made little sense for the Athenians;[79] the strength of the hoplite Hoplite was in the melee, and the sooner that could be brought about, the better, from the Athenian History_of_Athens point of view.[78] If the second theory is correct, this raises the further question of why the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, having hesitated for several days, then attacked. There may have been several strategic reasons for this; perhaps they were aware (or suspected) that the Athenians were expecting reinforcements.[51] Alternatively, since they may have felt the need to force some kind of victory—they could hardly remain at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon indefinitely.[51]
