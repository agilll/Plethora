Miletus was an ancient Greek Ancient_Greece city City on the western coast of Anatolia, near the mouth of the Maeander River Büyük_Menderes_River in ancient Caria.[3][4][5] Its ruins are located near the modern village of Balat Balat,_Fatih in Aydın Province Aidin_Eyalet, Turkey Ottoman_Empire. Before the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion in the middle of the 6th century BC, Miletus was considered the greatest and wealthiest of Greek Ancient_Greece cities.[6][7]

Evidence of first settlement at the site has been made inaccessible by the rise of sea level and deposition of sediments from the Maeander Büyük_Menderes_River. The first available evidence is of the Neolithic. In the early and middle Bronze age Bronze_Age the settlement came under Minoan Minoan_civilization influence. Legend has it that an influx of Cretans occurred displacing the indigenous Leleges. The site was renamed Miletus after a place in Crete.

The Late Bronze Age Bronze_Age, 13th century BC, saw the arrival of Luwian language Luwian_language speakers from south central Anatolia calling themselves the Carians. Later in that century other Greeks Ancient_Greece arrived. The city City at that time rebelled against the Hittite Empire Roman_Empire. After the fall of that empire the city City was destroyed in the 12th century BC and starting about 1000 BC was resettled extensively by the Ionian Greeks Ionia. Legend offers an Ionian Ionia foundation event sponsored by a founder named Neleus from the Peloponnesus Peloponnese.

The Greek Dark Ages Greek_Dark_Ages were a time of Ionian Ionia settlement and consolidation in an alliance called the Ionian League Ionian_League. The Archaic Period Archaic_Greece of Greece Ancient_Greece began with a sudden and brilliant flash of art and philosophy Ancient_Greek_philosophy on the coast of Anatolia. In the 6th century BC, Miletus was the site of origin of the Greek Ancient_Greece philosophical (and scientific) tradition, when Thales, followed by Anaximander and Anaximenes Anaximenes_of_Miletus (known collectively, to modern scholars, as the Milesian School Milesian_school) began to speculate about the material constitution of the world, and to propose speculative naturalistic (as opposed to traditional, supernatural Supernatural) explanations for various natural phenomena.

In antiquity Ancient_Greece the city City possessed a Harbor at the southern entry of a large bay, on which two more of the traditional twelve Ionian Ionia cities stood: Priene and Myus. The harbor of Miletus was additionally protected by the nearby small island Small_Island_(TV_film) of Lade Miletus. Over the centuries the gulf silted up with alluvium Alluvium carried by the Meander River Büyük_Menderes_River. Priene and Myus had lost their harbors by the Roman era Ancient_Rome, and Miletus itself became an inland town in the early Christian Christianity era; all three were abandoned to ruin as their economies were strangled by the lack of access to the sea. There is a Great Harbor Monument where, according to the New Testament New_Testament account, the apostle Paul Paul_the_Apostle stopped on his way back to Jerusalem by boat. He met the Ephesian Elders and then headed out to the beach to bid them farewell, recorded in the book of Acts 20 Acts_20:17-38.

During the Pleistocene epoch Geologic_time_scale the Miletus region was submerged in the Aegean Sea Aegean_Sea. It subsequently emerged slowly, the sea reaching a low level of about 130 meters (430 ft) below present level at about 18,000 BP Radiocarbon_dating. The site of Miletus was part of the mainland.

A gradual rise brought a level of about 1.75 meters (5 ft 9 in) below present at about 5500 BP Radiocarbon_dating, creating several karst Karst block islands of limestone Limestone, the location of the first settlements at Miletus. At about 1500 BC the karst Karst shifted due to small crustal movements and the islands consolidated into a peninsula. Since then the sea has risen 1.75 m but the peninsula has been surrounded by sediment Sediment from the Maeander Büyük_Menderes_River river and is now land-locked. Sedimentation of the harbor began at about 1000 BC, and by AD 300 Lake Bafa Lake_Bafa_Nature_Park had been created.[8]

Miletus is known to have early ties with Megara in Greece Ancient_Greece. According to some scholars, these two cities had built up a “colonisation alliance”. In the 7th/6th century BCE Common_Era they acted in accordance with each other.[17]

Both cities acted under the leadership and sanction of an Apollo oracle Delphi. Megara cooperated with that of Delphi. Miletus had her own oracle Delphi of Apollo Didymeus Milesios in Didyma. Also, there are many parallels in the political organisation of both cities.[17]

By the 6th century BC, Miletus had earned a maritime empire with many colonies, but brushed up against powerful Lydia at home, and the tyrant Tyrant Polycrates of its neighbor to the west, Samos.
