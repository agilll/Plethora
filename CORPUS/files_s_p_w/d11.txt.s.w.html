Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae is a mountain Mountain_warfare pass near the sea in northern Greece Ancient_Greece which was the site of several battles in antiquity Ancient_Greece, the most famous being that between Persians Achaemenid_Empire and Greeks Ancient_Greece in August 480 BCE Common_Era. Despite being greatly inferior in numbers, the Greeks Ancient_Greece held the narrow pass for three days with Spartan King Leonidas Leonidas_I fighting a last-ditch defence with a small force of Spartans Spartan_army and other Greek Ancient_Greece hoplites Hoplite. Ultimately the Persians Achaemenid_Empire took control of the pass, but the heroic defeat of Leonidas Leonidas_I would assume legendary proportions for later generations of Greeks Ancient_Greece, and within a year the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion would be repulsed at the battles of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis and Plataea Battle_of_Plataea.

By the first years of the 5th century BCE Common_Era, Persia Achaemenid_Empire, under the rule of Darius Darius_I (r. 522-486 BCE Common_Era), was already expanding into mainland Europe and had subjugated Thrace and Macedonia Republic_of_Macedonia. Next in king Darius Darius_I’ sights were Athens Classical_Athens and the rest of Greece Ancient_Greece. Just why Greece Ancient_Greece was coveted by Persia Achaemenid_Empire is unclear. Wealth and resources seem an unlikely motive; other more plausible suggestions include the need to increase the prestige of the king at home or to quell once and for all a collection of potentially troublesome rebel states on the western border of the empire.

Whatever the exact motives, in 491 BCE Common_Era Darius Darius_I sent envoys to call for the Greeks Ancient_Greece’ submission to Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule. The Greeks Ancient_Greece sent a no-nonsense reply by executing the envoys, and Athens Classical_Athens and Sparta promised to form an alliance for the defence of Greece Ancient_Greece. Darius Darius_I’ response to this diplomatic outrage was to launch a naval force of 600 ships and 25,000 men to attack the Cyclades and Euboea, leaving the Persians Achaemenid_Empire just one step away from the rest of Greece Ancient_Greece. In 490 BCE Common_Era Greek Ancient_Greece forces led by Athens Classical_Athens met the Persians Achaemenid_Empire in battle Battle at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon and defeated the invaders. The battle Battle would take on mythical status amongst the Greeks Ancient_Greece, but in reality it was merely the opening overture Overture of a long war with several other battles making up the principal acts. Persia Achaemenid_Empire, with the largest empire List_of_largest_empires in the world, was vastly superior in men and resources and now these would be fully utilised for a full-scale attack.

In 486 BCE Common_Era Xerxes Xerxes_I became king upon the death of Darius Darius_I and massive preparations for invasion were made. Depots of equipment and supplies were laid, a canal dug at Chalkidike Chalkidiki, and boat bridges built across the Hellespont Dardanelles to facilitate the movement of troops. Greece Ancient_Greece was about to face its greatest ever threat, and even the oracle Delphi at Delphi ominously advised the Athenians to ‘fly to the world’s end’.

When news of the invading force reached Greece Ancient_Greece, the initial Greek Ancient_Greece reaction was to send a force of 10,000 hoplites Hoplite to hold position at the valley of Tempē near Mt. Olympos Mount_Olympus, but these withdrew when the massive size of the invading army was revealed. Then after much discussion and compromise between Greek Ancient_Greece city-states, suspicious of each others' motives, a joint army of between 6,000 and 7,000 men was sent to defend the pass at Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae through which the Persians Achaemenid_Empire must enter mainland Greece Ancient_Greece. The Greek Ancient_Greece forces included 300 Spartans Battle_of_Thermopylae and their helots Helots with 2,120 Arcadians, 1,000 Lokrians, 1,000 Phokians, 700 Thespians Thespiae, 400 Corinthians Ancient_Corinth, 400 Thebans Thebes,_Greece, 200 men from Phleious, and 80 Mycenaeans Mycenaean_Greece.

The relatively small size of the defending force has been explained as a reluctance by some Greek Ancient_Greece city-states to commit troops so far north, and/or due to religious motives, for it was the period of the sacred games Sacred_Games_(novel) at Olympia Opel_Olympia and the most important Spartan religious festival, the Karneia Carnea, and no fighting was permitted during these events. Indeed, for this very reason, the Spartans Spartan_army had arrived too late at the earlier battle Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. Therefore, the Spartans Spartan_army, widely credited as being the best fighters in Greece Ancient_Greece and the only polis with a professional army, contributed only a small advance force of 300 hoplites Hoplite (from an estimated 8,000 available) to the Greek Ancient_Greece defensive force, these few being chosen from men with male heirs.

In addition to the land forces, the Greek Ancient_Greece poleis Polis sent a fleet of trireme Trireme warships which held position off the coast of Artemision Artemisium (or Artemesium) on the northern coast of Euboea, 40 nautical miles from Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae. The Greeks Ancient_Greece would amass over 300 triremes and perhaps their main purpose was to prevent the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet sailing down the inland coast of Lokris Locris and Boeotia.

The pass of Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae, located 150 km north of Athens Classical_Athens was an excellent choice for defence with steep mountains running down into the sea leaving only a narrow marshy area along the coast. The pass had also been fortified by the local Phokians who built a defensive wall Defensive_wall running from the so-called Middle Gate down to the sea. The wall was in a state of ruin, but the Spartans Spartan_army made the best repairs they could in the circumstances. It was here, then, in a 15 metre wide gap with a sheer cliff Cliff protecting their left flank and the sea on their right, that the Greeks Ancient_Greece chose to make a stand against the invading army. Having somewhere in the region of 80,000 troops at his disposal, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire king, who led the invasion in person, first waited four days in expectation that the Greeks Ancient_Greece would flee in panic. When the Greeks Ancient_Greece held their position, Xerxes Xerxes_I once again sent envoys to offer the defenders a last chance to surrender without bloodshed if the Greeks Ancient_Greece would only lay down their arms. Leonidas Leonidas_I’ bullish response to Xerxes Xerxes_I request was ‘molōn labe Elbe’ or ‘come and get them’ and so battle Battle commenced.

The two opposing armies were essentially representative of the two approaches to Classical warfare - the Persians Achaemenid_Empire favoured long-range assault using archers followed up with a cavalry Cavalry charge, whilst the Greeks Ancient_Greece favoured heavily-armoured hoplites Hoplite, arranged in a densely packed formation called the phalanx Phalanx, with each man carrying a heavy round bronze shield and fighting at close quarters using spears and swords. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry carried a lightweight (often crescent-shaped) wicker shield and were armed with a long dagger Dagger or battleaxe, a short spear, and composite bow Composite_bow. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire forces also included the Immortals Immortality, an elite force of 10,000 who were probably better protected with armour and armed with spears. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire cavalry Cavalry were armed as the foot soldiers, with a bow Bow_and_arrow and an additional two javelins for throwing and thrusting. Cavalry, usually operating on the flanks of the main battle Battle, were used to mop up opposing infantry put in disarray after they had been subjected to repeated salvos from the archers. Although the Persians Achaemenid_Empire had enjoyed the upper hand in previous contests during the recent Ionian revolt Ionian_Revolt, the terrain at Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae would better suit the Greek Ancient_Greece approach to warfare.

Although the Persian Achaemenid_Empire tactic of rapidly firing vast numbers of arrows into the enemy must have been an awesome sight, the lightness of the arrows meant that they were largely ineffective against the bronze-armoured hoplites Hoplite. Indeed, Spartan indifference is epitomised by Dieneces, who, when told that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire arrows would be so dense as to darken the sun, replied that in that case the Spartans Spartan_army would have the pleasure of fighting in the shade. At close quarters At_Close_Quarters, the longer spears, heavier swords, better armour, and rigid discipline of the phalanx formation Phalanx meant that the Greek Ancient_Greece hoplites Hoplite would have all of the advantages, and in the narrow confines of the terrain, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire would struggle to make their vastly superior numbers tell.

On the first day Xerxes Xerxes_I sent his Median Achaemenid_Empire and Kissian troops, and after their failure to clear the pass, the elite Immortals Immortality entered the battle Battle but in the brutal close-quarter fighting, the Greeks Ancient_Greece held firm. The Greek Ancient_Greece tactic of feigning a disorganised retreat and then turning on the enemy in the phalanx formation Phalanx also worked well, lessening the threat from Persian Achaemenid_Empire arrows and perhaps the hoplites Hoplite surprised the Persians Achaemenid_Empire with their disciplined mobility, a benefit of being a professionally trained army.

The second day followed the pattern of the first, and the Greek Ancient_Greece forces still held the pass. However, an unscrupulous traitor was about to tip the balance in favour of the invaders. Ephialtes Ephialtes_of_Trachis, son of Eurydemos, a local shepherd from Trachis, seeking reward from Xerxes Xerxes_I, informed the Persians Achaemenid_Empire of an alternative route --the Anopaia path-- which would allow them to avoid the majority of the enemy forces and attack their southern flank. Leonidas Leonidas_I had stationed the contingent of Phokian Phocis_(ancient_region) troops to guard this vital point but they, thinking themselves the primary target of this new development, withdrew to a higher defensive position when the Immortals Immortality attacked. This suited the Persians Achaemenid_Empire as they could now continue unimpeded along the mountain Mountain_warfare path and arrive behind the main Greek Ancient_Greece force. With their position now seemingly hopeless, and before their retreat was cut off completely, the bulk of the Greek Ancient_Greece forces were ordered to withdraw by Leonidas Leonidas_I.

The Spartan king, on the third day of the battle Battle, rallied his small force - the survivors from the original Spartan 300, 700 Thespians Thespiae and 400 Thebans Thebes,_Greece - and made a rearguard stand to defend the pass to the last man in the hope of delaying the Persians Achaemenid_Empire progress, in order to allow the rest of the Greek Ancient_Greece force to retreat or also possibly to await relief from a larger Greek Ancient_Greece force. Early in the morning, the hoplites Hoplite once more met the enemy, but this time Xerxes Xerxes_I could attack from both front and rear and planned to do so but, in the event, the Immortals Immortality behind the Greeks Ancient_Greece were late on arrival. Leonidas Leonidas_I moved his troops to the widest part of the pass to utilise all of his men at once, and in the ensuing clash the Spartan king was killed. His comrades then fought fiercely to recover the body of the fallen king. Meanwhile, the Immortals Immortality now entered the fray behind the Greeks Ancient_Greece who retreated to a high mound behind the Phokian Phocis_(ancient_region) wall. Perhaps at this point the Theban Theban_hegemony contingent may have surrendered (although this is disputed amongst scholars). The remaining The_Remaining hoplites Hoplite, now trapped and without their inspirational king, were subjected to a barrage of Persian Achaemenid_Empire arrows until no man was left standing. After the battle Battle, Xerxes Xerxes_I ordered that Leonidas Leonidas_I’ head be put on a stake and displayed at the battlefield. As Herodotus claims in his account of the battle Battle in book VII of The Histories Herodotus, the Oracle at Delphi had been proved right when she proclaimed that either Sparta or one of her kings must fall.

Meanwhile at Artemision Artemisium, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire were battling the elements rather than the Greeks Ancient_Greece, as they lost 400 triremes in a storm off the coast of Magnesia Battle_of_Magnesia and more in a second storm off Euboea. When the two fleets finally met, the Greeks Ancient_Greece fought late in the day and therefore limited the duration of each skirmish which diminished the numerical advantage held by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire. The result of the battle Battle was, however, indecisive and on news of Leonidas Leonidas_I’ defeat, the fleet withdrew to Salamis Battle_of_Salamis.

The battle Battle of Thermopylae Battle_of_Thermopylae, and particularly the Spartans Spartan_army' role in it, soon acquired mythical status amongst the Greeks Ancient_Greece. Free men, in respect of their own laws, had sacrificed themselves in order to defend their way of life against foreign aggression. As Simonedes’ epitaph Epitaph at the site of the fallen stated: ‘Go Go_(game) tell the Spartans Spartan_army, you who read: We took their orders and here lie dead’.

A glorious defeat maybe, but the fact remained that the way was now clear for Xerxes Xerxes_I to push on into mainland Greece Ancient_Greece. The Greeks Ancient_Greece, though, were far from finished, and despite many states now turning over to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire and Athens Classical_Athens itself being sacked, a Greek Ancient_Greece army led by Leonidas Leonidas_I’ brother Kleombrotos began to build a defensive wall Defensive_wall near Corinth. Winter halted the land campaign, though, and at Salamis Battle_of_Salamis the Greek Ancient_Greece fleet manoeuvred the Persians Achaemenid_Empire into shallow waters and won a resounding victory. Xerxes Xerxes_I returned home to his palace at Sousa John_Philip_Sousa and left the gifted Child_prodigy general Mardonius in charge of the invasion. After a series of political negotiations it became clear that the Persians Achaemenid_Empire would not gain victory through diplomacy and the two armies met at Plataea Battle_of_Plataea in August 479 BCE Common_Era. The Greeks Ancient_Greece, fielding the largest hoplite Hoplite army ever seen, won the battle Battle and finally ended Xerxes Xerxes_I’ ambitions in Greece Ancient_Greece.
