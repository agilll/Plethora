The Battle of Artemisium, or Battle of Artemision Artemisium, was a series of naval engagements over three days during the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece. The battle Battle took place simultaneously with the more famous land battle Battle at Thermopylae, in August or September 480 BC, off the coast of Euboea and was fought between an alliance of Greek Ancient_Greece city-states, including Sparta, Athens History_of_Athens, Corinth and others, and the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Xerxes I Xerxes_I.

The Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion was a delayed response to the defeat of the first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, which had been ended by the Athenian History_of_Athens victory at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon. King Xerxes Xerxes_IXerxes I Xerxes_Imassed a huge army and navy, and set out to conquer all of Greece Ancient_Greece. The Athenian History_of_Athens general Themistocles proposed that the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II Greeks Ancient_Greece block the advance of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army at the pass of Thermopylae and simultaneously block the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy at the Straits of Artemisium. An Allied Allies_of_World_War_II naval force of 271 triremes was thus dispatched to await the arrival of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.

Approaching Artemisium towards the end of summer, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy was caught in a gale off the coast of Magnesia Magnesia_Prefecture and lost around a third of their 1200 ships. After arriving at Artemisium, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire sent a detachment of 200 ships around the coast of Euboea in an attempt to trap the Greeks Ancient_Greece, but these were caught in another storm and shipwrecked. The main action of the battle Battle took place after two days of smaller engagements. The two sides fought all day, with roughly equal losses; however, the smaller Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet could not afford the losses.

After the engagement, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II received news of the defeat of the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II army at Thermopylae. Since their strategy required both Thermopylae and Artemisium to be held, and given their losses, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II decided to withdraw to Salamis Battle_of_Salamis. The Persians The_Persians overran and gained control over Phocis, then Boeotia, and finally entered Attica where they captured the now-evacuated Athens History_of_Athens. However, seeking a decisive victory over the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire were later defeated at the Battle of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis in late 480 BC. Fearing being trapped in Europe, Xerxes I Xerxes_I withdrew with much of his army to Asia Anatolia, leaving Mardonius to complete the conquest of Greece Ancient_Greece. The following year, however, saw an Allied Allies_of_World_War_II army decisively defeat the Persians Achaemenid_Empire at the Battle of Plataea, thereby ending the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion.

The main source Main_Source for the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[3] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his 'Enquiries' (Greek—Historia; English Kingdom_of_England—(The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–420 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[4] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented 'history' as we know it.[4] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[4]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[5][6] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[6] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On The Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[7] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[8] However, since the 19th century, archaeological finds that support his version of events have considerably rehabilitated his reputation.[9] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[9] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[10]

The Sicilian The_Sicilian historian Diodorus Siculus Diodorus_Siculus, writing in the 1st century BC in his Bibliotheca Historica Bibliotheca_historica, also provides an account of the Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars, partially derived from the earlier Greek Ancient_Greece historian Ephorus. This account is fairly consistent with Herodotus's.[11] The Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars are also described in less detail by a number of other ancient historians including Plutarch, Ctesias of Cnidus Knidos, and are referred to by other authors, such as the playwright Theatre Aeschylus. Archaeological evidence, such as the Serpent Column Serpent_Column, also supports some of Herodotus's specific claims.[12]

The Greek Ancient_Greece city-states of Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria had supported the unsuccessful Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt against the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Darius I Darius_I in 499-494 BC.
