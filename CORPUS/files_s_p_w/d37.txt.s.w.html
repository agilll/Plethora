Mardonius literally: "the mild one";[1] died 479 BC) was a leading Persian Achaemenid_Empire military commander during the Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars with Greece Ancient_Greece in the early 5th century BC who died at the Battle of Plataea Battle_of_Plataea.

Mardonius was the son of Gobryas, a Persian Achaemenid_Empire nobleman who had assisted the Achaemenid Achaemenid_Empire prince Darius Darius_I when he claimed the throne. The alliance between the new king and his friend was cemented by diplomatic marriages: Darius Darius_I married Gobryas' daughter, and Gobryas married Darius Darius_I' sister. Furthermore, Mardonius married Darius Darius_I' daughter Artozostra. Thus Darius Darius_I the Great was simultaneously Mardonius' uncle, father-in-law, and half-brother-in-law.

Darius Darius_I appointed Mardonius as one of his generals and, after the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, sent him in 492 BC to punish the Greek Ancient_Greece city-state Polis of Athens History_of_Athens for assisting the Ionians. On his way to Athens History_of_Athens, he used his army in the Ionian Ionia cities to depose the Greek Ancient_Greece tyrants and set up democratic governments, an action which surprised the Greeks Ancient_Greece at that time. Historians consider that he may have taken this action so that the Ionians would not revolt a second time after the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army had passed through.[2] His fleet and army then passed across the Hellespont Dardanelles. Mardonius first attacked Thasos, a Greek Ancient_Greece island which possessed gold mines. It became a tributary Vassal_state of the Achaemenid Achaemenid_Empire empire. The navy and the army continued onto Macedonia Republic_of_Macedonia, which was soon added to the Persian Empire as a fully subordinate client kingdom Client_state, becoming also part of its administrative system.[3][4]

However, after these victories, Mardonius’ fleet was destroyed in a storm off the coast near Mount Athos Mount_Athos. According to Herodotus, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire lost 300 ships and 20,000 men. Around this time, Mardonius was commanding the army in a battle Battle in Thrace. While Mardonius was wounded in the battle Battle, he was victorious, re-subjugating Thrace into the empire.[5] Nevertheless, the loss of the fleet meant that he had to retreat back into Asia Minor Anatolia.[6] He was relieved of his command by Darius Darius_I, who appointed Datis and Artaphernes junior to lead the invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece in 490 BC, and though they were subsequently successful in capturing Naxos and destroying Eretria, they were later defeated at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon.

Mardonius came back into favour under Darius Darius_I' successor Xerxes I Xerxes_I, Mardonius' cousin and brother-in-law. This view was opposed by another of Xerxes I Xerxes_I’ advisors, Artabanus Artabanus_of_Persia, who urged more caution in the matter. Herodotus, who portrays Mardonius as a somewhat evil adviser (as opposed to a number of other good advisers whose arguments are never followed), says that Mardonius simply wanted to become satrap Satrap (governor) of Greece Ancient_Greece and had a love for 'mischief and adventure'.[8]
