The second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece (480–479 BC) occurred during the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, as King Xerxes Xerxes_IXerxes I Xerxes_IPersia Achaemenid_Empireought to conquer all of Greece Ancient_Greece. The invasion began in spring 480 BC, when the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army crossed the Hellespont Dardanelles and marched through Thrace and Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia to Thessaly. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire advance was blocked at the pass of Thermopylae by a small Allied Allies_of_World_War_II force under King Leonidas Leonidas_I of Sparta; simultaneously, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet was blocked by an Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet at the straits of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium. However, a larger Allied Allies_of_World_War_II army fortified the narrow Isthmus of Corinth, protecting the Peloponnesus Peloponnese from Persian Achaemenid_Empire conquest. The main source Main_Source for the Great Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[3] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his 'Enquiries' (Greek—Historia; English Kingdom_of_England—(The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[4] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented 'history' as we know it.[4] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[4]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[5][6] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[6] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On The Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[7] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[8] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds that have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[9] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[9] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[10]

The Greek Ancient_Greece historian Diodorus Siculus Diodorus_Siculus from Sicily, writing in the 1st century BC in his Bibliotheca Historica Bibliotheca_historica, also provides an account of the Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars, partially derived from the earlier Greek Ancient_Greece historian Ephorus. This account is fairly consistent with Herodotus's.[11] The Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars are also described in less detail by a number of other ancient historians including Plutarch, Ctesias, and are alluded by other authors, such as the playwright Theatre Aeschylus. Archaeological evidence, such as the Serpent Column Serpent_Column, also supports some of Herodotus's specific claims.[12]

The Greek Ancient_Greece city-states of Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria had supported the unsuccessful Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt against the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Darius I Darius_I in 499–494 BC. Darius I Darius_I therefore began raising a huge new army with which he meant to completely subjugate Greece Ancient_Greece; however, in 486 BC, his Egyptian Ancient_Egypt subjects revolted, indefinitely postponing any Greek Ancient_Greece expedition.[14] Darius I Darius_I then died while preparing to march on Egypt Ptolemaic_Kingdom, and the throne of Persia Achaemenid_Empire passed to his son Xerxes I Xerxes_I.[22] Xerxes I Xerxes_I crushed the Egyptian revolt Ptolemy_V_Epiphanes, and very quickly restarted the preparations for the invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece.[23]

The numbers of troops that Xerxes I Xerxes_I mustered for the second invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece have been the subject of endless dispute, because the numbers given in ancient sources are very large indeed.
