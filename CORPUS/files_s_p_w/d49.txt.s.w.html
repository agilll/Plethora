The Delian League Delian_League, founded in 478 BC,[1] was an association of Greek Ancient_Greece city-states, with the amount of members numbering between 150 to 330[2][3][4]under the leadership of Athens History_of_Athens, whose purpose was to continue fighting the Persian Empire Roman_Empire after the Greek Ancient_Greece victory in the Battle of Plataea Battle_of_Plataea at the end of the Second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece. The League's modern[5] name derives from its official meeting place, the island of Delos, where congresses were held in the temple Ancient_Greek_temple and where the treasury stood until, in a symbolic gesture,[6] Pericles moved it to Athens History_of_Athens in 454 BC.[7]

Shortly after its inception, Athens History_of_Athens began to use the League's navy for its own purposes – which led to its naming by historians as the Athenian Empire Roman_Empire. This behavior frequently led to conflict between Athens History_of_Athens and the less powerful members of the League. By 431 BC, Athens History_of_Athens' heavy-handed control of the Delian League Delian_League prompted the outbreak of the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War; the League was dissolved upon the war's conclusion in 404 BC under the direction of Lysander, the Spartan commander.

The loose alliance of city City states which had fought against Xerxes I Xerxes_I's invasion had been dominated by Sparta and the Peloponnesian league Peloponnesian_League. With the withdrawal of these states, a congress was called on the holy island Lindisfarne of Delos to institute a new alliance to continue the fight against the Persians Achaemenid_Empire; hence the modern designation "Delian League Delian_League". According to Thucydides, the official aim of the League was to "avenge the wrongs they suffered by ravaging the territory of the king."[27]

In reality, this goal was divided into three main efforts—to prepare for future invasion, to seek revenge against Persia Achaemenid_Empire, and to organize a means of dividing spoils of war. The members The_Members were given a choice of either offering armed forces or paying a tax to the joint treasury; most states chose the tax.[27] League members swore to have the same friends and enemies, and dropped ingots of iron into the sea to symbolize the permanence of their alliance. The Athenian Delian_League politician Aristides would spend the rest of his life occupied in the affairs of the alliance, dying (according to Plutarch) a few years later in Pontus Kingdom_of_Pontus, whilst determining what the tax of new members was to be.[28]

In the first ten years of the league's existence, Cimon/Kimon forced Karystos in Euboea to join the league, conquered the island of Skyros and sent Athenian Delian_League colonists there.[29]

Over time, especially with the suppression of rebellions, Athens History_of_Athens exercised hegemony Hegemony over the rest of the league. Thucydides describes how Athens History_of_Athens's control over the League grew:

In some other respects the Athenians were not the old popular rulers they had been at first; and if they had more than their fair share of service, it was correspondingly easy for them to reduce any that tried to leave the confederacy.
