The Peace of Callias Callias_II is a purported treaty established around 449 BC between the Delian League Delian_League (led by Athens Classical_Athens) and Persia Achaemenid_Empire, ending the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. The peace was agreed as the first compromise treaty between Achaemenid Persia Achaemenid_Empire and a Greek Ancient_Greece city City.

The peace was negotiated by Callias Callias_II, an Athenian Delian_League politician. Persia Achaemenid_Empire had continually lost territory to the Greeks Ancient_Greece after the end of Xerxes I Xerxes_I's invasion in 479 BC. The exact date of the treaty is debated, although it is usually placed after the Battle of the Eurymedon Giants_(Greek_mythology) in 469 or 466 or the Battle of Cypriot Cyprus Salamis Wars_of_the_Delian_League c. 449. The Peace of Callias Callias_II gave autonomy to the Ionian Ionia states in Asia Minor Anatolia, prohibited the encroachment of Persian Achaemenid_Empire satrapies Satrap within three days march of the Aegean Aegean_Sea coast, and prohibited Persian Achaemenid_Empire ships from the Aegean Aegean_Sea. Athens Classical_Athens also agreed not to interfere with Persia Achaemenid_Empire's possessions in Asia Minor Anatolia, Cyprus, Libya Ancient_Libya or Egypt Ptolemaic_Kingdom [1](Athens Classical_Athens had recently lost a fleet aiding an Egyptian revolt Ptolemy_V_Epiphanes against Persia Achaemenid_Empire[2]).

Our knowledge of the Peace of Callias Callias_II comes from references by the 4th century BC orators Isocrates and Demosthenes as well as the historian Diodorus.[3] The ancient historian Ancient_history Theopompus deemed it a fabrication arguing that the inscription of the treaty was a fake – the lettering used hadn't come into practice until half a century after the treaty was purported to have been agreed. It is possible that the treaty never officially existed, and if it was concluded, its importance is disputed. Thucydides did not mention it, but Herodotus[4] says something that may reasonably be construed as supporting its existence, as does Plutarch, who thought it had either been signed after the Battle of the Eurymedon Giants_(Greek_mythology) in 466 BC, or that it had never been signed at all. In any case, there seems to have been some agreement reached ending hostilities with Persia Achaemenid_Empire after 450/449, which allowed Athens Classical_Athens to deal with the new threats from the other Greek Ancient_Greece states such as Corinth and Thebes Metropolis_of_Thebes_and_Livadeia, as well as Euboea which rebelled from the Delian League Delian_League shortly after this. These conflicts may have arisen when Athenian Delian_League 'allies' felt there was no longer a justification for the Delian League Delian_League (which had developed from the Spartan-led Hellenic League Greco-Persian_Wars that defeated Xerxes I Xerxes_I' invasion), as Persia Achaemenid_Empire was apparently no longer a threat. As Athens Classical_Athens demanded more and more tribute and exerted more political and economic control over its allies, the League became more of a true empire, and many of Athens Classical_Athens' 'allies' began to rebel.
