Harpagus, also known as Harpagos or Hypargus (Ancient Greek Ancient_Greece Ἅρπαγος; Akkadian Akkadian_language: Arbaku), was a Median Medes general from the 6th century BC, credited by Herodotus as having put Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great the Great on the throne through his defection during the battle Battle of Pasargadae.

According to Herodotus' Histories Herodotus, Harpagus was a member of the Median Medes royal house in service to King Astyages, the last king of Media.

When word reached Astyages that Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great was gathering his forces, he ordered Harpagus, as his primary general, to lead the army against Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great. After a three-day battle Battle on the plain Plain of Pasargadae, Harpagus took his revenge for the death of his son when he turned on the battlefield in favor of Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great, resulting in Astyages' defeat and the formation of the Persian Empire.

Herodotus accounts for the turn of Harpagus' support to a version of the cannibal Cannibalism feast of Thyestes.[1] He reports that Astyages, after having a dream that his daughter, Mandane, would give birth to a king who would overthrow him, ordered Harpagus to expose the child at birth. Harpagus, reluctant to spill his own royal blood Food_and_drink_prohibitions, gave the child (Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great) to a shepherd named Mitradates,[2] who raised him as his own son.

Ten years later, when Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great was discovered alive, Astyages cruelly punished Harpagus by killing Harpagus' only son and feeding him to the courtier Courtier during a banquet. It is said that Harpagus did not react during the banquet, other than to gather the pieces of his son and remove them for burial. Astyages then asked his Magi (priests) for their advice about the fate of Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great. They told him that the boy, who had been discovered while playing king of the mountain Mountain_gun with his friends, had fulfilled the prophecy of becoming a king, albeit in play, and was no longer a danger. On their advice, Astyages sent Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great to his parents, Cambyses I Cambyses_I and Mandane, in Anšan Anshan_(Persia) (southwestern Iran History_of_Iran near Shiraz).

Harpagus bided his time, sending gifts to Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great to keep contact with him, as he worked to turn the nobles of Media against Astyages. When they were ready, he sent a message to Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great, hidden in the belly of a hare, informing him that the Medians Medes would mutiny Mutiny on the field, should he take arms against his grandfather.

After the defeat of Astyages (550 BC) Harpagus started - according to Herodotus - a military career under the new ruler Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great:

Harpagus suggested using camels as the front line against the Lydians Lydia in Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great's war against Croesus, thereby scattering Scattering the Lydian Lydia cavalry Cavalry (the horses panicked at the smell of the dromedaries Dromedary).
Following a revolt by the Lydians Lydia and the death of Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great's infantry commander, General General_officer Mazares, Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great turned over the conquest of Asia Minor Anatolia to Harpagus, who went on to serve as Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great's most successful general.
The Median Medes general followed his victory at Lydia by conquering Ionia, Phoenicia, Caria, Lycia and many other regions of Asia Minor Anatolia (except Miletus, which had earned the favor of Cyrus II Cyrus_the_Great through their great sage Rishi Thales's advice to stay neutral in the Lydian Lydia war).
Harpagus was also known for innovations in engineering techniques, specifically, the use of earthwork Fortification ramps and mounds during sieges (a method later employed by Alexander Alexander_the_Great during his siege Siege of Tyre Tire) and for the use of mountain Mountain_gun climbers to scale opponents' walls.
Despite Harpagus' reputation for mercy, the residents of Xanthos in Lycia committed suicide Seppuku rather than surrender to him, saying that they had never been conquered.
Later life[edit]
