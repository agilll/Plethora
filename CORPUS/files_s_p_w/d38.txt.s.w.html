The first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, during the Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, began in 492 BC, and ended with the decisive Athenian History_of_Athens victory at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon in 490 BC. The invasion, consisting of two distinct campaigns, was ordered by the Persian Achaemenid_Empire king Darius I Darius_I primarily in order to punish the city-states of Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria. These cities had supported the cities of Ionia during their revolt against Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule, thus incurring the wrath of Darius I Darius_I. Darius I Darius_I also saw the opportunity to extend his empire into Europe, and to secure its western frontier Frontier.

The first campaign in 492 BC, led by Mardonius, re-subjugated Thrace and forced Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia to become a fully subordinate client kingdom Client_state part of Persia Achaemenid_Empire, after being a vassal Vassal to Persia Achaemenid_Empire as early as the late 6th century BC.[1] However, further progress was prevented when Mardonius's fleet was wrecked in a storm off the coast of Mount Athos Mount_Athos. The following year, having demonstrated his intentions, Darius I Darius_I sent ambassadors to all parts of Greece Ancient_Greece, demanding their submission. He received it from almost all of them, except Athens History_of_Athens and Sparta, both of whom executed the ambassadors. With Athens History_of_Athens still defiant, and Sparta now effectively at war with him, Darius I Darius_I ordered a further military campaign for the following year.

The second Persian Achaemenid_Empire campaign, in 490 BC, was under the command of Datis and Artaphernes. The expedition headed first to the island Naxos, which it captured and burned. It then island-hopped Island_hopping between the rest of the Cycladic Islands Cyclades, annexing each into the Persian Achaemenid_Empire empire. Reaching Greece Ancient_Greece, the expedition landed at Eretria, which it besieged, and after a brief time, captured. Eretria was razed and its citizens enslaved. Finally, the task force headed to Attica, landing at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, en route for Athens History_of_Athens. There, it was met by a smaller Athenian History_of_Athens army, which nevertheless proceeded to win a remarkable victory at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon.

This defeat prevented the successful conclusion of the campaign, and the task force returned to Asia Anatolia. Nevertheless, the expedition had fulfilled most of its aims, punishing Naxos and Eretria, and bringing much of the Aegean Aegean_Sea under Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule, as well as the full inclusion of Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia. The unfinished business from this campaign led Darius I Darius_I to prepare for a much larger invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, to firmly subjugate it, and to punish Athens History_of_Athens and Sparta. However, internal strife within the empire delayed this expedition, and Darius I Darius_I then died of old age. It was thus left to his son Xerxes I Xerxes_I to lead the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, beginning in 480 BC.

The main source Main_Source for the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[2] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his 'Enquiries' (Greek—Historia; English Kingdom_of_England—(The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[3] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented 'history' as we know it.[3] As the British author Tom Holland Tom_Holland_(actor) has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[3]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[4][5] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[5] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On The Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[6] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[7] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds which have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[8] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[8] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[9]

The Sicilian The_Sicilian historian Diodorus Siculus Diodorus_Siculus, writing in the 1st century BC in his Bibliotheca Historica Bibliotheca_historica, also provides an account of the Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars, partially derived from the earlier Greek Ancient_Greece historian Ephorus. This account is fairly consistent with Herodotus's.[10] The Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars are also described in less detail by a number of other ancient historians including Plutarch, Ctesias of Cnidus Knidos, and are alluded by other authors, such as the playwright Playwright Aeschylus. Archaeological evidence, such as the Serpent Column Serpent_Column, also supports some of Herodotus's specific claims.[11]

The first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece had its immediate roots in the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, the earliest phase of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. However, it was also the result of the longer-term interaction between the Greeks Ancient_Greece and Persians Achaemenid_Empire. In 500 BC the Persian Empire Roman_Empire was still relatively young and highly expansionist, but prone to revolts amongst its subject peoples.[12][13][14] Moreover, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire king Darius I Darius_I was a usurper, and had spent considerable time extinguishing revolts against his rule.[12] Even before the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, Darius I Darius_I had begun to expand the Empire Roman_Empire into Europe, subjugating Thrace, expanding past the Danube river, conquering Paeonia Peony,[15] and forcing Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia to become a client kingdom Client_state to Persia Achaemenid_Empire; though the latter retaining an amount of autonomy up to 492 BC.[16] Attempts at further expansion into the politically fractious world of Ancient Greece Ancient_Greece may have been inevitable.[13][17] However, the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt had directly threatened the integrity of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire empire, and the states of mainland Greece Ancient_Greece remained a potential menace to its future stability.[18] Darius I Darius_I thus resolved to subjugate and pacify Greece Ancient_Greece and the Aegean Aegean_Sea, and to punish those involved in the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt.[18][19]

The Ionian revolt Ionian_Revolt had begun with an unsuccessful expedition against Naxos, a joint venture between the Persian Achaemenid_Empire satrap Satrap Artaphernes and the Miletus tyrant Tyrant Aristagoras.[20] In the aftermath, Artaphernes decided to remove Aristagoras from power, but before he could do so, Aristagoras abdicated, and declared Miletus a democracy Athenian_democracy.[20] The other Ionian Ionia cities, ripe for rebellion, followed suit, ejecting their Persian-appointed tyrants, and declaring themselves democracies.[20][21] Aristagoras then appealed to the states of Mainland Greece Ancient_Greece for support, but only Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria offered to send troops.[22]

The involvement of Athens History_of_Athens in the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt arose from a complex set of circumstances, beginning with the establishment of the Athenian Democracy Athenian_democracy in the late 6th century BC.[22] In 510 BC, with the aid of Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I, King of Sparta, the Athenian History_of_Athens people had expelled Hippias, the tyrant Tyrant ruler of Athens History_of_Athens.[23] With Hippias's father Peisistratus Peisistratos, the family had ruled for 36 out of the previous 50 years and fully intended to continue Hippias's rule.[23] Hippias fled to Sardis to the court of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire satrap Satrap, Artaphernes, and promised control of Athens History_of_Athens to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire if they were to help restore him.[24] In the meantime, Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I helped install a pro-Spartan tyranny under Isagoras in Athens History_of_Athens, in opposition to Cleisthenes, the leader of the traditionally powerful Alcmaeonidae family, who considered themselves the natural heirs to the rule of Athens History_of_Athens.[25] In a daring response, Cleisthenes proposed to the Athenian History_of_Athens people that he would establish a 'democracy Athenian_democracy' in Athens History_of_Athens, much to the horror Horror_fiction of the rest of the aristocracy. Cleisthenes's reasons for suggesting such a radical course of action, which would remove much of his own family's power, are unclear; perhaps he perceived that days of aristocratic rule were coming to an end anyway; certainly he wished to prevent Athens History_of_Athens becoming a puppet of Sparta by whatever means necessary.[25] However, as a result of this proposal, Cleisthenes and his family were exiled from Athens History_of_Athens, in addition to other dissenting elements, by Isagoras. Having been promised democracy Athenian_democracy however, the Athenian History_of_Athens people seized the moment and revolted, expelling Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I and Isagoras.[26] Cleisthenes was thus restored to Athens History_of_Athens (507 BC), and at breakneck speed began to establish democratic government. The establishment of democracy Athenian_democracy revolutionised Athens History_of_Athens, which henceforth became one of the leading cities in Greece Ancient_Greece.[26] The new-found freedom and self-governance of the Athenians meant that they were thereafter exceptionally hostile to the return of the tyranny of Hippias, or any form of outside subjugation; by Sparta, Persia Achaemenid_Empire or anyone else.[26]

Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I, unsurprisingly, was not pleased with events, and marched on Athens History_of_Athens with the Spartan army Spartan_army.[27] Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I's attempts to restore Isagoras to Athens History_of_Athens ended in a debacle, but fearing the worst, the Athenians had by this point already sent an embassy to Artaphernes in Sardis, to request aid from the Persian Empire Roman_Empire.[28] Artaphernes requested that the Athenians give him a 'earth and water', a traditional token of submission, which the Athenian History_of_Athens ambassadors acquiesced to.[28] However, they were severely censured for this when they returned to Athens History_of_Athens.[28] At some point later Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I instigated a plot to restore Hippias to the rule of Athens History_of_Athens. This failed and Hippias again fled to Sardis and tried to persuade the Persians Achaemenid_Empire to subjugate Athens History_of_Athens.[29] The Athenians dispatched ambassadors to Artaphernes to dissuade him from taking action, but Artaphernes merely instructed the Athenians to take Hippias back as tyrant Tyrant.[22] Needless to say, the Athenians balked at this, and resolved instead to be openly at war with Persia Achaemenid_Empire.[29] Having thus become the enemy of Persia Achaemenid_Empire, Athens History_of_Athens was already in a position to support the Ionian Ionia cities when they began their revolt.[22] The fact that the Ionian Ionia democracies were inspired by the example of Athens History_of_Athens no doubt further persuaded the Athenians to support the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt; especially since the cities of Ionia were (supposedly) originally Athenian History_of_Athens colonies.[22]

The city City of Eretria also sent assistance to the Ionians for reasons that are not completely clear. Possibly commercial reasons were a factor; Eretria was a mercantile city City, whose trade was threatened by Persian Achaemenid_Empire dominance of the Aegean Aegean_Sea.[22] Herodotus suggests that the Eretrians supported the revolt in order to repay the support the Milesians Miletus had given Eretria in a past war against Chalcis.[30]

The Athenians and Eretrians sent a task force of 25 triremes to Asia Minor Anatolia.[31] Whilst there, the Greek Ancient_Greece army surprised and outmaneuvered Artaphernes, marching to Sardis and there burning the lower city City.[32] However, this was as much as the Greeks Ancient_Greece achieved, and they were then pursued back to the coast by Persian Achaemenid_Empire horsemen, losing many men in the process. Despite the fact their actions were ultimately fruitless, the Eretrians and in particular the Athenians had earned Darius I Darius_I's lasting enmity, and he vowed to punish both cities.[33] The Persian Achaemenid_Empire naval victory at the Battle of Lade Miletus (494 BC) all but ended the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, and by 493 BC, the last hold-outs were vanquished by the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet.[34] The revolt was used as an opportunity by Darius I Darius_I to extend the empire's border to the islands of the East Aegean Aegean_Sea[35] and the Propontis Sea_of_Marmara, which had not been part of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire dominions before.[36] The completion of the pacification of Ionia allowed the Persians Achaemenid_Empire to begin planning their next moves; to extinguish the threat to the empire from Greece Ancient_Greece, and to punish Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria.[37]

In the spring of 492 BC an expeditionary force, to be commanded by Darius I Darius_I's son-in-law Mardonius, was assembled, consisting of a fleet and a land army.[38] Whilst the ultimate aim was to punish Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria, the expedition also aimed to subdue as many of the Greek Ancient_Greece cities as possible.[38][39] Departing from Cilicia, Mardonius sent the army to march to the Hellespont Dardanelles, whilst he travelled with the fleet.[38] He sailed round the coast of Asia Minor Anatolia to Ionia, where he spent a short time Short_Time abolishing the tyrannies that ruled the cities of Ionia. Ironically, since the establishment of democracies had been a key factor in the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, he replaced the tyrannies with democracies.[38]

Thence the fleet continued on to the Hellespont Dardanelles, and when all was ready, shipped the land forces across to Europe.[38] The army then marched through Thrace, re-subjugating it, since these lands had already been added to the Persian Empire Roman_Empire in 512 BC, during Darius I Darius_I's campaign against the Scythians.[40] Upon reaching Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire forced it to become a fully subordinate part of the Persian Empire Roman_Empire; they had been vassals of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire since the late 6th century BC, but retained their general autonomy.[16]

Meanwhile, the fleet crossed to Thasos, resulting in the Thasians submitting to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[39] The fleet then rounded the coastline as far as Acanthus Acanthus_(plant) in Chalcidice Chalkidiki, before attempting to round the headland Headlands_and_bays of Mount Athos Mount_Athos.[39] However, they were caught in a violent storm, which drove them against the coastline of Athos Mount_Athos, wrecking (according to Herodotus) 300 ships, with the loss of 20,000 men.[39]

Then, whilst the army was camped in Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia, the Brygians Bryges, a local Thracian Thrace tribe, launched a night raid against the Persian Achaemenid_Empire camp, killing many of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, and wounding Mardonius.[41] Despite his injury, Mardonius made sure that the Brygians Bryges were defeated and subjugated, before leading his army back to the Hellespont Dardanelles; the remnants of the navy also retreated to Asia Anatolia.[41] Although this campaign ended ingloriously, the land approaches to Greece Ancient_Greece had been secured, and the Greeks Ancient_Greece had no doubt been made aware of Darius I Darius_I's intentions for them.[42]

Perhaps reasoning that the expedition of the previous year may have made his plans for Greece Ancient_Greece obvious, and weakened the resolve of the Greek Ancient_Greece cities, Darius I Darius_I turned to diplomacy in 491 BC. He sent ambassadors to all the Greek Ancient_Greece city City states, asking for "earth and water", a traditional token of submission.[43] The vast majority of cities did as asked, fearing the wrath of Darius I Darius_I. In Athens History_of_Athens, however, the ambassadors were put on trial and then executed; in Sparta, they were simply thrown down a well.[42] This firmly and finally drew the battle-lines for the coming conflict; Sparta and Athens History_of_Athens, despite their recent enmity, would together fight the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[42]

However, Sparta was then thrown into disarray by internal machinations Machinations_(band). The citizens of Aegina had submitted to the Persian Achaemenid_Empire ambassadors, and the Athenians, troubled by the possibility of Persia Achaemenid_Empire using Aegina as a naval base Military_base, asked Sparta to intervene.[44] Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I travelled to Aegina to confront the Aeginetans personally, but they appealed to Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I's fellow king Demaratus, who supported their stance.[45] Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I responded by having Demaratus declared illegitimate, with the help of the priests at Delphi (whom he bribed); Demaratus was replaced by his cousin Leotychides Leotychidas.[44] Now faced with two Spartan kings List_of_kings_of_Sparta, the Aeginetans capitulated, and handed over hostages to the Athenians as a guarantee of their good behaviour Good_Behaviour.[46] However, in Sparta news emerged of the bribes Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I had given at Delphi, and he was expelled from the city City.[47] He then sought to rally Protest the northern Peloponnesus Peloponnese to his cause, at which the Spartans Spartan_army relented, and invited him back to the city City.[44] By 491 BC though, Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I was widely considered insane and was sentenced to prison where he was found dead the following day.[44] Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I was succeeded by his half-brother Leonidas Leonidas_I.[44]

Taking advantage of the chaos in Sparta, which effectively left Athens History_of_Athens isolated, Darius I Darius_I decided to launch an amphibious expedition to finally punish Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria.[48] An army was assembled in Susa, and marched into Cilicia, where a fleet had been gathered.[48] Command of the expedition was given to Datis the Mede and Artaphernes, son of the satrap Satrap Artaphernes.

According to Herodotus, the fleet sent by Darius I Darius_I consisted of 600 triremes.[49] There is no indication in the historical sources of how many transport ships accompanied them, if any. Herodotus claimed that 3,000 transport ships accompanied 1,207 triremes during Xerxes I Xerxes_I's invasion in 480 BC.[50] Among modern historians, some have accepted this number of ships as reasonable; it has been suggested either that the number 600 represents the combined number of triremes and transport ships,[51][52] or that there were horse transports Horse_transports_in_the_Middle_Ages in addition to 600 triremes.[53]

Herodotus does not estimate the size of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army, only saying that they formed a "large infantry that was well packed".[54] Among other ancient sources, the poet Poetry Simonides Simonides_of_Ceos, a near-contemporary, says the campaign force numbered 200,000, while a later writer, the Roman Cornelius Nepos Cornelius_Nepos estimates 200,000 infantry and 10,000 cavalry Cavalry.[55] Plutarch and Pausanias Pausanias_of_Sparta both independently give 300,000, as does the Suda dictionary;[56][57][58] Plato and Lysias assert 500,000; and Justin 600,000.[59][60][61]

Modern historians generally dismiss these numbers as exaggerations.[53] One approach to estimate the number of troops is to calculate the number of marines carried by 600 triremes. Herodotus tells us that each trireme Trireme in the second invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece carried 30 extra marines, in addition to a probable 14 standard marines.[62] Thus, 600 triremes could easily have carried 18,000–26,000 infantry.[53][63] Numbers proposed for the Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry are in the range 18,000–100,000.[51][52][64][65][66] However, the consensus is around 25,000.[53][65]

The Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry used in the invasion was probably a heterogeneous group drawn from across the empire. However, according to Herodotus, there was at least a general conformity in the type of armour and style of fighting.[67] The troops were, generally speaking, armed with a bow Bow_and_arrow, 'short spear' and sword Sword, carried a wicker shield, and wore at most a leather jerkin.[67][68] The one exception to this may have been the ethnic Persian Achaemenid_Empire troops, who may have worn a corslet Corslet of scale armour Scale_armour.[67] Some contingents would have been armed somewhat differently;[67] for instance, the Saka were renowned axemen.[69] The 'elite' contingents of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry seem to have been the ethnic Persians Achaemenid_Empire, Medians Medes, Cissians Khuzestan_Province and the Saka;[67] Herodotus specifically mentions the presence of Persians Achaemenid_Empire and Saka at Marathon Battle_of_Marathon.[70] The style of fighting used by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire was probably to stand off from an enemy, using their bows (or equivalent) to wear down the enemy before closing in to deliver the coup de grace Coup_de_grâce with spear and sword Sword.[67]

Estimates for the cavalry Cavalry are usually in the 1,000–3,000 range.[53][71] The Persian Achaemenid_Empire cavalry Cavalry was usually provided by the ethnic Persians Achaemenid_Empire, Bactrians Bactria, Medes, Cissians Khuzestan_Province, and Saka; most of these probably fought as lightly armed missile Missile cavalry Cavalry.[67][72] The fleet must have had at least some proportion of transport ships, since the cavalry Cavalry was carried by ship; whilst Herodotus claims the cavalry Cavalry was carried in the triremes, this is improbable. Lazenby estimates 30–40 transport ships would be required to carry 1,000 cavalry Cavalry.[53]
