The early history of Iran History_of_Iran may be divided into three phases: (1) the prehistoric Prehistory period, beginning with the earliest evidence of humans on the Iranian plateau Iranian_Plateau (c. 100,000 BC An_Unearthly_Child) and ending roughly at the start of the 1st millennium BC, (2) the protohistoric Protohistory period, covering approximately the first half of the 1st millennium BC, and (3) the period of the Achaemenian Achaemenid_Empire dynasty (6th to 4th century BC), when Iran History_of_Iran entered the full light of written history. The civilization of Elam, centred off the plateau Plateau in lowland Khūzestān, is an exception, for written history began there as early as it did in neighbouring Mesopotamia (c. 3000 BC).

The sources for the prehistoric Prehistory period are entirely archaeological. Early excavation in Iran History_of_Iran was limited to a few sites. In the 1930s archaeological exploration increased, but work was abruptly halted by the outbreak of World War II World_War_II. After the war ended, interest in Iranian archaeology revived quickly, and, from 1950 until archaeological study was dramatically curtailed after 1979, numerous excavations revolutionized the study of prehistoric Prehistory Iran History_of_Iran.

For the protohistoric Protohistory period the historian is still forced to rely primarily on archaeological evidence, but much information comes from written sources as well. Whereas the Iranian plateau Iranian_Plateau did not experience the rise of urban, literate civilization in the late 4th and early 3rd millennia on the Mesopotamian Mesopotamia pattern, lowland Khūzestān did.
