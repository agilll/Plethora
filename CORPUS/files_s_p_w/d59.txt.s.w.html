Themistocles (/θəˈmɪstəˌkliːz/; Greek Ancient_Greece: Θεμιστοκλῆς Greek Ancient_Greece pronunciation: [tʰemistoklɛ̂ːs] Themistoklẽs; "Glory Perth_Glory_FC of the Law";[1] c. 524–459 BC 459_BC) was an Athenian Classical_Athens politician and general. He was one of a new breed Dog_breed of non-aristocratic politicians who rose to prominence in the early years of the Athenian democracy Athenian_democracy. As a politician, Themistocles was a populist Populism, having the support of lower-class Athenians, and generally being at odds with the Athenian Classical_Athens nobility. Elected archon Archon in 493 BC, he convinced the polis to increase the naval power of Athens Classical_Athens, a recurring theme in his political career. During the first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece he fought at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon[2] (490 BC) and was possibly one of the ten Athenian Classical_Athens strategoi Strategos (generals) in that battle Battle.[citation needed]

In the years after Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, and in the run-up to the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of 480-479 BC, Themistocles became the most prominent politician in Athens Classical_Athens. He continued to advocate a strong Athenian Classical_Athens Navy, and in 483 BC he persuaded the Athenians to build a fleet of 200 triremes; these proved crucial in the forthcoming conflict with Persia Achaemenid_Empire. During the second invasion, he effectively exercised command of the Greek Ancient_Greece allied navy at the battles of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium and Salamis Battle_of_Salamis in 480 BC. Due to subterfuge on the part of Themistocles, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I lured the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet into the Straits of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, and the decisive Greek Ancient_Greece victory there was the turning point in the invasion, which ended the following year with the defeat of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire at the land Battle of Plataea.

After the conflict ended, Themistocles continued his pre-eminence among Athenian Classical_Athens politicians. However, he aroused the hostility of Sparta by ordering the re-fortification of Athens Classical_Athens, and his perceived arrogance began to alienate him from the Athenians. In 472 or 471 BC, he was ostracised, and went into exile in Argos. The Spartans Spartan_army now saw an opportunity to destroy Themistocles, and implicated him in the alleged treasonous plot of 478 BC of their own general Pausanias Pausanias_of_Sparta. Themistocles thus fled from Greece Ancient_Greece. Alexander Alexander_the_Great I of Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia (reigned 498-454 BC) temporarily gave him sanctuary at Pydna before he traveled to Asia Minor Anatolia, where he entered the service of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire king Artaxerxes I Artaxerxes_I_of_Persia (reigned 465-424 BC). He was made governor of Magnesia Magnesia_on_the_Maeander, and lived there for the rest of his life.

Themistocles died in 459 BC 459_BC, probably of natural causes. Themistocles's reputation was posthumously rehabilitated, and he was re-established as a hero of the Athenian Classical_Athens (and indeed Greek Ancient_Greece) cause. Themistocles can still reasonably be thought of as "the man most instrumental in achieving the salvation Salvation of Greece Ancient_Greece" from the Persian Achaemenid_Empire threat, as Plutarch describes him. His naval policies would have a lasting impact on Athens Classical_Athens as well, since maritime power became the cornerstone of the Athenian Empire Roman_Empire and golden age. Thucydides assessed Themistocles as "a man who exhibited the most indubitable signs of genius; indeed, in this particular he has a claim on our admiration quite extraordinary and unparalleled".[citation needed]

Themistocles was born in the Attic Classical_Athens deme Deme of Phrearrhioi around 524 BC,[3] the son of Neocles, who was, in the words of Plutarch "no very conspicuous man".[4] His mother is more obscure; according to Plutarch, she was either a Thracian Thrace woman called Abrotonon, or Euterpe, a Carian Carians from Halicarnassus.[4] Like many contemporaries, little is known of his early years. Some authors report that he was unruly as a child and was consequently disowned by his father.[5][6] Plutarch considers this to be false.[7] Plutarch indicates that, on account of his mother's background, Themistocles was considered something of an outsider; furthermore the family appear to have lived in an immigrant district of Athens Classical_Athens, Cynosarges, outside the city City walls.[4] However, in an early example of his cunning, Themistocles persuaded "well-born" children to exercise with him in Cynosarges, thus breaking down the distinction between "alien and legitimate".[4] Plutarch further reports that Themistocles was preoccupied, even as a child, with preparing for public life.[7] His teacher is said to have told him:[7]

"My boy, you will be nothing insignificant, but definitely something great, either for good or evil."

Themistocles left three sons by Archippe, daughter to Lysander of Alopece,[8]—Archeptolis, Polyeuctus, and Cleophantus. Plato the philosopher Philosopher mentions Cleophantus as a most excellent horseman, but otherwise insignificant person. And Themistocles had two sons older than these three, Neocles and Diocles Diocletian. Neocles died when he was young by the bite of a horse Cavalry, and Diocles Diocletian was adopted by his grandfather, Lysander. Themistocles had many daughters, of whom Mnesiptolema, whom he had by a second marriage, was wife to Archeptolis, her brother by another mother, and became priestess of Cybele; Italia was married to Panthoides, of the island of Chios; Sybaris to Nicomedes Nicomedes_IV_of_Bithynia the Athenian Classical_Athens. After the death of Themistocles, his nephew, Phrasicles, went to Magnesia Magnesia_on_the_Maeander, and married, with her brothers' consent, another daughter, Nicomache, and took charge of her sister Asia Anatolia, the youngest of all ten children.

Themistocles grew up in a period of upheaval in Athens Classical_Athens. The tyrant Tyrant Peisistratos had died in 527 BC, passing power to his sons, Hipparchus and Hippias.[9] Hipparchus was murdered in 514 BC, and in response to this, Hippias became paranoid and started to rely increasingly on foreign mercenaries Mercenary to keep a hold on power.[10] The head of the powerful, but exiled (according to Herodotus only—the fragmentary Archon List for 525/4 shows a Cleisthenes, an Alcmaeonid Alcmaeonidae, holding office in Athens Classical_Athens during this period) Alcmaeonid Alcmaeonidae family, Cleisthenes, began to scheme to overthrow Hippias and return to Athens Classical_Athens.[11] In 510 BC, he persuaded the Spartan king Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I to launch a full-scale attack on Athens Classical_Athens, which succeeded in overthrowing Hippias.[11] However, in the aftermath, the other noble ('eupatrid Eupatridae') families of Athens Classical_Athens rejected Cleisthenes, electing Isagoras as archon Archon, with the support of Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I.[11] On a personal level, Cleisthenes wanted to return to Athens Classical_Athens; however, he also probably wanted to prevent Athens Classical_Athens becoming a Spartan client state. Outmaneuvering the other nobles, he proposed to the Athenian Classical_Athens people a radical program in which political power would be invested in the people—a "democracy Athenian_democracy".[11] The Athenian Classical_Athens people thus overthrew Isagoras, repelled a Spartan attack under Cleomenes I Cleomenes_I, and invited Cleisthenes to return to Athens Classical_Athens, to put his plan into action.[12] The establishment of the democracy Athenian_democracy was to radically change Athens Classical_Athens:

"And so it was that the Athenians found themselves suddenly a great power...they gave vivid Vivid_(band) proof of what equality and freedom of speech might achieve"[13]

The new system of government in Athens Classical_Athens opened up a wealth of opportunity for men like Themistocles, who previously would have had no access to power.[14] Moreover, the new institutions of the democracy Athenian_democracy required skills that had previously been unimportant in government. Themistocles was to prove himself a master of the new system; "he could infight, he could network, he could spin Spin_bowling... and crucially, he knew how to make himself visible."[14] Themistocles moved to the Ceramicus Kerameikos, a down-market part of Athens Classical_Athens. This move marked him out as a 'man of the people', and allowed him to interact Interaction more easily with ordinary citizens. He began building up a support base among these newly empowered citizens:

"he wooed the poor; and they, not used to being courted, duly loved him back. Touring the taverns, the markets, the docks, canvassing where no politician had thought to canvas before, making sure never to forget a single voter's name, Themistocles had set his eyes on a radical new constituency"[14]

However, he took care to ensure that he did not alienate the nobility of Athens Classical_Athens.[14] He began to practice law, the first person in Athens Classical_Athens to prepare for public life in this way.[14] His ability as attorney and arbitrator, used in the service of the common people, gained him further popularity.[15]

Themistocles probably turned 30 in 494 BC, which qualified him to become an archon Archon, the highest of the magistracies Roman_magistrate in Athens Classical_Athens.[14] On the back of his popularity, he evidently decided to run for this office and was elected Archon Eponymous, the highest government office in the following year (493 BC).[14] Themistocles's archonship saw the beginnings of a major theme in his career; the advancement of Athenian Classical_Athens sea-power. Under his guidance, the Athenians began the building of a new port at Piraeus, to replace the existing facilities at Phalerum.[14] Although further away from Athens Classical_Athens, Piraeus offered three natural harbours, and could be easily fortified.[16] Since Athens Classical_Athens was to become an essentially maritime power during the 5th century BC, Themistocles's policies were to have huge significance for the future of Athens Classical_Athens, and indeed Greece Ancient_Greece. In advancing naval power, Themistocles was probably advocating a course of action he thought essential for the long-term prospects of Athens Classical_Athens.[14] However, as Plutarch implies, since naval power relied on the mass mobilisation of the common citizens (thetes Solonian_Constitution) as rowers, such a policy put more power into the hands of average Athenians—and thus into Themistocles's own hands.[16]

After Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, probably in 489, Miltiades, the hero of the battle Battle, was seriously wounded in an abortive attempt to capture Paros. Taking advantage of his incapacitation, the powerful Alcmaeonid Alcmaeonidae family arranged for him to be prosecuted.[17] The Athenian Classical_Athens aristocracy, and indeed Greek Ancient_Greece aristocrats in general, were loath to see one person pre-eminent, and such maneuvers were commonplace.[17] Miltiades was given a massive fine for the crime of 'deceiving the Athenian Classical_Athens people', but died weeks later as a result of his wound.[17] In the wake of this prosecution, the Athenian Classical_Athens people chose to use a new institution of the democracy Athenian_democracy, which had been part of Cleisthenes's reforms, but remained so far unused.[17] This was 'ostracism Ostracism'—each Athenian Classical_Athens citizen was required to write on a shard of pottery Ancient_Greek_art (ostrakon Ostracon) the name of a politician that they wished to see exiled for a period of ten years.[17] This may have been triggered by Miltiades's prosecution, and used by the Athenians to try and stop such power-games among the noble families.[17] Certainly, in the years (487 BC) following, the heads of the prominent families, including the Alcmaeonids Alcmaeonidae, were exiled.[17] The career of a politician in Athens Classical_Athens thus became fraught with more difficulty, since displeasing the population was likely to result in exile.[17]

Themistocles, with his power-base firmly established among the poor, moved naturally to fill the vacuum Power_vacuum left by Miltiades's death, and in that decade became the most influential politician in Athens Classical_Athens.[17] However, the support of the nobility began to coalesce around the man who would become Themistocles's great rival—Aristides.[18] Aristides cast himself as Themistocles's opposite—virtuous, honest and incorruptible—and his followers called him "the just".[18] Plutarch suggests that the rivalry between the two had begun when they competed over the love of a boy: "... they were rivals for the affection of the beautiful Stesilaus of Ceos Kea_(island), and were passionate beyond all moderation."[19]

During the decade, Themistocles continued to advocate the expansion of Athenian Classical_Athens naval power.[17] The Athenians were certainly aware throughout this period that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire interest in Greece Ancient_Greece had not ended; Darius Darius_I's son and successor, Xerxes I Xerxes_I, had continued the preparations for the invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece.[20] Themistocles seems to have realised that for the Greeks Ancient_Greece to survive the coming onslaught Onslaught_(comics) required a Greek navy Hellenic_Navy that could hope to face up to the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy, and he therefore attempted to persuade the Athenians to build such a fleet.[14][17] Aristides, as champion of the zeugites Zeugites (the upper, 'hoplite-class') vigorously opposed such a policy.[18]

In 483 BC, a massive new seam of silver was found in the Athenian Classical_Athens mines at Laurium.[21] Themistocles proposed that the silver should be used to build a new fleet of 200 triremes, while Aristides suggested it should instead be distributed among the Athenian Classical_Athens citizens.[22] Themistocles avoided mentioning Persia Achaemenid_Empire, deeming that it was too distant a threat for the Athenians to act on, and instead focused their attention on Aegina.[21] At the time, Athens Classical_Athens was embroiled in a long-running war with the Aeginetans, and building a fleet would allow the Athenians to finally defeat them at sea.[21] As a result, Themistocles's motion was carried easily, although only 100 warships of the trireme Trireme type were to be built.[21] Aristides refused to countenance this; conversely Themistocles was not pleased that only 100 ships would be built.[22] Tension between the two camps built over the winter, so that the ostracism Ostracism of 482 BC became a direct contest between Themistocles and Aristides.[22] In what has been characterized as the first referendum Referendum, Aristides was ostracised, and Themistocles's policies were endorsed.[22] Indeed, becoming aware of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire preparations for the coming invasion, the Athenians voted for the construction of more ships than Themistocles had initially asked for.[22] In the run up to the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion, Themistocles had thus become the foremost politician in Athens Classical_Athens.[15]

In 481 BC, a congress of Greek Ancient_Greece city-states was held, during which 30 or so[citation needed] states agreed to ally themselves against the forthcoming invasion.[23] The Spartans Spartan_army and Athenians were foremost in this alliance, being sworn enemies of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[24] The Spartans Spartan_army claimed the command of land forces, and since the Greek Ancient_Greece (hereafter referred to as "Allied Allies_of_World_War_I") fleet would be dominated by Athens Classical_Athens, Themistocles tried to claim command of the naval forces.[25] However, the other naval powers, including Corinth and Aegina refused to give command to the Athenians, and Themistocles pragmatically backed down.[25] Instead, as a compromise, the Spartans Spartan_army (an insignificant naval power), in the person of Eurybiades were to command the naval forces.[26] It is clear from Herodotus, however, that Themistocles would be the real leader of the fleet.[27]

The 'congress' met again in the spring of 480 BC. A Thessalian Thessaly delegation suggested that the allies could muster in the narrow Vale of Tempe Vale_of_Tempe, on the borders of Thessaly, and thereby block Xerxes I Xerxes_I's advance.[28] A force of 10,000 hoplites Hoplite was dispatched under the command of the Spartan polemarch Polemarch Euenetus and Themistocles to the Vale of Tempe Vale_of_Tempe, which they believed the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army would have to pass through. Themistocles now developed a second strategy. The route to southern Greece Ancient_Greece (Boeotia, Attica and the Peloponnesus Peloponnese) would require the army of Xerxes I Xerxes_I to travel through the very narrow pass of Thermopylae.[30] This could easily be blocked by the Greek Ancient_Greece hoplites Hoplite, despite the overwhelming numbers of Persians Achaemenid_Empire; furthermore, to prevent the Persians Achaemenid_Empire bypassing Thermopylae by sea, the Athenian Classical_Athens and allied navies could block the straits of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium.[30] However, after the Tempe Vale_of_Tempe debacle, it was uncertain whether the Spartans Spartan_army would be willing to march out from the Peloponnesus Peloponnese again.[31] To persuade the Spartans Spartan_army to defend Attica, Themistocles had to show them that the Athenians were willing to do everything necessary for the success of the alliance. In short, the entire Athenian Classical_Athens fleet must be dispatched to Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium.

To do this, every able-bodied Athenian Classical_Athens male would be required to man the ships. This in turn meant that the Athenians must prepare to abandon Athens Classical_Athens.[31] Persuading the Athenians to take this course was undoubtedly one of the highlights of Themistocles's career.[32] As Holland has it:

"What precise heights of oratory Rhetoric he attained, what stirring and memorable phrases he pronounced, we have no way of knowing...only by the effect it had on the assembly can we gauge what surely must have been its electric and vivifying quality—for Themistocles' audacious Audacious-class_aircraft_carrier proposals, when put to the vote, were ratified. His proposals accepted, Themistocles issued orders for the women and children of Athens Classical_Athens to be sent to the city City of Troezen, safely inside the Peloponnesus Peloponnese.[33] He was then able to travel to a meeting of the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I, at which he proposed his strategy; with the Athenian Classical_Athens fleet fully committed Becky_Mode to the defence of Greece Ancient_Greece, the other Allies Allies_of_World_War_I accepted his proposals.[30]

In the aftermath of Thermopylae, Boeotia fell to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, who then began to advance on Athens Classical_Athens.[40] The Peloponnesian Allies Allies_of_World_War_I prepared to now defend the Isthmus of Corinth, thus abandoning Athens Classical_Athens to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[41] From Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium, the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I fleet sailed to the island of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, where the Athenian Classical_Athens ships helped with the final evacuation of Athens Classical_Athens. To bring about this battle Battle, Themistocles used a cunning mix of subterfuge and misinformation, psychologically exploiting Xerxes I Xerxes_I's desire to finish the invasion.[44] Xerxes I Xerxes_I's actions indicate that he was keen to finish the conquest of Greece Ancient_Greece in 480 BC, and to do this, he needed a decisive victory over the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I fleet.[45] Themistocles sent a servant, Sicinnus, to Xerxes I Xerxes_I, with a message proclaiming that Themistocles was "on king's side and prefers that your affairs prevail, not the Hellenes Ancient_Greece".[46] Themistocles claimed that the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I commanders were infighting, that the Peloponnesians were planning to evacuate that very night, and that to gain victory all the Persians Achaemenid_Empire needed to do was to block the straits.[46] In performing this subterfuge, Themistocles seems to have been trying to lure the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet into the Straits.[44] The message The_Message_(Grandmaster_Flash_and_the_Furious_Five_song) also had a secondary purpose, namely that in the event of an Allied Allies_of_World_War_I defeat, the Athenians would probably receive some degree of mercy from Xerxes I Xerxes_I (having indicated their readiness to submit).[44] At any rate, this was exactly the kind of news that Xerxes I Xerxes_I wanted to hear.[44] Xerxes I Xerxes_I evidently took the bait, and the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet was sent out to effect the block.[47] Perhaps overconfident and expecting no resistance, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire navy sailed into the Straits,[48] only to find that, far from disintegrating, the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I navy was ready for battle Battle.[49]

Salamis Battle_of_Salamis was the turning point in the second Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion, and indeed the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars in general.[55] While the battle Battle did not end the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion, it effectively ensured that all Greece Ancient_Greece would not be conquered, and allowed the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I to go on the offensive in 479 BC. A number of historians believe that Salamis Battle_of_Salamis is one of the most significant battles in human history.[56][57][58] Since Themistocles' long-standing advocacy of Athenian Classical_Athens naval power enabled the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I fleet to fight, and his stratagem brought about the Battle of Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, it is probably not an exaggeration to say, as Plutarch does, that Themistocles, "...is thought to have been the man most instrumental in achieving the salvation Salvation of Hellas."[21][35]

The Allied Allies_of_World_War_I victory at Salamis Battle_of_Salamis ended the immediate threat to Greece Ancient_Greece, and Xerxes I Xerxes_I now returned to Asia Anatolia with part of the army, leaving his general Mardonius to attempt to complete the conquest.[59] Mardonius wintered in Boeotia and Thessaly, and the Athenians were thus able to return to their city City, which had been burnt and razed by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, for the winter.[60] For the Athenians, and Themistocles personally, the winter would be a testing one. The Peloponnesians refused to countenance marching north of the Isthmus to fight the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army; the Athenians tried to shame them into doing so, with no success.[61]

During the winter, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I held a meeting at Corinth to celebrate their success, and award prizes for achievement.[62] However, perhaps tired of the Athenians pointing out their role at Salamis Battle_of_Salamis, and of their demands for the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I to march north, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I awarded the prize for civic achievement to Aegina.[61][63] Furthermore, although the admirals all voted for Themistocles in second place, they all voted for themselves in first place, so that no-one won the prize for individual achievement. "[when] Themistocles entered the stadium, the audience neglected the contestants all day long to gaze on him, and pointed him out with admiring applause to visiting strangers, so that he too was delighted, and confessed to his friends that he was now reaping in full measure the harvest of his toils in behalf of Hellas."[63]

"Themistocles once declared to the people [of Athens Classical_Athens] that he had devised a certain measure which could not be revealed to them, though it would be helpful and salutary for the city City, and they ordered that Aristides alone should hear what it was and pass judgment on it. However, as happened to many prominent individuals in the Athenian democracy Athenian_democracy, Themistocles's fellow citizens grew jealous of his success, and possibly tired of his boasting.[61][67] It is probable that in early 479 BC, Themistocles was stripped of his command; instead, Xanthippus was to command the Athenian Classical_Athens fleet, and Aristides the land forces.[61][68] Though Themistocles was no doubt politically and militarily active for the rest of the campaign, no mention of his activities in 479 BC is made in the ancient sources.[69] In the summer of that year, after receiving an Athenian Classical_Athens ultimatum, the Peloponnesians finally agreed to assemble an army and march to confront Mardonius, who had reoccupied Athens Classical_Athens in June.[70] At the decisive Battle of Plataea, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_I destroyed the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army, while apparently on the same day, the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I navy destroyed the remnants of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet at the Battle of Mycale Battle_of_Mycale.[71] These twin victories completed the Allied Allies_of_World_War_I triumph, and ended the Persian Achaemenid_Empire threat to Greece Ancient_Greece.[71]

Whatever the cause of Themistocles's unpopularity in 479 BC, it obviously did not last long. Both Diodorus and Plutarch suggest he was quickly restored to the favour of the Athenians.[16][72] Indeed, after 479 BC, he seems to have enjoyed a relatively long period of popularity.[73]

In the aftermath of the invasion, the Athenians began rebuilding their city City under the guidance of Themistocles.[16] They wished to restore the fortifications of Athens Classical_Athens, but the Spartans Spartan_army objected on the grounds that no place north of the Isthmus should be left that the Persians Achaemenid_Empire could use as a fortress.[72] Themistocles urged the citizens to build the fortifications as quickly as possible, then went to Sparta as an ambassador to answer the charges levelled by the Spartans Spartan_army. There, he assured them that no building work was on-going, and urged them to send emissaries to Athens Classical_Athens to see for themselves.[74] By the time the ambassadors arrived, the Athenians had finished building, and then detained the Spartan ambassadors when they complained about the presence of the fortifications.[74] By delaying in this manner, Themistocles gave the Athenians enough time to fortify the city City, and thus ward off any Spartan attack aimed at preventing the re-fortification of Athens Classical_Athens.[74] Furthermore, the Spartans Spartan_army were obliged to repatriate Themistocles in order to free their own ambassadors.[16][74] However, this episode may be seen as the beginning of the Spartan mistrust of Themistocles, which would return to haunt him.[16]

Themistocles also now returned to his naval policy,[16] and more ambitious undertakings that would increase the dominant position of his native state.[75] He further extended and fortified the port complex at Piraeus, and "fastened the city City [Athens Classical_Athens] to the Piraeus, and the land to the sea".[16] Themistocles probably aimed to make Athens Classical_Athens the dominant naval power in the Aegean Aegean_Sea.[75] Indeed, Athens Classical_Athens would create the Delian League Delian_League in 478 BC, uniting the naval power of the Aegean Islands Aegean_Islands and Ionia under Athenian Classical_Athens leadership.[76] Themistocles introduced tax breaks for merchants and artisans, to attract both people and trade to the city City to make Athens Classical_Athens a great mercantile centre.[77] He also instructed the Athenians to build 20 triremes per year, to ensure that their dominance in naval matters continued.[77] Plutarch reports that Themistocles also secretly proposed to destroy the beached ships of the other Allied Allies_of_World_War_I navies to ensure complete naval dominance—but was overruled Objection_(law) by Aristides and the council of Athens Classical_Athens.[78]

It is possible to draw some conclusions about Themistocles's character. Perhaps his most evident trait was his massive ambition; "In his ambition he surpassed all men";[15] "he hankered after public office rather as a man in delirium Delirium might crave a cure".[14] He was proud and vain,[25] and anxious for recognition of his deeds.[95] His relationship with power was of a particularly personal nature; while he undoubtedly desired the best for Athens Classical_Athens, many of his actions also seem to have been made in self-interest.[14] He also appears to have been corrupt (at least by modern standards), and was known for his fondness of bribes.[18]

Yet, set against these negative traits, was an apparently natural brilliance and talent for leadership:[14]

"Themistocles was a man who exhibited the most indubitable signs of genius; indeed, in this particular he has a claim on our admiration quite extraordinary and unparalleled. By his own native capacity Native_capacity, alike unformed and unsupplemented by study, he was at once the best judge in those sudden crises which admit of little or of no deliberation, and the best prophet Muhammad of the future, even to its most distant possibilities. An able theoretical expositor of all that came within the sphere Celestial_spheres of his practice, he was not without the power of passing an adequate judgment in matters in which he had no experience. He could also excellently divine the good and evil which lay hid in the unseen future. In fine, whether we consider the extent of his natural powers, or the slightness of his application, this extraordinary man must be allowed to have surpassed all others in the faculty of intuitively meeting an emergency."[90]
Both Herodotus and Plato record variations of an anecdote in which Themistocles responded with subtle sarcasm to an undistinguished man who complained that the great politician owed his fame merely to the fact that he came from Athens Classical_Athens. As Herodotus tells it:

"Timodemus of Aphidnae Afidnes, who was one of Themistocles' enemies but not a man of note, was crazed with envy and spoke bitterly to Themistocles of his visit to Lacedaemon Sparta, saying that the honors he had from the Lacedaemonians Sparta were paid him for Athens Classical_Athens' sake and not for his own. This he kept saying until Themistocles replied, 'This is the truth of the matter: if I had been a man of Belbina I would not have been honored in this way by the Spartans Spartan_army, nor would you, sir, for all you are a man of Athens Classical_Athens.' Such was the end of that business."[96]

Themistocles was undoubtedly intelligent, but also possessed natural cunning; "the workings of his mind [were] infinitely mobile and serpentine Snake".[14] Themistocles was evidently sociable and appears to have enjoyed strong personal loyalty from his friends.[14][82] At any rate, it seems to have been Themistocles's particular mix of virtues and vices that made him such an effective politician.[14]

Themistocles died with his reputation in tatters, a traitor to the Athenian Classical_Athens people; the "saviour of Greece Ancient_Greece" had turned into the enemy of liberty.[98] However, his reputation in Athens Classical_Athens was rehabilitated by Pericles in the 450s BC 450s_BC, and by the time Herodotus wrote his history, Themistocles was once again seen as a hero.[99] Thucydides evidently held Themistocles in some esteem, and is uncharacteristically flattering in his praise for him (see above).[90] Diodorus also extensively praises Themistocles, going as far as to offer a rationale for the length at which he discusses him: "Now on the subject of the high merits of Themistocles, even if we have dwelt over-long on the subject in this digression, we believed it not seemly that we should leave his great ability unrecorded."[100] Indeed, Diodorus goes so far as to say that

"But if any man, putting envy aside, will estimate closely not only the man's natural gifts but also his achievements, he will find that on both counts Themistocles holds first place among all of whom we have record. Therefore one may well be amazed Amazed that the Athenians were willing to rid themselves of a man of such genius."[93]

Since Diodorus's history includes such luminaries as Alexander Alexander_the_Great I the Great and Hannibal, this is high praise indeed. Plutarch offers a more nuanced view of Themistocles, with more of a critique of Themistocles's character. He does not detract from Themistocles's achievements, but also highlights his failings.[19]

Undoubtedly the greatest achievement of Themistocles's career was his role in the defeat of Xerxes I Xerxes_I's invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece.
