The Battle of Lade Miletus was a naval battle Naval_warfare which occurred during the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt, in 494 BC. It was fought between an alliance of the Ionian Ionia cities (joined by the Lesbians) and the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Darius Darius_I the Great, and resulted in a decisive victory for the Persians Achaemenid_Empire which all but ended the revolt.

The Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt was triggered by the dissatisfaction of the Greek Ancient_Greece cities of Asia Minor Anatolia with the tyrants appointed by Persia Achaemenid_Empire to rule them. In 499 BC, the then-tyrant of Miletus, Aristagoras, launched a joint expedition with the Persian Achaemenid_Empire satrap Satrap Artaphernes to conquer Naxos, in an attempt to bolster his position in Miletus. The mission was a debacle, and sensing his imminent removal as tyrant Tyrant, Aristagoras chose to incite the whole of Ionia into rebellion against the Persian Achaemenid_Empire king Darius Darius_I the Great. Initially, in 498 BC, the Ionians went on the offensive, supported by troops from Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria, capturing Sardis, before suffering defeat at the Battle of Ephesus. The revolt then spread to Caria and Cyprus. Three years of Persian Achaemenid_Empire campaigning across Asia Minor Anatolia followed, with no decisive effect. By 494 BC the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army and navy had regrouped, and made straight for the epicentre Epicenter of the rebellion at Miletus.

The Ionians sought to defend Miletus by sea, leaving the defense of Miletus to the Milesians Miletus. The Ionian Ionia fleet gathered at the island of Lade Miletus, off the coast of Miletus. The Persians The_Persians were uncertain of victory at Lade Miletus, so attempted to persuade some of the Ionian Ionia contingents to defect. Although this was unsuccessful at first, when the Persians Achaemenid_Empire finally attacked the Ionians, the Samian Samos fleet accepted the Persian Achaemenid_Empire offer. As the Persian Achaemenid_Empire and Ionian Ionia fleets met, the Samians Samos sailed away from the battle Battle, causing the collapse of the Ionian Ionia battle Battle line. Although the Chian Chios contingent and a few other ships remained and fought bravely against the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, the battle Battle was lost.

With the defeat at Lade Miletus, the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt was all but ended. The next year, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire reduced the last rebel strongholds, and began the process of bringing peace to the region. The Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt constituted the first major conflict between ancient Greece Ancient_Greece and Persia Achaemenid_Empire, and as such represents the first phase of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars. Although Asia Minor Anatolia had been brought back into the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fold, Darius Darius_I vowed to punish Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria for their support of the revolt. Moreover, seeing that the myriad city City states of Greece Ancient_Greece posed a continued threat to the stability of his empire, he decided to conquer the whole of Greece Ancient_Greece. In 492 BC, the first Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece, the next phase of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, would begin as a direct consequence of the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt.

Practically the only primary source for the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus.[1] Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[2] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his 'Enquiries' (Greek—Historia; English Kingdom_of_England—(The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[3] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented 'history' as we know it.[3] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[3]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[4][5] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[5] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On The Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[6] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[7] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds which have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[8] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[8] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[9]

In the dark age that followed the collapse of the Mycenaean civilization Mycenaean_Greece, significant numbers of Greeks Ancient_Greece had emigrated to Asia Minor Anatolia and settled there. These settlers were from three tribal groups: the Aeolians, Dorians and Ionians.[10] The Ionians had settled about the coasts of Lydia and Caria, founding the twelve cities which made up Ionia.[10] These cities were Miletus, Myus and Priene in Caria; Ephesus, Colophon Colophon_(city), Lebedos Lebedus, Teos, Clazomenae Klazomenai, Phocaea and Erythrae in Lydia; and the islands of Samos and Chios.[11] The cities of Ionia had remained independent until they were conquered by the famous Lydian Lydia king Croesus, in around 560 BC.[12] The Ionian Ionia cities then remained under Lydian Lydia rule until Lydia was in turn conquered by the nascent Achaemenid Empire Roman_Empire of Cyrus Cyrus_the_Great.[13] The Persians The_Persians found the Ionians difficult to rule. Elsewhere in the empire, Cyrus Cyrus_the_Great was able to identify elite native groups to help him rule his new subjects—such as the priesthood Clergy of Judea.[14] No such group existed in Greek Ancient_Greece cities at this time; while there was usually an aristocracy, this was inevitably divided into feuding factions.[14] The Persians The_Persians thus settled for the sponsoring a tyrant Tyrant in each Ionian Ionia city City, even though this drew them into the Ionians' internal conflicts. Furthermore, a tyrant Tyrant might develop an independent streak, and have to be replaced.[14] The tyrants themselves faced a difficult task; they had to deflect the worst of their fellow citizens' hatred, while staying in the favour of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[14]

About 40 years after the Persian Achaemenid_Empire conquest of Ionia, and in the reign of the fourth Persian Achaemenid_Empire king, Darius Darius_I the Great, the stand-in Milesian Miletus tyrant Tyrant Aristagoras found himself in this familiar predicament Predicament.[15] In 500 BC, Aristagoras was approached by some exiles from Naxos, who asked him to take control of the island.[16] Seeing an opportunity to strengthen his position in Miletus by conquering Naxos, Aristagoras approached the satrap Satrap of Lydia, Artaphernes, proposing a joint attack on Naxos, to which Artaphernes assented.[17]

The expedition sailed in the spring of 499 BC but quickly descended into a debacle[18] The force laid siege Siege to the Naxians for four months, but eventually the Persians Achaemenid_Empire and Aristagoras both ran out of money. The force therefore sailed despondently back to the mainland.[19] Aristagoras found himself in dire straits Dire_Straits and fully expected to be stripped of his position by Artaphernes. In a desperate attempt to save himself, Aristagoras chose to incite his own subjects, the Milesians Miletus, to revolt against their Persian Achaemenid_Empire masters, thereby beginning the Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt.[20] Although Herodotus presents the revolt as a consequence of Aristagoras's personal motives, it is clear that Ionia must have been ripe for rebellion anyway, the primary grievance being the tyrants installed by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[1] Aristagoras's actions have thus been likened to tossing a flame into a kindling box; they incited rebellion across Ionia (and Aeolis and Doris), and tyrannies were everywhere abolished, and democracies established in their place.[21]

Aristagoras had brought all of Hellenic Ancient_Greece Asia Minor Anatolia into revolt, but evidently realised that the Greeks Ancient_Greece would need other allies in order to fight the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[22] In the winter of 499 BC, he sailed to mainland Greece Ancient_Greece to try to recruit allies. He failed to persuade the Spartans Spartan_army, but the cities of Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria agreed to support the rebellion.[22] In the spring of 498 BC, an Athenian History_of_Athens force of twenty triremes, accompanied by five from Eretria, for a total of twenty-five triremes set sail for Ionia.[23] They joined up with the main Ionian Ionia force near Ephesus.[24] This force was then guided by the Ephesians Ephesus through mountains to Sardis, Artaphernes's satrapal capital.[23] The Greeks Ancient_Greece caught the Persians Achaemenid_Empire unawares, and were able to capture the lower city City. However the lower city City then caught fire, and the Greeks Ancient_Greece, demoralised, then retreated from the city City, and began to make their way back to Ephesus.[25] The Persians The_Persians troops in Asia Minor Anatolia followed the Greek Ancient_Greece force, catching them outside Ephesus. It is clear that the demoralised and tired Greeks Ancient_Greece were no match for the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, and were completely routed in the ensuing battle Battle at Ephesus.[23] The Ionians who escaped the battle Battle made for their own cities, while the remaining Athenians and Eretrians managed to return to their ships, and sailed back to Greece Ancient_Greece.[23][26]

Despite these setbacks, the revolt spread further. The Ionians sent men to the Hellespont Dardanelles and Propontis Sea_of_Marmara, and captured Byzantium and the other nearby cities.[27] They also persuaded the Carians to join the rebellion.[27] Furthermore, seeing the spread of the rebellion, the kingdoms of Cyprus also revolted against Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule without any outside persuasion.[28] For the next three years, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army and navy were fully occupied with fighting the rebellions in Caria and Cyprus, and Ionia seems to have had an uneasy peace during these years.[29] At the height of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire counter-offensive, Aristagoras, sensing the untenability of his position, decided to abandon his position as leader of Miletus, and of the revolt, and he left Miletus. Herodotus, who evidently has a rather negative view of him, suggests that Aristagoras simply lost his nerve Nerve_agent and fled.[30]

By the sixth year of the revolt (494 BC), the Persian Achaemenid_Empire forces had regrouped. The available land forces were gathered into one army, and were accompanied by a fleet supplied by the re-subjugated Cypriots, and the Egyptians Ancient_Egypt, Cilicians and Phoenicians Phoenicia. The Persians The_Persians headed directly to Miletus, paying little attention to other strongholds, presumably intending to tackle the revolt at its epicentre Epicenter.[31] The Median Achaemenid_Empire general Datis, an expert on Greek Ancient_Greece affairs, was certainly dispatched to Ionia by Darius Darius_I at this time. It is therefore possible that he was in overall command of this Persian Achaemenid_Empire offensive.[1] Hearing of the approach of this force, the Ionians met at the Panionium (the sacred meeting ground), and decided not to attempt to fight on land, leaving the Milesians Miletus to defend their walls. Instead, they opted to gather every ship they could, and make for the island of Lade Miletus, off the coast of Miletus, in order to "fight for Miletus at sea".[31]

Herodotus says that there were 600 ships in the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet,[33] provided by the Phoenicians Phoenicia (who were most eager to fight), the Egyptians Ancient_Egypt, Cilicians, and the Cypriots, whose own revolt had recently been subdued.[31] The Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet may have been commanded by the veteran Median Achaemenid_Empire general Datis; Persian Achaemenid_Empire records seem to suggest that he was sent by Darius Darius_I to Ionia at around about the time of Lade Miletus.[29] However, Herodotus does not name any Persian Achaemenid_Empire commanders in this campaign.

When the Persians Achaemenid_Empire arrived off the coast of Lade Miletus and learned the number of Ionian Ionia ships, they began to worry that they would not be able to defeat the Greeks Ancient_Greece, and feared Darius Darius_I's wrath should they fail.[33] The Ionian Ionia tyrants who had been expelled at the beginning of the revolt were present, and according to Herodotus, they were now given instructions by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire:

"Men of Ionia, let each one of you now show that he has done good service to the king's house; let each one of you try to separate your own countrymen from the rest of the allied power Allies_of_World_War_I. Set this promise before them: they will suffer no harm for their rebellion, neither their temples nor their houses will be burnt, nor will they in any way be treated more violently than before. The tyrants thus sent messages to their own kinsman, but the Ionians refused the offers. Critically, each group thought that only they had been approached—there does not seem to have been any discussion of this offer between the different contingents, and the possibility for treachery does not seem to have been realised.[33]

Dionysius Dionysius_I_of_Syracuse thus began an intensive training program, leading the fleet out every day to train the rowers in ramming manoeuvers, and the marines in combat. For seven days the Ionians accepted this regime, but being unused to the hard work, they refused to obey thereafter, and stayed in camp instead.[35] According to Herodotus, upon seeing the resultant discontent and division in the Ionian Ionia camp, the Samians Samos decided to accept the Persian Achaemenid_Empire offer of lenience in return for desertion.[36] However, some modern historians reject the notion of dissent in the Greek camp List_of_Jupiter_trojans_(Greek_camp). Soon after the rebellion against Dionysius Dionysius_I_of_Syracuse, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet moved to attack the Ionians, who sailed out to meet them. The ensuing battle Battle was evidently confused, since Herodotus admits that "which of the Ionians were brave men or cowards then in that sea-fight I cannot exactly say; for they all blame each other".[37] It is nevertheless clear that very early on in the battle Battle, the Samian Samos contingent hoisted their sails, as had been agreed, and fled the battlefield. However, 11 Samian Samos ships refused to desert the other Ionians, and remained at the battle Battle. At some later date, the Samians Samos erected a pillar in their marketplace commemorating the bravery and sacrifice of these crews.[37] Seeing the Samians Samos leave, their neighbours on the western wing, the Lesbians, also fled. The whole west-wing of the Ionian Ionia battle Battle line thus very quickly collapsed.[37] Other Ionian Ionia contingents also fled as the situation became more desperate.

Only the large Chian Chios navy seems to have stood their ground, perhaps accompanied by a few other ships. They fought valiantly, but had huge casualties.[38] Eventually the remaining Chian Chios ships sailed away back to Chios, thereby ending the battle Battle.[39]

With the defeat of the Ionian Ionia fleet, the revolt was effectively over. Miletus was closely invested, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire "mining the walls and using every device against it, until they utterly captured it". According to Herodotus, most of the men were killed, and the women and children were enslaved.[40] Archaeological evidence partially substantiates this, showing widespread signs of destruction, and abandonment of much of the city City in the aftermath of Lade Miletus.[29] However, some Milesians Miletus did remain in (or quickly returned to) Miletus, though the city City would never recapture its former greatness.[1] Miletus was thus notionally "left empty of Milesians Miletus";[41] the Persians Achaemenid_Empire took the city City and coastal land for themselves, and gave the rest of the Milesian Miletus territory to Carians from Pedasus.[42] The captive Milesians Miletus were brought before Darius Darius_I in Susa, who settled them on the coast of the Persian Gulf Persian_Gulf, near the mouth of the Tigris.

Many Samians Samos were appalled by the actions of their generals at Lade Miletus, and resolved to emigrate before their old tyrant Tyrant, Aeaces, returned to rule them. They accepted an invitation from the people of Zancle Messina to settle on the coast of Sicily, and took with them such Milesians Miletus as had escaped from the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[41] Samos itself was spared from destruction by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire because of the Samian Samos defection at Lade Miletus.[43] Meanwhile, Dionysius Dionysius_I_of_Syracuse of Phocaea went to Sicily, and established himself as a pirate Piracy, preying on Carthaginian Carthage ships.[43]

Most of Caria surrendered to the Persians Achaemenid_Empire in aftermath of Lade Miletus, although some strongholds had to be captured through force.[43] The Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet and army wintered at Miletus, before setting out in 493 BC to finally stamp out the last embers Embers of the revolt. They attacked and captured the islands of Chios, Lesbos and Tenedos and then moved over to the mainland, and captured each of the remaining cities of Ionia.[44] Although the cities of Ionia were undoubtedly harrowed in the aftermath, none seems to have suffered quite the fate of Miletus.[45] The Persian Achaemenid_Empire army then re-conquered the settlements on the Asian Asia side of the Propontis Sea_of_Marmara, while the fleet sailed up the European coast of the Hellespont Dardanelles, taking each settlement in turn. With all of Asia Minor Anatolia now firmly returned to Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule, the revolt was finally over.[46]

For the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, the only unfinished business that remained by the end of 493 BC was to exact punishment on Athens History_of_Athens and Eretria for supporting the revolt.[47] The Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt had severely threatened the stability of Darius Darius_I's empire, and the states of mainland Greece Ancient_Greece would continue to threaten that stability unless dealt with.
