The Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War (431–404 BC) was an ancient Greek Ancient_Greece war fought by the Delian League Delian_League led by Athens Classical_Athens against the Peloponnesian League Peloponnesian_League led by Sparta. Historians have traditionally divided the war into three phases. In the first phase, the Archidamian War Peloponnesian_War, Sparta launched repeated invasions of Attica, while Athens Classical_Athens took advantage of its naval supremacy to raid the coast of the Peloponnese and attempt to suppress signs of unrest in its empire. This period of the war was concluded in 421 BC, with the signing of the Peace of Nicias. That treaty, however, was soon undermined by renewed fighting in the Peloponnese. In 415 BC, Athens Classical_Athens dispatched a massive expeditionary force to attack Syracuse Syracuse_Orange_football in Sicily; the attack failed disastrously, with the destruction of the entire force, in 413 BC. This ushered in the final phase of the war, generally referred to either as the Decelean War, or the Ionian Ionia War. In this phase, Sparta, now receiving support from Persia Achaemenid_Empire, supported rebellions in Athens Classical_Athens' subject states in the Aegean Sea Aegean_Sea and Ionia, undermining Athens Classical_Athens' empire, and, eventually, depriving the city City of naval supremacy. The destruction of Athens Classical_Athens' fleet at Aegospotami Battle_of_Aegospotami effectively ended the war, and Athens Classical_Athens surrendered in the following year. Corinth and Thebes Metropolis_of_Thebes_and_Livadeia demanded that Athens Classical_Athens should be destroyed and all its citizens should be enslaved, but Sparta refused.

The term "Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War " was never used by Thucydides, by far its major historian: that the term is all but universally used today is a reflection of the Athens-centric sympathies of modern historians. As prominent historian J. B. Bury remarks, the Peloponnesians would have considered it the "Attic War Attic_War".[1]

The Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War reshaped the ancient Greek Ancient_Greece world. On the level of international International_law relations, Athens Classical_Athens, the strongest city-state Polis in Greece Ancient_Greece prior to the war's beginning, was reduced to a state of near-complete subjection, while Sparta became established as the leading power of Greece Ancient_Greece. The economic costs of the war were felt all across Greece Ancient_Greece; poverty Poverty became widespread in the Peloponnese, while Athens Classical_Athens found itself completely devastated, and never regained its pre-war prosperity.[2][3] The war also wrought subtler changes to Greek Ancient_Greece society; the conflict between democratic Athens Classical_Athens and oligarchic Oligarchy Sparta, each of which supported friendly political factions within other states, made civil war Civil_war a common occurrence in the Greek Ancient_Greece world.

Greek Ancient_Greece warfare, meanwhile, originally a limited and formalized form of conflict, was transformed into an all-out struggle between city-states, complete with atrocities on a large scale. Shattering religious and cultural taboos, devastating vast swathes of countryside, and destroying whole cities, the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War marked the dramatic end to the fifth century BC and the golden age of Greece Ancient_Greece.[4]

As the preeminent Athenian Classical_Athens historian, Thucydides, wrote in his influential History of the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War, "The growth of the power of Athens Classical_Athens, and the alarm which this inspired in Lacedaemon Sparta, made war inevitable."[5] Indeed, the nearly fifty years of Greek Ancient_Greece history that preceded the outbreak of the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War had been marked by the development of Athens Classical_Athens as a major power in the Mediterranean Mediterranean_Sea world. Its empire began as a small group of city-states, called the Delian League—from the island of Delos, on which they kept their treasury—that came together to ensure that the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars were truly over. After defeating the Persian Achaemenid_Empire invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece in the year 480 BC, Athens Classical_Athens led the coalition of Greek Ancient_Greece city-states that continued the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars with attacks on Persian Achaemenid_Empire territories in the Aegean Aegean_Sea and Ionia. What then ensued was a period, referred to as the Pentecontaetia (the name given by Thucydides), in which Athens Classical_Athens increasingly became in fact an empire,[6] carrying out an aggressive war against Persia Achaemenid_Empire and increasingly dominating other city-states. Athens Classical_Athens proceeded to bring under its control all of Greece Ancient_Greece except for Sparta and its allies, ushering in a period which is known to history as the Athenian Empire Roman_Empire. By the middle of the century, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire had been driven from the Aegean Aegean_Sea and forced to cede control of a vast range of territories to Athens Classical_Athens. At the same time, Athens Classical_Athens greatly increased its own power; a number of its formerly independent allies were reduced, over the course of the century, to the status of tribute-paying subject states of the Delian League Delian_League. Friction between Athens Classical_Athens and the Peloponnesian states, including Sparta, began early in the Pentecontaetia; in the wake of the departure of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire from Greece Ancient_Greece, Sparta attempted to prevent the reconstruction of the walls of Athens Classical_Athens (without the walls, Athens Classical_Athens would have been defenseless Defenseless against a land attack and subject to Spartan control), but was rebuffed.[8] According to Thucydides, although the Spartans Spartan_army took no action at this time, they "secretly felt aggrieved".[9] Conflict between the states flared up again in 465 BC, when a helot Helots revolt broke out in Sparta. In 459 BC 459_BC, Athens Classical_Athens took advantage of a war between its neighbors Megara and Corinth, both Spartan allies, to conclude an alliance with Megara, giving the Athenians a critical foothold on the Isthmus of Corinth. A fifteen-year conflict, commonly known as the First Peloponnesian War First_Peloponnesian_War, ensued, in which Athens Classical_Athens fought intermittently against Sparta, Corinth, Aegina, and a number of other states. Sparta and its allies, with the exception of Corinth, were almost exclusively land-based powers, able to summon large land armies which were very nearly unbeatable (thanks to the legendary Spartan forces).
