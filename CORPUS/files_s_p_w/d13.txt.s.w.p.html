Ancient_Greece is a country in southeastern Europe, known in Ancient_Greece as Hellas or Ellada, and consisting of a mainland and an Archipelago of islands. Ancient_Greece is the birthplace of Western_philosophy (Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle), literature (Homer and Hesiod), Greek_mathematics (Pythagoras and Euclid), history (Herodotus), drama (Sophocles, Euripides, and Aristophanes), the Ancient_Olympic_Games, and Athenian_democracy. The concept of an atomic universe was first posited in Ancient_Greece through the work of Democritus and Leucippus. The process of today's Scientific_method was first introduced through the work of Thales of Miletus and those who followed him. Mainland Ancient_Greece is a large peninsula surrounded on three sides by the Mediterranean_Sea (branching into the Ionian_Sea in the west and the Aegean_Sea in the east) which also comprises the islands known as the Cyclades and the Dodecanese (including Rhodes), the Ionian_Islands (including Corfu), the isle of Crete, and the southern peninsula known as the Peloponnese.

The Geography of Ancient_Greece greatly influenced the culture in that, with few natural resources and surrounded by water, the people eventually took to the sea for their livelihood. Mountains cover eighty percent of Ancient_Greece and only small rivers run through a rocky landscape which, for the most part, provides little encouragement for Agriculture. Consequently, the early Ancient_Greece colonized neighboring islands and founded settlements along the coast of Anatolia (also known as Anatolia, modern day Ottoman_Empire). The Ancient_Greece became skilled seafaring people and traders who, possessing an abundance of raw materials for construction in stone, and great skill, built some of the most impressive structures in Ancient_Greece.

The designation Hellas derives from Hellen, the son of Deucalion and Pyrrha who feature prominently in Ovid's tale of the Flood_myth in his Metamorphoses. Deucalion and Pyrrha repopulate the land once the flood waters have receded by casting stones which become people, the first being Hellen. Contrary to popular opinion, Hellas and Ellada have nothing to do with Helen of Troy from Homer's Iliad. Ovid, however, did not Roman_currency the designation. Ancient_Greece history is most easily understood by dividing it into time periods. The region was already settled, and Agriculture initiated, during the Paleolithic era as evidenced by finds at Petralona_cave and Franchthi_Cave caves (two of the oldest human habitations in the world). The Neolithic (c. 6000 - c. 2900 Common_Era) is characterized by permanent settlements (primarily in northern Ancient_Greece), Domestication of animals, and the further development of Agriculture. Archaeological finds in northern Ancient_Greece (Thessaly, Republic_of_Macedonia, and Sesklo, among others) suggest a migration from Anatolia in that the Pottery cups and bowls and figures found there share qualities distinctive to Neolithic finds in Anatolia. These inland settlers were primarily farmers, as northern Ancient_Greece was more conducive to Agriculture than elsewhere in the region, and lived in one-room stone houses with a roof of timber and Pottery daubing.

The Cycladic_culture (c. 3200-1100 Common_Era) flourished in the islands of the Aegean_Sea (including Delos, Naxos and Paros) and provides the earliest evidence of continual human habitation in that region. During the Cyclades Period, houses and temples were built of finished stone and the people made their living through fishing and trade. This period is usually divided into three phases: Early Cyclades, Middle Cyclades, and Late Cyclades with a steady development in art and architecture. The latter two phases overlap and finally merge with the Minoan_civilization, and differences between the periods become indistinguishable.

The Minoan_civilization (2700-1500 Common_Era) developed on the island of Crete, and rapidly became the dominant Naval_warfare in the region. The term `Minoan_civilization' was coined by the Archaeology Sir Arthur_Evans, who uncovered the Minoan_civilization palace of Knossos in 1900 CE and named the culture for the ancient Minoan_civilization Minos. The name by which the people knew themselves is not known. The Minoan_civilization was thriving, as the Cycladic_culture seems to have been, long before the accepted modern dates which mark its existence and probably earlier than 6000 Common_Era.

The Minoan_civilization developed a Egyptian_hieroglyphs known as Linear_A (which has not yet been deciphered) and made advances in ship building, construction, ceramics, the arts and sciences, and warfare. Minos was credited by ancient historians (Thucydides among them) as being the first person to establish a navy with which he colonized, or conquered, the Cyclades. Archaeological and geological evidence on Crete suggests this civilization fell due to an overuse of the land causing Deforestation though, traditionally, it is accepted that they were conquered by the Mycenaean_Greece. The eruption of the Minoan_eruption on the nearby island of Santorini (modern day Santorini) between 1650 and 1550 Common_Era, and the resulting Tsunami, is acknowledged as the final cause for the fall of the Minoan_civilization. The isle of Crete was deluged and the cities and villages destroyed. This event has been frequently cited as Plato's inspiration in creating his myth of Atlantis in his dialogues of the Critias and Timaeus_of_Locri.

The Mycenaean_Greece appear to have been greatly influenced by the Minoan_civilization of Crete in their worship of earth goddesses and sky gods, which, in time, become the Classical_antiquity pantheon of Ancient_Greece.

The gods and goddesses provided the Ancient_Greece with a solid paradigm of the creation of the universe, the world, and human beings. An early myth relates how, in the beginning, there was nothing but chaos in the form of Unending waters. From this chaos came the goddess Eurynome who separated the water from the air and began her Dance of creation with the Serpents_in_the_Bible Ophion. From their Dance, all of creation sprang and Eurynome was, originally, the Great Mother_goddess and Creator of All Things.

By the time Hesiod and Homer were writing (8th century Common_Era), this story had changed into the more familiar myth concerning the titans, Zeus' war against them, and the birth of the Twelve_Olympians with Zeus as their chief. Whichever model was followed, however, the gods clearly interacted regularly with the humans who worshipped them and were a large part of daily life in Ancient_Greece.
