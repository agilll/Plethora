To block the Persian Achaemenid_Empire advance, a small force of Greeks Ancient_Greece blocked the pass of Thermopylae, while an Athenian-dominated Allied Allies_of_World_War_II navy engaged the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet in the nearby straits of Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium. This allowed the Persians Achaemenid_Empire to conquer Phocis, Boeotia, Attica, and Euboea. The Allies Allies_of_World_War_II prepared to defend the Isthmus of Corinth whilst the fleet was withdrawn to nearby Salamis Salamis_Island.

The main source Main_Source for the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars is the Greek Ancient_Greece historian Herodotus. Herodotus, who has been called the 'Father of History',[6] was born in 484 BC in Halicarnassus, Asia Minor Anatolia (then under Persian Achaemenid_Empire overlordship). He wrote his Enquiries (Greek Ancient_Greece: Ιστορίαι; English Kingdom_of_England: (The) Histories Herodotus) around 440–430 BC, trying to trace the origins of the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars, which would still have been relatively recent history (the wars finally ending in 450 BC).[7] Herodotus's approach was entirely novel, and at least in Western society, he does seem to have invented "history" as we know it.[7] As Holland has it: "For the first time, a chronicler set himself to trace the origins of a conflict not to a past so remote so as to be utterly fabulous, nor to the whims and wishes of some god, nor to a people's claim to manifest destiny Manifest_destiny, but rather explanations he could verify personally."[7]

Some subsequent ancient historians, despite following in his footsteps, criticised Herodotus, starting with Thucydides.[8][9] Nevertheless, Thucydides chose to begin his history where Herodotus left off (at the Siege of Sestos), and therefore evidently felt that Herodotus's history was accurate enough not to need re-writing or correcting.[9] Plutarch criticised Herodotus in his essay "On the Malignity of Herodotus", describing Herodotus as "Philobarbaros" (barbarian-lover), for not being pro-Greek enough, which suggests that Herodotus might actually have done a reasonable job of being even-handed.[10] A negative view of Herodotus was passed on to Renaissance Europe, though he remained well read.[11] However, since the 19th century his reputation has been dramatically rehabilitated by archaeological finds which have repeatedly confirmed his version of events.[12] The prevailing modern view is that Herodotus generally did a remarkable job in his Historia, but that some of his specific details (particularly troop numbers and dates) should be viewed with skepticism Skepticism.[12] Nevertheless, there are still some historians who believe Herodotus made up much of his story.[13]

The Sicilian The_Sicilian historian Diodorus Siculus Diodorus_Siculus, writing in the 1st century BC in his Bibliotheca Historica Bibliotheca_historica, also provides an account of the Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars, partially derived from the earlier Greek Ancient_Greece historian Ephorus. This account is fairly consistent with Herodotus's.[14] The Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars are also described in less detail by a number of other ancient historians including Plutarch, Ctesias of Cnidus Knidos, and are alluded by other authors, such as the playwright Theatre Aeschylus. Archaeological evidence, such as the Serpent Column Serpent_Column, also supports some of Herodotus's specific claims.[15]

The Greek Ancient_Greece city-states of Athens Classical_Athens and Eretria had supported the unsuccessful Ionian Revolt Ionian_Revolt against the Persian Empire Roman_Empire of Darius I Darius_I in 499-494 BC, led by the satrap Satrap of Miletus, Aristagoras. In 491 BC, Darius I Darius_I sent emissaries to all the Greek Ancient_Greece city-states, asking for a gift of 'earth and water' in token of their submission to him.[21] Having had a demonstration of his power the previous year, the majority of Greek Ancient_Greece cities duly obliged. Darius I Darius_I therefore began raising a huge new army with which he meant to completely subjugate Greece Ancient_Greece; however, in 486 BC, his Egyptian Ancient_Egypt subjects revolted, indefinitely postponing any Greek Ancient_Greece expedition.[17] Darius I Darius_I then died whilst preparing to march on Egypt Ptolemaic_Kingdom, and the throne of Persia Achaemenid_Empire passed to his son Xerxes I Xerxes_I.[24] Xerxes I Xerxes_I crushed the Egyptian revolt Ptolemy_V_Epiphanes, and very quickly restarted the preparations for the invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece.[25] Since this was to be a full-scale invasion, it required long-term planning, stock-piling and conscription Conscription.[25] Xerxes I Xerxes_I decided that the Hellespont Dardanelles would be bridged to allow his army to cross to Europe, and that a canal should be dug across the isthmus Isthmus_of_Corinth of Mount Athos Mount_Athos (rounding which headland Headlands_and_bays, a Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet had been destroyed in 492 BC).[26] These were both feats of exceptional ambition, which would have been beyond any other contemporary state.[26] By early 480 BC, the preparations were complete, and the army which Xerxes I Xerxes_I had mustered at Sardis marched towards Europe, crossing the Hellespont Dardanelles on two pontoon bridges.[27]

The Athenians had also been preparing for war with the Persians Achaemenid_Empire since the mid-480s BC 480s_BC, and in 482 BC the decision was taken, under the guidance of the Athenian Classical_Athens politician Themistocles, to build a massive fleet of triremes that would be necessary for the Greeks Ancient_Greece to fight the Persians Achaemenid_Empire.[28] However, the Athenians did not have the manpower to fight on land and sea; and therefore combatting the Persians Achaemenid_Empire would require an alliance of Greek Ancient_Greece city City states. In 481 BC, Xerxes I Xerxes_I sent ambassadors around Greece Ancient_Greece asking for earth and water, but made the very deliberate omission of Athens Classical_Athens and Sparta.[29] Support thus began to coalesce around these two leading states. A congress of city City states met at Corinth in late autumn Late_Autumn_(1960_film) of 481 BC,[30] and a confederate alliance of Greek Ancient_Greece city-states was formed. It had the power to send envoys asking for assistance and to dispatch troops from the member states to defensive points after joint consultation. Initially the 'congress' agreed to defend the narrow Vale of Tempe Vale_of_Tempe, on the borders of Thessaly, and thereby block Xerxes I Xerxes_I's advance.[32] However, once there, they were warned by Alexander Alexander_the_Great I of Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia that the vale could be bypassed through the pass by the modern village of Sarantaporo, and that the army of Xerxes I Xerxes_I was overwhelming, the Greeks Ancient_Greece retreated.[33] Shortly afterwards, they received the news that Xerxes I Xerxes_I had crossed the Hellespont Dardanelles. The Persians The_Persians were at a significant tactical advantage, outnumbering the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II, and also having "better sailing" ships.[96] The "better sailing" that Herodotus mentions was probably due to the superior seamanship of the crews;[96] most of the Athenian Classical_Athens ships (and therefore the majority of the fleet) were newly built as according to Themistocles' request to the Athenians to build a fleet of 200 triremes in 483 BC, and had inexperienced crews. It is not entirely clear what this was, but it probably involved rowing into gaps between enemy ships and then ramming them in the side.[98] This maneuver would have required skilled crews, and therefore the Persians Achaemenid_Empire would have been more likely to employ it; the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II however, developed tactics specifically to counter this.[98]

There has been much debate as to the nature of the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet compared to the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet. Much of this centres on the suggestion, from Herodotus, that the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II ships were heavier, and by implication less maneuverable.[99] The source List_of_Charmed_characters of this heaviness is uncertain; possibly the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II ships were bulkier in construction, or that the ships were water-logged since they had not been dried out in the winter (though there is no real evidence for either suggestion).[98] Another suggestion is that the heaviness was caused by the weight of fully armored hoplite Hoplite marines (20 fully armored hoplites Hoplite would have weighed 2 tons).[98] This 'heaviness', whatever its cause, would further reduce the likelihood of them employing the diekplous.[98] It is therefore probable that the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II had extra marines on board if their ships were less maneuverable, since boarding would then be the main tactic available to them (at the cost of making the ships even heavier).[98] Indeed, Herodotus refers to the Greeks Ancient_Greece capturing ships at Artemisium Battle_of_Artemisium, rather than sinking them.[74] It has been suggested that the weight of the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II ships may also have made them more stable in the winds off the coast of Salamis Salamis_Island, and made them less susceptible to ramming (or rather, less liable to sustain damage when rammed).[100]

The actual The_Actual_(band) battle Battle of Salamis Salamis_Island is not well described by the ancient sources, and it is unlikely that anyone (other than perhaps Xerxes I Xerxes_I) involved in the battle Battle had a clear idea what was happening across the width of the straits.[48][102] What follows is more of a discussion than a definitive account.

In the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet, the Athenians were on the left, and on the right were probably the Spartans Spartan_army (although Diodorus says it was the Megareans and Aeginetians); the other contingents were in the center.[103][104] The Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet probably formed into two ranks, since the straits would have been too narrow for a single line of ships.[105] Herodotus has the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet in a line running north-south, probably with the northern flank off the coast of modern-day Saint George Saint_George's Islet (Ayios Georgis), and the southern flank off the coast of Cape Vavari (part of Salamis Salamis_Island).[106] Diodorus suggests the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet was aligned east-west, spanning the straits between Salamis Salamis_Island and Mount Aigaleo Mount_Aigaleo; however, it is unlikely that the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II would have rested one of their flanks against Persian Achaemenid_Empire occupied territory.[106]

It seems relatively certain that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet was sent out to block the exit from the Straits the evening before the battle Battle. Herodotus clearly believed that the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet actually entered the Straits at nightfall, planning to catch the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II as they fled.[107] However, modern historians have greatly debated this point, with some pointing out the difficulties of maneuvering in this confined space by night, and others accepting Herodotus's version.[108][109] There are thus two possibilities; that during the night the Persians Achaemenid_Empire simply blocked the exit to the Straits, and then entered the straits in daylight; or that they entered the straits and positioned themselves for battle Battle during the night.[108][109] Regardless of when they attempted it, it seems likely that the Persians Achaemenid_Empire pivoted their fleet off the tip of Cape Vavari, so that from an initial east-west alignment (blocking the exit), they came round to a north-south alignment (see diagram).[110] The Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet seems to have been formed into three ranks of ships (according to Aeschylus);[48] with the powerful Phoenician Phoenicia fleet on the right flank next to Mount Aigaleo Mount_Aigaleo, the Ionian Ionia contingent on the left flank and the other contingents in the centre.[103]

Diodorus says that the Egyptian Ancient_Egypt fleet was sent to circumnavigate Circumnavigation Salamis Salamis_Island, and block the northern exit from the Straits.[111] If Xerxes I Xerxes_I wanted to trap the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II completely, this maneuver would have made sense (especially if he was not expecting the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II to fight).[48] However, Herodotus does not mention this (and possibly alludes to the Egyptian Ancient_Egypt presence in the main battle Battle), leading some modern historians to dismiss it;[110] though again, others accept it as a possibility.[48] Xerxes I Xerxes_I had also positioned around 400 troops on the island known as Psyttaleia, in the middle of the exit from the straits, in order to kill or capture any Greeks Ancient_Greece who ended up there (as a result of shipwreck Shipwreck or grounding).[51]

Regardless of what time they entered the straits, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire did not move to attack the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II until daylight. Since they were not planning to flee after all, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II would have been able to spend the night preparing for battle Battle, and after a speech by Themistocles, the marines boarded and the ships made ready to sail.[56] According to Herodotus, this was dawn, and as the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II "were putting out to sea the barbarians immediately attacked them".[56][112] If the Persians Achaemenid_Empire only entered the straits at dawn, then the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II would have had the time to take up their station in a more orderly fashion.[108]

Aeschylus claims that as the Persians Achaemenid_Empire approached (possibly implying that they were not already in the Straits at dawn), they heard the Greeks Ancient_Greece singing their battle Battle hymn Psalms (paean Paean) before they saw the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet:

O sons of the Greeks Ancient_Greece, go,
Liberate your country, liberate
Your children, your women, the seats of your fathers' gods,
And the tombs of your forebears Varg_Vikernes: now is the struggle for all things.


Herodotus recounts that, according to the Athenians, as the battle Battle began the Corinthians Ancient_Corinth hoisted their sails and began sailing away from the battle Battle, northwards up the straits.[114] However, he also says that other Greeks Ancient_Greece denied this story.[114] If this did in fact occur, one possible interpretation is that these ships had been a decoy sent to reconnoitre the northern exit from the straits, in case the arrival of the encircling Egyptian Ancient_Egypt detachment was imminent (if indeed this also occurred).[108] Another possibility (not exclusive of the former) is that the departure of the Corinthians Ancient_Corinth triggered the final approach of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, suggesting as it did that the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet was disintegrating.[108] At any rate, if they indeed ever left, the Corinthians Ancient_Corinth soon returned to the battle Battle.[114]

Approaching the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet in the crowded Straits, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire appear to have become disorganised and cramped in the narrow waters.[104][108] Moreover, it would have become apparent that, far from disintegrating, the Greek Ancient_Greece fleet was lined up, ready to attack them.[105][108] However, rather than attacking immediately, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II initially appeared to back their ships away as if in fear.[112] According to Plutarch, this was to gain better position, and also in order to gain time until the early morning wind Wind.[115] Herodotus recounts the legend that as the fleet had backed away, they had seen an apparition of a woman, asking them "Madmen, how far will ye yet back your ships?"[116] However, he more plausibly suggests that whilst the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II were backing water, a single ship shot forward to ram the nearest Persian Achaemenid_Empire vessel. The Athenians would claim that this was the ship of the Athenian Classical_Athens Ameinias Ameinias_of_Athens of Pallene Pallini; the Aeginetans would claim it as one of their ships.[112] The whole Greek Ancient_Greece line then followed suit and made straight for the disordered Persian Achaemenid_Empire battle Battle line.[117]

The details The_Details of the rest of the battle Battle are generally sketchy, and no one involved would have had a view of the entire battlefield.[108] Triremes were generally armed with a large ram at the front, with which it was possible to sink an enemy ship, or at least disable it by shearing off the banks of oars on one side.[98][118] If the initial ramming was not successful, marines boarded the enemy ship and something similar to a land battle Battle ensued.[98] Both sides had marines on their ships for this eventuality; the Greeks Ancient_Greece with fully armed hoplites Hoplite;[108] the Persians Achaemenid_Empire probably with more lightly armed infantry.[119]

Across the battlefield, as the first line of Persian Achaemenid_Empire ships was pushed back by the Greeks Ancient_Greece, they became fouled in the advancing second and third lines of their own ships.[121] On the Greek left Greek_Left, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire admiral Admiral Ariabignes (a brother of Xerxes I Xerxes_I)[121] was killed early in the battle Battle; left disorganised and leaderless, the Phoenician Phoenicia squadrons appear to have been pushed back against the coast, many vessels running aground.[108] In the centre, a wedge of Greek Ancient_Greece ships pushed through the Persians Achaemenid_Empire lines, splitting the fleet in two.[108] According to Plutarch, Ariabignes was killed by Ameinias Ameinias_of_Athens and Socles (Greek Ancient_Greece: Σωκλής) of Pallene Pallini. When Ariabignes attempted to board on their ship, they hit him with their spears, and thrust him into the sea.[122] Plutarch also mentions that it was Artemisia Artemisia_I_of_Caria who recognized Ariabignes' body floating among the shipwrecks and brought it back to Xerxes I Xerxes_I.[123]

Herodotus recounts that Artemisia Artemisia_I_of_Caria, the Queen of Halicarnassus, and commander of the Carian Carians contingent, found herself pursued by the ship of Ameinias Ameinias_of_Athens of Pallene Pallini. In her desire to escape, she attacked and rammed another Persian Achaemenid_Empire vessel, thereby convincing the Athenian Classical_Athens captain Sea_captain that the ship was an ally; Ameinias Ameinias_of_Athens accordingly abandoned the chase.[124] However, Xerxes I Xerxes_I, looking on, thought that she had successfully attacked an Allied Allies_of_World_War_II ship, and seeing the poor performance of his other captains commented that "My men have become women, and my women men".[125] The friendly ship she sank was a Calyndian ship and the king of the Calyndians, Damasithymos Damasithymus (Greek Ancient_Greece: Δαμασίθυμος) was on it.[126][127] None of the crew of the Calyndian ship survived.[128]

The Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet began to retreat towards Phalerum, but according to Herodotus, the Aeginetans ambushed them as they tried to leave the Straits.[129] The remaining The_Remaining Persian Achaemenid_Empire ships limped back to the harbour Harbor of Phalerum and the shelter of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army.[130] The Athenian Classical_Athens general Aristides then took a detachment of men across to Psyttaleia to slaughter the garrison that Xerxes I Xerxes_I had left there.[131] The exact Persian Achaemenid_Empire casualties are not mentioned by Herodotus. However, he writes that the next year, the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet numbered 300 triremes.[132] The number of losses then depends on the number of ships the Persian Achaemenid_Empire had to begin with; something in the range of 200–300 seems likely, based on the above estimates for the size of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet. According to Herodotus, the Persians Achaemenid_Empire suffered many more casualties than the Greeks Ancient_Greece because most Persians Achaemenid_Empire did not know how to swim.[121] Xerxes I Xerxes_I, sitting on Mount Aigaleo Mount_Aigaleo on his throne, witnessed the carnage Carnage_the_Executioner.[133] Some ship-wrecked Phoenician Phoenicia captains tried to blame the Ionians for cowardice before the end of the battle Battle.[133] Xerxes I Xerxes_I, in a foul mood, and having just witnessed an Ionian Ionia ship capture an Aeginetan ship, had the Phoenicians Phoenicia beheaded for slandering "more noble men".[133] According to Diodorus, Xerxes I Xerxes_I "put to death those Phoenicians Phoenicia who were chiefly responsible for beginning the flight, and threatened to visit upon the rest the punishment they deserved", causing the Phoenicians Phoenicia to sail to Asia Anatolia when night fell.[134]

In the immediate aftermath of Salamis Salamis_Island, Xerxes I Xerxes_I attempted to build a pontoon bridge Pontoon_bridge or causeway Causeway across the straits, in order to use his army to attack the Athenians; however, with the Greek Ancient_Greece fleet now confidently patrolling the straits, this proved futile.[90] Herodotus tells us that Xerxes I Xerxes_I held a council of war, at which the Persian Achaemenid_Empire general Mardonius tried to make light of the defeat:

Sire, be not grieved nor greatly distressed because of what has befallen us. It is not on things of wood Wood that the issue hangs for us, but on men and horses...If then you so desire, let us straightway attack the Peloponnese, or if it pleases you to wait, that also we can do...It is best then that you should do as I have said, but if you have resolved to lead your army away, even then I have another plan. Do not, O king, make the Persians Achaemenid_Empire the laughing-stock of the Greeks Ancient_Greece, for if you have suffered harm, it is by no fault of the Persians Achaemenid_Empire. Nor can you say that we have anywhere done less than brave men should, and if Phoenicians Phoenicia and Egyptians Ancient_Egypt and Cyprians and Cilicians have so done, it is not the Persians Achaemenid_Empire who have any part in this disaster. It is for me, however, to enslave and deliver Hellas to you with three hundred thousand of your host whom I will choose.[135]

Fearing that the Greeks Ancient_Greece might attack the bridges across the Hellespont Dardanelles and trap his army in Europe, Xerxes I Xerxes_I resolved to do this, taking the greater part of the army with him.[136] Mardonius handpicked the troops who were to remain with him in Greece Ancient_Greece, taking the elite infantry units and cavalry Cavalry, to complete the conquest of Greece Ancient_Greece.[90] All of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire forces abandoned Attica, however, with Mardonius over-wintering in Boeotia and Thessaly; the Athenians were thus able to return to their burnt city Shahr-e_Sukhteh for the winter.[90]

The following year, 479 BC, Mardonius recaptured Athens Classical_Athens (the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II army still preferring to guard the Isthmus). However, the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II, under Spartan leadership, eventually agreed to try to force Mardonius to battle Battle, and marched on Attica.[137] Mardonius retreated to Boeotia to lure the Greeks Ancient_Greece into open terrain and the two sides eventually met near the city City of Plataea (which had been razed the previous year).[137] There, at the Battle of Plataea, the Greek Ancient_Greece army won a decisive victory, destroying much of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire army and ending the invasion of Greece Ancient_Greece; whilst at the near-simultaneous Battle of Mycale Battle_of_Mycale the Allied Allies_of_World_War_II fleet destroyed much of the remaining Persian Achaemenid_Empire fleet.[137]

The Battle of Salamis Salamis_Island marked the turning point in the Greco-Persian wars Greco-Persian_Wars.[92] After Salamis Salamis_Island, the Peloponnese, and by extension Greece Ancient_Greece as an entity, was safe from conquest; and the Persians Achaemenid_Empire suffered a major blow to their prestige and morale (as well as severe material losses).[138] At the following battles of Plataea and Mycale Battle_of_Mycale, the threat of conquest was removed, and the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II were able to go on the counter-offensive.[139] The Greek Ancient_Greece victory allowed Macedon Achaemenid_Macedonia to revolt against Persian Achaemenid_Empire rule; and over the next 30 years, Thrace, the Aegean Islands Aegean_Islands and finally Ionia would be removed from Persian Achaemenid_Empire control by the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II, or by the Athenian-dominated successor, the Delian League Delian_League.[140] Salamis Salamis_Island started a decisive swing Swing_bowling in the balance of power toward the Greeks Ancient_Greece, which would culminate in an eventual Greek Ancient_Greece victory, severely reducing Persian Achaemenid_Empire power in the Aegean Aegean_Sea.[141]

Like the Battles of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon and Thermopylae, Salamis Salamis_Island has gained something of a 'legendary' status (unlike, for instance, the more decisive Battle of Plataea), perhaps because of the desperate circumstances and the unlikely odds.[7] A significant number of historians have stated that Salamis Salamis_Island is one of the most significant battles in human history (though the same is often stated of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon).[5][3][100][102] In a more extreme form of this argument, some historians argue that if the Greeks Ancient_Greece had lost at Salamis Salamis_Island, the ensuing conquest of Greece Ancient_Greece by the Persians Achaemenid_Empire would have effectively stifled the growth of Western Civilization as we know it.[142] This view is based on the premise that much of modern Western society, such as philosophy Ancient_Greek_philosophy, science, personal freedom and democracy Athenian_democracy are rooted in the legacy of Ancient Greece Ancient_Greece.[7] Thus, this school of thought argues that, given the domination of much of modern history by Western Civilization, Persian Achaemenid_Empire domination of Greece Ancient_Greece might have changed the whole trajectory of human history.[5] It is also worth mentioning that the celebrated blossoming of hugely influential Athenian Classical_Athens culture occurred only after the Persian Achaemenid_Empire wars were won.[143][144][145]

Militarily, it is difficult to draw many lessons from Salamis Salamis_Island, because of the uncertainty about what actually happened. Once again the Allies Allies_of_World_War_II chose their ground well in order to negate Persian Achaemenid_Empire numbers, but this time (unlike Thermopylae) had to rely on the Persians Achaemenid_Empire launching an unnecessary attack for their position to count.[93] (Hale Hale,_Greater_Manchester, John R.). Since it brought about that attack, perhaps the most important military lesson is to be found in the use of deception by Themistocles to bring about the desired response from the enemy.[93]
