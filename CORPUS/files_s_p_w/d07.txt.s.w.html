Society and values

The world of the colonizing states was aristocratic in the sense that a small number of exclusive clans within cities monopolized citizenship and political control. At Corinth, for example, political control was monopolized by the adult males of a single clan Clan, the Bacchiadae. They perhaps numbered no more than a couple of hundred. At Athens Classical_Athens there was a general class of Eupatridae, a word that just means people of good descent—i.e., aristocrats. (The word may have had a simultaneous but narrower application to one single genos Genos. This, however, is disputed, and, in any event, that hypothetical family was only one among many privileged genē. The case, therefore, is not analogous to that of the Bacchiadae.) It is unlikely that the Eupatridae were as rigidly defined as the Bacchiadae, and the negative tradition that Solon in the early 6th century deprived them of their exclusive claim to political office may just be the excessively formal and precise way in which later ancient commentators described a positive change by which power was made more generally available than it had been before.

Another way of institutionalizing relationships between the nationals of different states was epigamia Epigamia, an arrangement by which the offspring of marriage were treated as citizens of the wife’s polis if the husband settled there; and so was the husband. Athens Classical_Athens, for example, granted epigamia Epigamia to Euboea as late as the 5th century, a time when Athenian Classical_Athens citizenship was fiercely protected. There are still earlier instances: usually one hears of epigamia Epigamia when for one reason or another it was being suspended or denied. Thus, there was an early arrangement between the islands of Andros and Paros, which, Plutarch says, ended when relations went sour. More interesting is the statement, again by Plutarch, that there was no intermarriage between members of two of the villages, or demes Deme, of Attica, Pallene Pallini and Hagnous. Far from being evidence that these places were somehow originally separate states, the prohibition was more like a ban on endogamy Endogamy: in other words, the two communities were regarded—like members of a family—as being too close to be allowed to intermarry.
