By the first years of the 5th century Common_Era, Achaemenid_Empire, under the rule of Darius_I (r. 522-486 Common_Era), was already expanding into mainland Europe and had subjugated Thrace and Republic_of_Macedonia. The next objective was to quell once and for all the collection of potentially troublesome rebel states on the western border of the Roman_Empire. In 490 Common_Era Ancient_Greece forces led by History_of_Athens met the Achaemenid_Empire in Battle at Battle_of_Marathon and defeated the invaders. The Battle would take on mythical status amongst the Ancient_Greece, but in reality it was merely the opening Overture of a long war with several other battles making up the principal acts. In 486 Common_Era Xerxes_I became king, and he invaded first the Cyclades and then the Ancient_Greece mainland after victory against a token Ancient_Greece force at Battle_of_Thermopylae in August 480 Common_Era. At the same time as Battle_of_Thermopylae, an indecisive Naval_warfare took place at Artemisium, where the Ancient_Greece held off the superior numbers of the Achaemenid_Empire fleet but were obliged to regroup at Battle_of_Salamis. In September 490 Common_Era, with the brilliant general Themistocles dictating strategy, the allied Ancient_Greece fleet drew the Achaemenid_Empire into the narrow confines of the straits between Battle_of_Salamis and the mainland and won a resounding victory.

Following the defeat of his armada and the close of the campaign season, Xerxes_I returned home to his palace at John_Philip_Sousa and left the Child_prodigy general Mardonius in charge of the invasion. The Achaemenid_Empire position was still strong despite the defeat at sea - they still controlled much of Ancient_Greece and their huge land army was intact. The_Persians were no doubt confident of success. They had a massive army, the Ancient_Greece were potentially divided, and the only Blip_(website) in the long chain of land victories they had enjoyed in the previous 50 years had been the setback at Battle_of_Marathon.

The two opposing armies were essentially representative of the two approaches to Classical warfare - the Achaemenid_Empire favoured long-range assault using archers followed up with a Cavalry charge, whilst the Ancient_Greece favoured heavily-armoured Hoplite, arranged in a densely packed formation at least eight men deep called the Phalanx, with each man carrying a heavy round bronze shield and fighting at close quarters using spears and swords. The Achaemenid_Empire infantry carried a lightweight wicker shield and were armed with a long Dagger or battleaxe, a short spear, and Composite_bow. The Achaemenid_Empire forces also included the Immortality - an elite force who were probably better protected with armour and armed with spears, a contingent of well-armed Ancient_Egypt marines and several thousand Hoplite from allied Ancient_Greece states, notably the Thebes,_Greece. The Achaemenid_Empire Cavalry were armed, as the foot soldiers, with a Bow_and_arrow and an additional two javelins for throwing and thrusting. Cavalry, usually operating on the flanks of the main Battle, were used to mop up opposing infantry put in disarray after they had been subjected to repeated salvos from the archers. Although the Achaemenid_Empire had enjoyed the upper hand in previous contests with Ancient_Greece during the recent Ionian_Revolt, Battle_of_Thermopylae had shown that organised Ancient_Greece Hoplite could be a tough military Areca_nut to crack.

Another important factor was that although the Achaemenid_Empire tactic of rapidly firing vast numbers of arrows into the enemy must have been an awesome sight, the lightness of the arrows meant that they were largely ineffective against the bronze-armoured Hoplite unless fired at close range and at unprotected body parts. At_Close_Quarters, the longer spears, heavier swords, better armour, and rigid discipline of the Phalanx meant that the Ancient_Greece Hoplite would have all of the advantages on broken terrain. The_Persians would have to entice the enemy into good flat land to optimise their Cavalry advantage and the greater degree of mobility offered by their lighter-armoured troops.

In the spring of 479 Common_Era the Achaemenid_Empire fleet re-grouped at Samos, whilst in June Mardonius left his winter quarters in Thessaly and once again invaded Attica before withdrawing to hold a line in Boeotia, north of the river Asopus, where he built a large fortified camp.

The Ancient_Greece fleet, meanwhile, re-grouped at Aegina and then sailed to hold station at Delos whilst the land army mobilised. In July the Spartan_army moved towards Battle_of_Plataea and met up with the other Ancient_Greece contingents at Eleusis before all moved into position, forming a 7 km long front just 3-4 km opposite the Achaemenid_Empire, below the low hills of Cithaeron.

Mardonius had the Achaemenid_Empire hold the right flank, and in the centre were the Medes and the Bactria, Indians, and Sacae groups. On the left flank were the pro-Persian Ancient_Greece. Cavalry forces sat slightly back, one group on each flank. Along the Ancient_Greece front, the Spartan_army, Tegeans, and Thespiae held the right flank and the Athenians, Megara, and Plataeans the left flank, with everyone else in the centre. Once in position, everybody waited.
