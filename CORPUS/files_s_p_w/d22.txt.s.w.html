Sparta was located in the fertile Eurotas valley of Laconia in the southeast Peloponnese. The area was first settled in the Neolithic period and an important settlement developed in the Bronze Age Bronze_Age. Archaeological evidence, however, suggests that Sparta itself was a new settlement created from the 10th century BCE Common_Era.

In the late 8th century BCE Common_Era, Sparta subjugated most of neighbouring Messenia and its population was made to serve Spartan interests. The Spartan political system was unusual in that it had two hereditary kings from two separate families. These monarchs were particularly powerful when one of them led the army on campaign. The kings were also priests of Zeus and they sat on the council of elders known as the gerousia Gerousia. This body consisted of 28 over-60 years of age males who held the position for life. The gerousia Gerousia led the citizen assembly, probably proposing issues on which to vote and it was also the highest court in Sparta. The assembly (Ekklēsia) met once a month and was open to all citizens who voted by the simple method of shouting. There was also an executive committee of five ephors Ephor (ephoroi) chosen by lot from the citizen body, able only to serve for a maximum of one year and who were ineligible for future office. Two of the ephors Ephor also accompanied one of the kings when on campaign. Just how these different political elements interacted is not known for certain but clearly a degree of consensus was necessary for the state apparatus to function. It may also explain Sparta’s reputation as being a conservative state slow to make decisions in foreign policy.

Like all Greek Ancient_Greece societies Sparta was dominated by male citizens and the most powerful of those came from a select group of families. These were the landed aristocracy Landed_nobility, and following reforms credited to Lycurgus Lycurgus_of_Sparta in the 6th century BCE Common_Era (or even earlier), citizens could not indulge in agricultural activities - this was the lot of the helots Helots - but they had to devote themselves to athletic and military training and politics. Helots could not own property and so could not rise to become full-citizens, and this lack of social mobility Social_mobility would come back to haunt Sparta in later centuries. Reduced by constant wars in the 5th and 4th centuries BCE Common_Era, the Spartan hoplites Hoplite (homoioi) became dangerously small in number (8,000 in 490 BCE Common_Era to 700 in 371 BCE Common_Era), so much so, that non-Spartiate soldiers had to be enlisted and their loyalty and interest in Sparta’s ambitions was questionable.

Women in Sparta had a better lot than in other Greek Ancient_Greece city-states. In Sparta they could own property which they often gained through dowries Dowry and inheritances.
