The Achaemenid Empire (550–330 BC), also called the Achaemenid_EmpireEmpirean empire based in Western_Asia, founded by Cyrus_the_Great. Ranging at its greatest extent from the Balkans and Eastern_Europe proper in the west to the Indus_River in the east, it was larger than any previous empire in history, spanning 5.5 million square kilometers. Incorporating various peoples of different origins and faiths, it is notable for its successful model of a centralised, bureaucratic administration (through satraps under the King of Kings), for building infrastructure such as road systems and a Chapar_Khaneh, the use of an Official_language across its territories, and the development of civil services and a large professional army. The empire's successes inspired similar systems in later empires.

By the 7th century BC, the Achaemenid_Empire had settled in the southwestern portion of the Iranian_Plateau in the region of Persis, which came to be their heartland.From this region, Cyrus_the_Great advanced to defeat the Medes, Lydia, and the Neo-Babylonian Empire, establishing the Achaemenid Empire. Alexander_the_Great III the Great, an Media_Composer admirer of Cyrus_the_Great,conquered most of the empire by 330 BC.Upon Alexander_the_Great III's death, most of the empire's former territory came under the rule of the Ptolemaic_Kingdom and Seleucid Empire, in addition to other minor territories which gained independence at that time. The Iranian elites of the central Deccan_Plateau reclaimed power by the second century BC under the Parthian Empire.

The Achaemenid Empire is noted in Western history as the Antagonist of the Ancient_Greece city-states during the Greco-Persian_Wars and for the Jewish_emancipation of the History_of_the_Jews_in_China exiles in Babylon. The historical mark of the empire went far beyond its territorial and military influences and included cultural, social, technological and religious influences as well. Despite the lasting conflict between the two states, many Athenians adopted Achaemenid_Empire customs in their daily lives in a reciprocal cultural exchange, some being employed by or allied to the Achaemenid_Empire kings. The impact of Cyrus_the_Great's edict is mentioned in Judeo-Christian texts, and the empire was instrumental in the spread of Zoroastrianism as far east as History_of_China. The empire also set the tone for the politics, History and history of modern History_of_Iran.

The Achaemenid Empire was created by nomadic Achaemenid_Empire. The name "Achaemenid_Empire" is a Greek_language and Latin pronunciation of the native word referring to the country of the people originating from Persis (Old_Persian), their home territory located north of the Persian_Gulf in southwestern History_of_Iran.

The Achaemenid Empire was not the first Iranian empire, as by 6th century BC another group of ancient Iranian_peoples had already established the short-lived Median Empire. The Medes had originally been the dominant Iranian group in the region, freeing themselves of Assyria domination and rising to power at the end of the seventh century BC, incorporating the Achaemenid_Empire into their empire.

The Iranian_peoples had arrived in the region of what is today History_of_Iran c. 1000 BC and had for a number of centuries fallen under the domination of the Neo-Assyrian Empire (911–609 BC), based in northern Mesopotamia. However, the Medes (together with the Scythians, Babylonia), Cimmerians, Achaemenid_Empire and Chaldeans played a major role in the overthrow of the Assyria empire and establishment of the first Achaemenid_Empire empire.

The term Achaemenid_Empire means "of the family of the Achaemenis/Achaemenes" (Old_Persian a Bahuvrihi compound translating to "having a friend's mind"). Despite the derivation of the name, Achaemenes was himself a minor seventh-century ruler of the Anshan in southwestern History_of_Iran, and a Vassal_state of Assyria. It was not until the time of the emperor Cyrus_the_Great (Cyrus_the_Great of Achaemenid_Empire), a descendant of Achaemenes, that the Achaemenid Empire developed the prestige of an empire and set out to incorporate the existing empires of the ancient east, becoming the vast Persian Empire of ancient legend.

At some point in 550 BC, Cyrus_the_Great rose in rebellion against the Medes (most likely due to their mismanagement of Persis), eventually conquering the Medes and creating the first Achaemenid_Empire empire. Cyrus_the_Great the Great utilized his tactical genius,as well as his understanding of the socio-political conditions governing his territories, to eventually incorporate into the Empire neighbouring Lydia and the Neo-Babylonian Empire, also leading the way for his successor, Cambyses_II, to venture into Ptolemaic_Kingdom and defeat the Twenty-sixth_Dynasty_of_Egypt Dynasty of Ptolemaic_Kingdom.

Cyrus_the_Great the Great's political Palgrave_Macmillan was reflected in his management of his newly formed empire, as the Persian Empire became the first to attempt to govern many different ethnic groups on the principle of equal responsibilities and rights for all people, so long as subjects paid their taxes and kept the peace.Additionally, the king agreed not to interfere with the local customs, religions, and trades of its subject states,a unique quality that eventually won Cyrus_the_Great the support of the Neo-Babylonian_Empire. This system of management ultimately became an issue for the Achaemenid_Empire, as with a larger empire came the need for order and control, leading to expenditure of resources and mobilization of troops to quell local rebellions, and weakening the central power of the king. By the time of Darius_III, this disorganization had almost led to a disunited realm.

The_Persians from whom Cyrus_the_Great hailed were originally nomadic pastoralists in the western Iranian_Plateau and by 850 BC were calling themselves the Persepolis and their constantly shifting territory Parsua, for the most part localized around Persis. As Achaemenid_Empire gained power, they developed the infrastructure to support their growing influence, including creation of a capital named Pasargadae and an opulent City named Persepolis.

Begun during the rule of Darius_I "the Great" and completed some 100 years later,Persepolis was a symbol of the empire serving both as a ceremonial centre and a center of government.It had a special set of gradually progressive stairways named "All Countries"around which carved relief decoration depicted scenes of heroism, Hunter-gatherer, natural themes, and presentation of the gifts to the Achaemenid_Empire kings by their various subjects, possibly during the spring festival, Nowruz. The core structure was composed of a multitude of square rooms or halls, the biggest of which was called Apadana.Tall, decorated columns welcomed visitors and emphasized the height of the structure. Later on, Darius_I also utilized Susa and Ecbatana as his governmental centres, developing them to a similar metropolitan status.

Accounts of the Achaemenid_family_tree can be derived from either documented Ancient_Greece or Roman accounts, or from existing documented Achaemenid_Empire accounts such as those found in the Behistun_Inscription. However, since most existing accounts of this vast empire are in works of Ancient_Greek_philosophy and historians, and since many of the original Achaemenid_Empire documents are lost, not to mention being subject to varying scholarly views on their origin and possible motivations behind them, it is difficult to create a definitive and completely objective list. Nonetheless, it is clear that Cyrus_the_Great and Darius_I were critical in the expansion of the empire. Cyrus_the_Great is often believed to be the son of Cambyses_I, grandson of Cyrus_I, the father of Cambyses_II, and a relative of Darius_I through a shared ancestor, Teispes. Cyrus_I the Great is also believed to have been a family member (possibly grandson) of the Medes king Astyages through his mother, Mandane of Media. A minority of scholars argue that perhaps Achaemenes was a Retrograde_and_prograde_motion creation of Darius_I in order to reconcile his connection with Cyrus_I after gaining power.

Ancient_Greece writers provide some legendary information about Achaemenes by calling his tribe the Pasargadae and stating that he was "raised by an eagle". Plato, when writing about the Achaemenid_Empire, identified Achaemenes with Perses, ancestor of the Achaemenid_Empire in Greek_mythology. According to Plato, Achaemenes was the same person as Perses, a son of the Ethiopian queen Andromeda_Galaxy and the Ancient_Greece hero Perseus, and a grandson of Zeus. Later writers believed that Achaemenes and Perseus were different people, and that Perses was an ancestor of the king.This account further confirms that Achaemenes could well have been a significant Anshan leader and an ancestor of Cyrus_I the Great. Regardless, both Cyrus_I the Great and Darius_I the Great were related, prominent kings of Achaemenid_Empire, under whose rule the empire expanded to include much of the ancient world.

In 559 BC, Cambyses_I the Elder was succeeded as the king of Anšān by his son Cyrus_I the Great, who also succeeded the still-living Arsames as the King of Achaemenid_Empire, thus reuniting the two realms. Cyrus_I is considered to be the first true king of the Persian Empire, as his predecessors were subservient to the Medes. Cyrus_I the Great conquered Media, Lydia, and Babylon. Cyrus_I was politically shrewd, modeling himself as the "savior" of conquered nations, often allowing displaced people to return, and giving his subjects freedom to practice local customs. To reinforce this image, he instituted policies of Freedom_of_religion, and restored temples and other infrastructure in the newly acquired cities (Most notably the Jewish inhabitants of Babylon, as recorded in the Cyrus_Cylinder and the Tanakh). As a result of his tolerant policies he came to be known by those of the Jewish faith as "the anointed of the Lord."

His immediate successors were less successful. Cyrus_I' son Cambyses_II conquered Ancient_Egypt in 525 BC, but died in July 522 BC during a revolt led by a Sacerdotalism Clan that had lost its power following Cyrus_I' conquest of Media. The cause of his death remains uncertain, although it may have been the result of an accident.

According to Herodotus, Cambyses_II had originally ventured into Ancient_Egypt to take revenge for the Pharaoh Amasis_II's trickery when he sent a fake Ancient_Egypt Bride whose family Amasis_II had murdered, instead of his own daughter, to wed Cambyses_II. Additionally negative reports of mistreatment caused by Amasis_II, given by Phanes_(mythology) of Halicarnassus, a wise counsellor serving Amasis_II, further bolstered Cambyses_II's resolve to venture into Ancient_Egypt. Amasis_II died before Cambyses_II could face him, but his successor Psamtik_III was defeated by Cambyses_II in the Battle of Pelusium.

While Cambyses_II was in Ancient_Egypt, the Zoroastrianism priests, whom Herodotus called Magi, usurped the throne for one of their own, Bardiya, who then pretended to be Cambyses_II's younger brother Bardiya (Ancient_Greece: Bardiya or Tanaoxares/Tanyoxarkes), who had been assassinated some three years earlier. Owing to the strict rule of Cambyses_II, especially his stance on taxation,and his long absence in Ancient_Egypt, "the whole people, Perses, Medes and all the other nations," acknowledged the usurper, especially as he granted a remission of taxes for three years.Cambyses II himself would not be able to quell the imposters, as he died on the way back from Ancient_Egypt.

The claim that Bardiya had impersonated Bardiya (Bardiya), is derived from Darius_I the Great and the records at the Behistun_Inscription. Historians are divided over the possibility that the story of the impostor was invented by Darius_I as justification for his coup.Darius I made a similar claim when he later captured Babylon, announcing that the Babylon king was not, in fact, Nebuchadnezzar_III, but an impostor named Nidintu-bel.

According to the Behistun_Inscription, Bardiya ruled for seven months before being overthrown in 522 BC by Darius_I the Great (Darius_I) (Old_Persian Dāryavuš, "who holds firm the good", also known as Darayarahush or Darius_I the Great). The Magi, though persecuted, continued to exist, and a year following the death of the first pseudo-Smerdis (Bardiya), saw a second pseudo-Smerdis (named Vahyazdāta) attempt a coup. The coup, though initially successful, failed.

Herodotus writes that the native leadership debated the best form of government for the empire. It was agreed that an Oligarchy would divide them against one another, and Democracy would bring about Ochlocracy resulting in a charismatic leader resuming the Monarchy. Therefore, they decided a new monarch was in order, particularly since they were in a position to choose him. Darius_I was chosen monarch from among the leaders. He was cousin to Cambyses_II and Bardiya (Bardiya), claiming Ariaramnes as his ancestor.

The Achaemenid_Empire thereafter consolidated areas firmly under their control. It was Cyrus_I the Great and Darius_I the Great who, by sound and farsighted administrative planning, brilliant military maneuvering, and a Humanism world view, established the greatness of the Achaemenid_Empire and, in less than thirty years, raised them from an obscure tribe to a world power. It was during the reign of Darius_I the Great (Darius_I) that Persepolis was built (518–516_BC) and which would serve as capital for several generations of Achaemenid_Empire kings. Ecbatana (Hagmatāna "City of Gatherings", modern: Hamadan) in Media was greatly expanded during this period and served as the summer capital.

By the 5th century BC the Kings of Achaemenid_Empire were either ruling over or had subordinated territories encompassing not just all of the Achaemenid_Empire Plateau and all of the territories formerly held by the Assyrian Empire (Mesopotamia, the Levant, Cyprus and Egypt), but beyond this all of Anatolia and Armenia, as well as the Transcaucasia and parts of the North_Caucasus, Azerbaijan, Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, all of Bulgaria, Peony, Thrace and Republic_of_Macedonia to the north and west, most of the Black_Sea coastal regions, parts of Central_Asia as far as the Aral_Sea, the Amu_Darya and Syr_Darya to the north and north-east, the Hindu_Kush and the western Indus_River Drainage_basin (corresponding to modern Afghanistan and Pakistan) to the far east, parts of northern Arabia to the south, and parts of northern Ancient_Libya to the south-west, and parts of Oman, History_of_China, and the United_Arab_Emirates.

The Ionian_Revolt in 499 BC, and associated revolts in Aeolis, Doris, Cyprus and Caria, were military rebellions by several regions of Anatolia against Achaemenid_Empire rule, lasting from 499 to 493 BC. At the heart of the rebellion was the dissatisfaction of the Ancient_Greece cities of Anatolia with the tyrants appointed by Achaemenid_Empire to rule them, along with the individual actions of two Miletus tyrants, Histiaeus and Aristagoras. In 499 BC, the then Tyrant of Miletus, Aristagoras, launched a joint expedition with the Achaemenid_Empire Satrap Artaphernes to conquer Naxos, in an attempt to bolster his position in Miletus (both financially and in terms of prestige). The mission was a debacle, and sensing his imminent removal as Tyrant, Aristagoras chose to incite the whole of Ionia into rebellion against the Achaemenid_Empire king Darius_I the Great.

The_Persians continued to reduce the cities along the west coast that still held out against them, before finally imposing a peace settlement in 493 BC on Ionia that was generally considered to be both just and fair. The Ionian_Revolt constituted the first major conflict between Ancient_Greece and the Achaemenid Empire, and as such represents the first phase of the Greco-Persian_Wars. Anatolia had been brought back into the Achaemenid_Empire fold, but Darius_I had vowed to punish History_of_Athens and Eretria for their support of the revolt. Moreover, seeing that the political situation in Ancient_Greece posed a continued threat to the stability of his Empire, he decided to embark on the conquest of all of Ancient_Greece. The first campaign of the invasion was to bring the territories in the Balkans back within the empire.The Achaemenid_Empire grip over these territories had loosened following the Ionian_Revolt. In 492 BC, the Achaemenid_Empire general Mardonius re-subjugated Thrace and made Achaemenid_Macedonia a fully subordinate part of the empire; it had been a Vassal_state as early as the late 6th century BC, but retained a great deal of autonomy. However, in 490 BC the Achaemenid_Empire forces were defeated by the Athenians at the Battle of Battle_of_Marathon and Darius_I would die before having the chance to launch an invasion of Ancient_Greece.

Xerxes_I (485–465 BC, Old_Persian Xerxes_I "Hero Among Kings"), son of Darius_I, vowed to complete the job. He organized a massive invasion aiming to conquer Ancient_Greece. His army entered Ancient_Greece from the north, meeting little or no resistance through Republic_of_Macedonia and Thessaly, but was delayed by a small Ancient_Greece force for three days at Battle_of_Thermopylae. A simultaneous Naval_warfare at Battle_of_Artemisium was tactically indecisive as large storms destroyed ships from both sides. The Battle was stopped prematurely when the Ancient_Greece received news of the defeat at Battle_of_Thermopylae and retreated. The Battle was a strategic victory for the Achaemenid_Empire, giving them uncontested control of Battle_of_Artemisium and the Aegean_Sea.

Following his victory at the Battle of Battle_of_Thermopylae, Xerxes_I sacked the evacuated City of Classical_Athens and prepared to meet the Ancient_Greece at the strategic Isthmus of Corinth and the Saronic_Gulf. In 480 BC the Ancient_Greece won a decisive victory over the Achaemenid_Empire fleet at the Battle of Battle_of_Salamis and forced Xerxes_I to retire to Sardis. The land army which he left in Ancient_Greece under Mardonius retook Classical_Athens but was eventually destroyed in 479 BC at the Battle of Battle_of_Plataea. The final defeat of the Achaemenid_Empire at Battle_of_Mycale encouraged the Ancient_Greece cities of Anatolia to revolt, and the Achaemenid_Empire lost all of their territories in Europe; Republic_of_Macedonia once again became independent.

After Xerxes_I was assassinated, he was succeeded by his eldest son Artaxerxes_I_of_Persia. It was during his reign that Elamite_language ceased to be the language of government, and Aramaic_language gained in importance. It was probably during this reign that the Solar_calendar was introduced as the national calendar. Under Artaxerxes_I_of_Persia, Zoroastrianism became the De_facto religion of state.

After Achaemenid_Empire had been defeated at Battle of Giants_(Greek_mythology) (469 BC), military action between Ancient_Greece and Achaemenid_Empire was halted. When Artaxerxes_I_of_Persia took power, he introduced a new Achaemenid_Empire strategy of weakening the Athenians by funding their enemies in Ancient_Greece. This indirectly caused the Athenians to move the treasury of the Delian_League from the island of Delos to the Acropolis_of_Athens. This funding practice inevitably prompted renewed fighting in 450 BC, where the Ancient_Greece attacked at the Battle of Cyprus. After Cimon's failure to attain much in this expedition, the Peace of Callias_II was agreed between Classical_Athens, Argos and Achaemenid_Empire in 449 BC.

In addition, Artaxerxes_I_of_Persia gave him Skepsis to provide him with clothes, and he also gave him Percote with bedding for his house.

Artaxerxes_II_of_Persia was the longest reigning of the Achaemenid_Empire kings and it was during this 45-year period of relative peace and stability that many of the monuments of the era were constructed. Artaxerxes_II_of_Persia moved the capital back to Persepolis, which he greatly extended. The extraordinary innovation of the Zoroastrianism shrines can also be dated to his reign, and it was probably during this period that Zoroastrianism spread from Armenia throughout Anatolia and the Levant. The construction of temples, though serving a religious purpose, was not a purely selfless act, as they also served as an important source of income. From the List_of_kings_of_Babylon, the Achaemenid_Empire had taken over the concept of a mandatory Temple_tax, a one-tenth Tithe which all inhabitants paid to the Temple nearest to their land or other source of income. In retrospect, Artaxerxes_II_of_Persia is generally regarded as an amiable man who lacked the moral fibre to be a really successful ruler.
