Hoplites Hoplite (Greek Ancient_Greece: Ὁπλιτης) were citizen-soldiers of Ancient Greek Ancient_Greece city-states who were primarily armed with spears and shields. Hoplite soldiers utilized the phalanx formation Phalanx in order to be effective in war with fewer soldiers. The formation discouraged the soldiers from acting alone, for this would compromise the formation and minimize its strengths.[1] The hoplites Hoplite were primarily represented by free citizens—propertied farmers and artisans—who were able to afford the bronze armour suit and weapons (estimated at a third to a half of its able-bodied adult male population).[2] Hoplites Hoplite were not professional soldiers and often lacked sufficient military training. Although some states did maintain a small elite professional unit, hoplite Hoplite soldiers were relied on heavily and made up the bulk of ancient Greek Ancient_Greece armies of the time.[3]

In the 8th or 7th century BC, Greek Ancient_Greece armies adopted a military innovation known as the phalanx formation Phalanx. The formation proved successful in defeating the Persians Achaemenid_Empire when employed by the Athenians at the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon in 490 BC during the First Greco-Persian War Greco-Persian_Wars. The Persian Achaemenid_Empire archers and light troops who fought in the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon failed, because their bows were too weak for their arrows to penetrate the wall of Greek Ancient_Greece shields that made up the phalanx formation Phalanx. The phalanx Phalanx was also employed by the Greeks Ancient_Greece at the Battle of Thermopylae in 480 BC and at the Battle of Plataea Battle_of_Plataea in 479 BC during the Second Greco-Persian War Greco-Persian_Wars.

The word hoplite Hoplite (Greek Ancient_Greece: ὁπλίτης hoplitēs; pl. ὁπλῖται hoplitai) derives from hoplon Aspis (ὅπλον, plural hopla Hopla ὅπλα), the name for the type of shield used by the soldiers. However, the shield was more commonly known as an aspis Aspis, so the word hopla Hopla may refer to the soldiers' weapons or even their full armament.[4] In the modern Hellenic Army Hellenic_Army, the word hoplite Hoplite (Greek Ancient_Greece: oπλίτης) is used to refer to an infantryman Infantry.

The fragmented political structure of Ancient Greece Ancient_Greece, with many competing city-states, increased the frequency of conflict, but at the same time limited the scale of warfare. Limited manpower did not allow most Greek Ancient_Greece city-states to form large armies which could operate for long periods because they were generally not formed from professional soldiers. Most soldiers had careers as farmers or workers and returned to these professions after the campaign. All hoplites Hoplite were expected to take part in any military campaign when called for duty by leaders of the state. The Lacedaemonian Sparta citizens of Sparta were renowned for their lifelong combat training and almost mythical military prowess, while their greatest adversaries, the Athenians, were exempted from service only after the 60th year of their lives. This inevitably reduced the potential duration of campaigns and often resulted in the campaign season being restricted to one summer.

Armies generally marched directly to their destination, and in some cases the battlefield was agreed to by the contestants in advance. Battles were fought on level ground, and hoplites Hoplite preferred to fight with high terrain on both sides of the phalanx Phalanx so the formation could not be flanked. An example of this was the Battle of Thermopylae, where the Spartans Spartan_army specifically chose a narrow coastal pass to make their stand against the massive Persian Achaemenid_Empire army. The vastly outnumbered Greeks Ancient_Greece held off the Persians Achaemenid_Empire for seven days.

When battles occurred, they were usually set piece and intended to be decisive. The battlefield would be flat and open to facilitate phalanx Phalanx warfare. These battles were usually short and required a high degree of discipline. At least in the early classical Classical_antiquity period, when cavalry Cavalry was present, its role was restricted to protection of the flanks of the phalanx Phalanx, pursuit of a defeated enemy, and covering a retreat if required. Light infantry Light_infantry and missile Projectile troops took part in the battles but their role was less important. Before the opposing phalanxes engaged, the light troops would skirmish with the enemy's light forces, and then protect the flanks and rear of the phalanx Phalanx.

The military structure created by the Spartans Spartan_army was a rectangular phalanx formation Phalanx. The formation was organized from eight to ten rows deep and could cover a front of a quarter of a mile or more if sufficient hoplites Hoplite were available. The two lines would close to a short distance to allow effective use of their spears, while the psiloi Psiloi threw stones and javelins from behind their lines. The shields would clash and the first lines (protostates Protostates) would stab at their opponents, at the same time trying to keep in position. The ranks behind them would support them with their own spears and the mass of their shields gently pushing them, not to force them into the enemy formation but to keep them steady and in place. The soldiers The_Soldiers in the back provided motivation to the ranks in the front being that most hoplites Hoplite were close community members. At certain points, a command would be given to the phalanx Phalanx or a part thereof to collectively take a certain number of steps forward (ranging from half to multiple steps). This was the famed othismos.[5]

At this point, the phalanx Phalanx would put its collective weight to push back the enemy line and thus create fear and panic among its ranks. There could be multiple such instances of attempts to push, but it seems from the accounts of the ancients that these were perfectly orchestrated and attempted organized en masse. Once one of the lines broke, the troops would generally flee from the field, sometimes chased by psiloi Psiloi, peltasts Peltast, or light cavalry Light_cavalry.

If a hoplite Hoplite escaped, he would sometimes be forced to drop his cumbersome aspis Aspis, thereby disgracing himself to his friends and family (becoming a ripsaspis, one who threw his shield).[6] To lessen the amount of casualties inflicted by the enemy during battles, soldiers were positioned to stand shoulder to shoulder with their hoplon Aspis. The hoplites Hoplite' most prominent citizens and generals led from the front. Thus, the whole war could be decided by a single field battle Battle; victory was enforced by ransoming the fallen back to the defeated, called the "Custom of the Greeks Ancient_Greece".

Individual hoplites Hoplite carried their shields on their left arm, protecting not only themselves but also the soldier to the left. This meant that the men at the extreme right of the phalanx Phalanx were only half-protected. In battle Battle, opposing phalanxes would exploit this weakness by attempting to overlap the enemy's right flank.[7] It also meant that, in battle Battle, a phalanx Phalanx would tend to drift to the right (as hoplites Hoplite sought to remain behind the shield of their neighbour). The most experienced hoplites Hoplite were often placed on the right side of the phalanx Phalanx, to counteract these problems. According to Plutarch's Sayings of Spartans Spartan_army, "a man carried a shield for the sake of the whole line".[8]

The phalanx Phalanx is an example of a military formation in which single combat and other individualistic forms of battle Battle were suppressed for the good of the whole. In earlier Homeric, dark age combat, the words and deeds of supremely powerful heroes turned the tide of battle Battle. Instead of having individual heroes, hoplite Hoplite warfare relied heavily on the community and unity of soldiers. With friends and family pushing on either side and enemies forming a solid wall of shields in front, the hoplite Hoplite had little opportunity for feats of technique and weapon skill, but great need for commitment and mental toughness Mental_toughness. By forming a human wall to provide a powerful defensive armour, the hoplites Hoplite became much more effective while taking fewer casualties. The hoplites Hoplite had much discipline and were taught to be loyal and trustworthy. They had to trust Trust_law their neighbours for mutual protection, so a phalanx Phalanx was only as strong as its weakest elements. Its effectiveness depended on how well the hoplites Hoplite could maintain this formation while in combat, and how well they could stand their ground, especially when engaged against another phalanx Phalanx. The more disciplined and courageous the army, the more likely it was to win—often engagements between the various city-states of Greece Ancient_Greece would be resolved by one side fleeing after their phalanx Phalanx had broken formation.

Body armour[edit]
Each hoplite Hoplite provided his own equipment. Thus, only those who could afford such weaponry fought as hoplites Hoplite; as with the Roman Republican Second_Spanish_Republic army it was the middle classes who formed the bulk of the infantry. Equipment was not standardized, although there were doubtless trends in general designs over time, and between city-states. Hoplites Hoplite had customized armour, the shield was decorated with family or clan Clan emblems, although in later years these were replaced by symbols or monograms of the city City states. The equipment might well be passed down in families, since it would have been expensive to manufacture.

The hoplite Hoplite army consisted of heavily armoured infantrymen. Their armour, also called panoply, was made of full bronze, weighing nearly 32 kilograms (70 lb). The average farmer-peasant hoplite Hoplite typically wore no armour, carrying only a shield, a spear, and perhaps a helmet plus a secondary weapon.The linothorax Linothorax was the most popular type armour worn by the hoplites Hoplite, since it was cost-effective and provided decent protection. The richer upper-class hoplites Hoplite typically had a bronze cuirass Cuirass of either the bell or muscled variety, a bronze helmet Bronze_Helmet_(Poland) with cheekplates, as well as greaves and other armour. The design of the helmets used varied through time. The Corinthian helmet Corinthian_helmet was at first standardized and was a very successful design. Later variants included the Chalcidian helmet Chalcidian_helmet, a lightened version of the Corinthian helmet Corinthian_helmet, and the very simple Pilos Pileus_(hat) helmet worn by the later hoplites Hoplite. Often the helmet was decorated with one, sometimes more horsehair Horsehair crests, and/or bronze animal horns and ears. Helmets were often painted as well. The Thracian helmet Phrygian_helmet had a large visor to further increase protection. In later periods, linen Linen breastplates called linothorax Linothorax were used, as they were tougher and cheaper to make. The linen Linen was 0.5-centimetre Centimetre (0.20 in) thick.

By contrast with hoplites Hoplite, other contemporary infantry (e.g., Persian Achaemenid_Empire) tended to wear relatively light armour, use wicker shields, and were armed with shorter spears, javelins, and bows. The most famous are the peltasts Peltast, light-armed troops who wore no armour and were armed with a light shield, javelins and a short sword Gladius. The Athenian Classical_Athens general Iphicrates developed a new type of armour and arms for his mercenary Mercenary army, which included light linen Linen armour, smaller shields and longer spears, whilst arming his peltasts Peltast with larger shields, helmets and a longer spear, thus enabling them to defend themselves more easily against enemy hoplites Hoplite. With this new type of army he defeated a Spartan army Spartan_army in 392 BC. Nevertheless, most hoplites Hoplite stuck to the traditional arms and armour.

Hoplites Hoplite carried a large concave shield called an aspis Aspis (often referred to as a hoplon Aspis), measuring between 80–100 centimetres (31–39 in) in diameter and weighing between 6.5–8 kilograms (14–18 lbs).[9][10] This large shield was made possible partly by its shape, which allowed it to be supported on the shoulder. The hoplon Aspis shield was put together in three layers: the center layer was made of thick wood Wood, the outside layer facing the enemy was made of bronze, and leather made up the inside of the shield. The revolutionary Revolution part of the shield was, in fact, the grip. Known as an Argive Argos grip, it placed the handle at the edge of the shield, and was supported by a leather fastening (for the forearm) at the centre. These two points of contact eliminated the possibility of the shield swaying to the side after being struck, and as a result soldiers rarely lost their shields. This allowed the hoplite Hoplite soldier more mobility with the shield, as well as the ability to capitalize on its offensive capabilities and better support the phalanx Phalanx. The large hoplon Aspis shields, designed for pushing ahead, were the most essential equipment for the hoplites Hoplite.[11]

The main offensive weapon used was a 2.4–4.5-metre (7.9–14.8 ft) long and 2.5-centimetre Centimetre (1 in) in diameter spear called a doru, or dory Dory. It was held with the right hand, with the left hand holding the hoplite Hoplite's shield. Soldiers usually held their spears in an underhand position when approaching but once they came into close contact with their opponents, they were held in an overhand position ready to strike. The spearhead The_Spearhead was usually a curved leaf shape, while the rear of the spear had a spike called a sauroter ("lizard-killer") which was used to stand the spear in the ground (hence the name). It was also used as a secondary weapon if the main shaft snapped, or for the rear ranks to finish off fallen opponents as the phalanx Phalanx advanced over them. In addition to being used as a secondary weapon, the sauroter also doubled to balance the spear, but not for throwing purposes. It is a matter of contention, among historians, whether the hoplite Hoplite used the spear overarm or underarm Axilla. Held underarm Axilla, the thrusts would have been less powerful but under more control, and vice versa. It seems likely that both motions were used, depending on the situation. If attack was called for, an overarm motion was more likely to break through an opponent's defence. The upward thrust is more easily deflected by armour due to its lesser leverage. However, when defending, an underarm Axilla carry absorbed more shock and could be 'couched' under the shoulder for maximum stability. It should also be said that an overarm motion would allow more effective combination of the aspis Aspis and doru if the shield wall Shield_wall had broken down, while the underarm Axilla motion would be more effective when the shield had to be interlocked with those of one's neighbours in the battle-line. Hoplites Hoplite in the rows behind the lead would almost certainly have made overarm thrusts. The rear ranks held their spears underarm Axilla, and raised their shields upwards at increasing angles. This was an effective defence against missiles, deflecting their force.

Hoplites Hoplite also carried a sword Gladius, mostly a short sword Gladius called a xiphos Xiphos, but later also longer and heavier types. The short sword Gladius was a secondary weapon, used if or when their spears were broken or lost, or if the phalanx Phalanx broke rank. The xiphos Xiphos usually had a blade Sword around 60 centimetres (24 in) long; however, those used by the Spartans Spartan_army were often only 30–45 centimetres long. This very short xiphos Xiphos would be very advantageous Advantageous in the press that occurred when two lines of hoplites Hoplite met, capable of being thrust through gaps in the shieldwall Shield_wall into an enemy's unprotected groin or throat, while there was no room to swing Swing_bowling a longer sword Gladius. Such a small weapon would be particularly useful after many hoplites Hoplite had started to abandon body armour Body_armor during the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War. Hoplites Hoplite could also alternatively carry the kopis, a heavy knife with a forward-curving blade Sword.

Dark age warfare transitioned into hoplite Hoplite warfare in the 8th century BC. Historians and researchers have debated the reason and speed of the transition for centuries. So far 3 popular theories exist:

Developed by Anthony Snodgrass Anthony_Snodgrass, the Gradualist Theory states that the hoplite Hoplite style of battle Battle developed in a series of steps as a result of innovations in armour and weaponry.[12] Chronologically dating the archeological findings of hoplite Hoplite armour and using the findings to approximate the development of the phalanx formation Phalanx, Snodgrass claims that the transition took approximately 100 years to complete from 750–650 BC.[13] The progression of the phalanx Phalanx took time because as the phalanx Phalanx matured it required denser formations that made the elite warriors recruit Greek Ancient_Greece citizens.[14] The large amounts of hoplite Hoplite armour needed to then be distributed to the populations of Greek Ancient_Greece citizens only increased the time for the phalanx Phalanx to be implemented. Snodgrass believes, only once the armour was in place that the phalanx formation Phalanx became popular.[13]

The Rapid Adaptation model was developed by historians Paul Cartledge Paul_Cartledge and Victor Handley_Page_Victor Hanson Tommy_Hanson.[15] The historians believe that the phalanx Phalanx was created individually by military forces, but was so effective that others had to immediately adapt their way of war to combat the formation.[15] Rapid Adoptionists Adoptionism propose that the double grip, hoplon Aspis shield that was required for the phalanx formation Phalanx was so constricting in mobility that once it was introduced, dark age, free flowing warfare was inadequate to fight against the hoplites Hoplite only escalating the speed of the transition.[12] Quickly, the phalanx formation Phalanx and hoplite Hoplite armour became widely used throughout Ancient Greece Ancient_Greece. Cartledge Frank_Cartledge and Hanson Tommy_Hanson estimate the transition took place from 725–675 BC.[15]

Developed by Hans Van Wees, the Extended Gradualist theory is the most lengthy of the three popular transition theories. Van Wees depicts iconography Iconography found on pots of the Dark Ages Greek_Dark_Ages believing that the foundation of the phalanx formation Phalanx was birthed during this time.[12] Specifically, he uses an example of the Chigi Vase Chigi_vase to point out that hoplite Hoplite soldiers were carrying normal spears as well as javelins on their backs. Matured hoplites Hoplite did not carry long range weapons including javelins.[13] The Chigi vase Chigi_vase is important for our knowledge of the hoplite Hoplite soldier because it is one, if not the only one representation of the hoplite Hoplite formation, known as the phalanx Phalanx, in Greek art Ancient_Greek_art.[16] This led Van Wees to believe that there was a transitional period from long range warfare Range_Warfare of the Dark Ages Greek_Dark_Ages to the close combat of hoplite Hoplite warfare. Some other evidence of a transitional period lies within the text of Spartan Poet Tyrtaios Tyrtaeus, when he wrote, "…will they draw back for the pounding [of the missiles, no,] despite the battery Artillery_battery of great hurl-stones, the helmets shall abide the rattle [of war unbowed]".[17] At no point in future texts does Tyrtaios Tyrtaeus involve any talk of missiles or rocks making another case for a transitional period. Extended Gradualists argue that hoplite Hoplite warriors did not fight in a true phalanx Phalanx until the 5th century BC.[12] Making estimations of the speed of the transition reached as long as 300 years, from 750–450 BC.

The exact time when hoplite Hoplite warfare was developed is uncertain, the prevalent theory being that it was established sometime during the 8th or 7th century BC, when the "heroic age was abandoned and a far more disciplined system introduced" and the Argive Argos shield became popular.[18] Peter Krentz argues that "the ideology of hoplitic warfare as a ritualized contest developed not in the 7th century [BC], but only after 480, when non-hoplite arms began to be excluded from the phalanx Phalanx".[19] Anagnostis Agelarakis Anagnostis_Agelarakis, based on recent archaeo-anthropological discoveries of the earliest monumental polyandrion (communal burial of male warriors) at Paros Island in Greece Ancient_Greece, unveils a last quarter of the 8th century BC date for a hoplitic phalangeal military organization.[20]

The rise and fall of hoplite Hoplite warfare was tied to the rise and fall of the city-state Polis. As discussed above, hoplites Hoplite were a solution to the armed clashes between independent city-states. As Greek Ancient_Greece civilization found itself confronted by the world at large, particularly the Persians Achaemenid_Empire, the emphasis in warfare shifted. Confronted by huge numbers of enemy troops, individual city-states could not realistically fight alone. During the Greco-Persian Wars Greco-Persian_Wars (499–448 BC), alliances between groups of cities (whose composition varied over time) fought against the Persians Achaemenid_Empire. This drastically altered the scale of warfare and the numbers of troops involved. The hoplite Hoplite phalanx Phalanx proved itself far superior to the Persian Achaemenid_Empire infantry at such conflicts as the Battle of Marathon Battle_of_Marathon, Thermopylae, and the Battle of Plataea Battle_of_Plataea.

During this period, Athens Classical_Athens and Sparta rose to a position of political eminence in Greece Ancient_Greece, and their rivalry in the aftermath of the Persian Achaemenid_Empire wars brought Greece Ancient_Greece into renewed internal conflict. However, the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War was on a scale unlike conflicts before. Fought between leagues of cities, dominated by Athens Classical_Athens and Sparta respectively, the pooled manpower and financial resources allowed a diversification of warfare. Hoplite warfare was in decline; there were three major battles in the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War, and none proved decisive. Instead there was increased reliance on navies, skirmishers, mercenaries Mercenary, city City walls, siege Siege engines, and non-set piece tactics. These reforms made wars of attrition possible and greatly increased the number of casualties. In the Persian Achaemenid_Empire war, hoplites Hoplite faced large numbers of skirmishers and missile-armed troops, and such troops (e.g., peltasts Peltast) became much more commonly used by the Greeks Ancient_Greece during the Peloponnesian War Peloponnesian_War. As a result, hoplites Hoplite began wearing less armour, carrying shorter swords, and in general adapting for greater mobility; this led to the development of the ekdromos light hoplite Hoplite.
