Ionia (Ancient Greek Ancient_Greece: Ἰωνία, Ionía or Ἰωνίη, Ioníe) was an ancient region on the central part of the western coast of Anatolia in present-day Turkey, the region nearest İzmir Occupation_of_Smyrna, which was historically Smyrna. It consisted of the northernmost territories of the Ionian League Ionian_League of Greek Ancient_Greece settlements. Never a unified state, it was named after the Ionian tribe Ionians who, in the Archaic Period Archaic_Greece (600–480 BC), settled mainly the shores and islands of the Aegean Sea Aegean_Sea. Ionian Ionia states were identified by tradition and by their use of Eastern Greek Ancient_Greece.

Ionia proper comprised a narrow coastal strip from Phocaea in the north near the mouth of the river Hermus Gediz_River (now the Gediz Gediz_River), to Miletus in the south near the mouth of the river Maeander Büyük_Menderes_River, and included the islands of Chios and Samos. It was bounded by Aeolia Thessaly to the north, Lydia to the east and Caria to the south. The cities within the region figured large in the strife between the Persian Empire Roman_Empire and the Greeks Ancient_Greece.

According to Greek Ancient_Greece tradition, the cities of Ionia were founded by colonists from the other side of the Aegean Aegean_Sea. Their settlement was connected with the legendary history of the Ionic Ionia people in Attica, which asserts that the colonists were led by Neleus and Androclus Androcles, sons of Codrus, the last king of Athens History_of_Athens. In accordance with this view the "Ionic Ionia migration", as it was called by later chronologers, was dated by them one hundred and forty years after the Trojan War Trojan_War, or sixty years after the return of the Heracleidae into the Peloponnese.[1][2]

Ionia was of small extent, not exceeding 150 kilometres (90 mi) in length from north to south, with a breadth varying from 60 to 90 kilometres (40 to 60 mi), but to this must be added the peninsula of Mimas, together with the two islands. So intricate is the coastline that the voyage along its shores was estimated at nearly four times the direct distance. A great part of this area was, moreover, occupied by mountains. Of these the most lofty and striking were Mimas and Corycus, in the peninsula which stands out to the west, facing the island of Chios; Sipylus Mount_Sipylus, to the north of Smyrna, Corax, extending to the south-west from the Gulf of Smyrna, and descending to the sea between Lebedus and Teos; and the strongly marked range of Mycale, a continuation of Messogisin the interior, which forms the bold headland Headlands_and_bays of Trogilium or Mycale, opposite Samos. None of these mountains attains a height of more than 1,200 metres (3,940 ft). The district comprised three extremely fertile valleys formed by the outflow of three rivers, among the most considerable in Asia Minor Anatolia: the Hermus Gediz_River in the north, flowing into the Gulf of Smyrna, though at some distance from the city City of that name; the Caster, which flowed under the walls of Ephesus; and the Maeander Büyük_Menderes_River, which in ancient times discharged its waters into the deep gulf that once bathed the walls of Miletus, but which has been gradually filled up by this river's deposits. With the advantage of a peculiarly fine climate, for which this part of Asia Minor Anatolia has been famous in all ages, Ionia enjoyed the reputation in ancient times of being the most fertile of all the rich provinces of Asia Minor Anatolia; and even in modern times, though very imperfectly Imperfectly cultivated, it produces abundance of fruit of all kinds, and the raisins and figs of Smyrna supply almost all the markets of Europe.[3]
